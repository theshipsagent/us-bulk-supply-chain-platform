# NDRF Ghost Fleets and RRF Vessel Location Research Report

**Date:** February 5, 2026
**Prepared for:** US Flag Fleet Analysis Project
**Research Focus:** NDRF Reserve Fleet Locations and RRF Vessel Layberth Sites

---

## Executive Summary

This report documents comprehensive research on the National Defense Reserve Fleet (NDRF) "ghost fleets" at three primary locations and identifies current layberth locations for Ready Reserve Force (RRF) vessels in the project dataset. The NDRF currently maintains approximately 100 vessels across three major anchorage sites, with significant reductions over recent years due to disposal and scrapping programs.

---

## 1. JAMES RIVER RESERVE FLEET (JRRF)

### Location Details
- **Site:** Fort Eustis, Virginia
- **Coordinates:** 37°07′13″N 76°38′47″W (37.120393°N 76.646469°W)
- **Waterway:** James River
- **Status:** Active NDRF site (oldest of original eight NDRF sites)

### Current Fleet Status (2024-2025)
- **Vessel Count:** Approximately 4 vessels remaining (as of September 2024)
- **Historical Peak:** Over 800 vessels during peak Cold War period
- **Recent Reduction:** Dramatic decline from over 100 vessels in the 1990s

### Vessel Composition
- **Retention Vessels:** 2 ships designated for retention (maintained for potential future use)
- **Non-Retention Vessels:** 2 ships designated for disposal
- **Status:** 1 of the disposal vessels already under contract to be hauled away

### Vessel Types
- Dry cargo ships (break-bulk carriers)
- Limited number of tankers
- Military auxiliaries (historically)
- Submarine rescue vessels (historic - USS Kittiwake ASR-13, USS Ortolan ASR-22)

### Management
- **Oversight:** U.S. Department of Transportation, Maritime Administration (MARAD)
- **Purpose:** Strategic reserve for national defense and emergency purposes
- **Maintenance Status:** Vessels are "mothballed" - preserved in reduced operational status

### Recent Activity
- **Scrapping Program:** Ongoing disposal of obsolete vessels
- **Environmental Concerns:** Nearby Newport News, Portsmouth, and Norfolk shipyards have limited interest in recycling due to pollution risks
- **Government Sales:** MARAD resumed sales from the James River "ghost fleet" for disposal

### Historical Context
The James River Reserve Fleet is colloquially known as the "ghost fleet" and is visible from areas along the James River near Fort Eustis. The fleet has been dramatically reduced from its Cold War peak of several hundred vessels.

## 2. YORK RIVER ANCHORAGES

### Location Details
- **Site:** York River, Virginia
- **Visibility:** Viewable from Yorktown waterfront
- **Regulatory Status:** Naval anchorage reserved for exclusive use of naval vessels

### Current Status
- **Vessel Information:** Limited public information available on current vessels
- **Anchorage Designation:** Between Yorktown and Naval Mine Depot
- **Primary Use:** U.S. Navy vessel anchorage

### Management
- **Authority:** Captain of the Port, Norfolk, Virginia
- **Regulatory Reference:** 33 CFR 110.166 - York River, Va., naval anchorage

### Research Notes
The York River anchorage is primarily designated for active naval vessels rather than NDRF ghost fleet storage. Limited information is available on specific vessels currently at anchor in this location. This may include transient MSC vessels or vessels awaiting maintenance at nearby Norfolk facilities.

## 3. BEAUMONT RESERVE FLEET (BRF)

### Location Details
- **Site:** Beaumont, Texas
- **Coordinates:** 30°1′30″N 94°0′39″W
- **Waterway:** Neches River at McFadden Bend Cutoff
- **Status:** Active NDRF site (only Gulf Coast anchorage remaining)

### Current Fleet Status (2025)
- **Vessel Count:** 27 vessels (as of July 2025)
- **Capacity:** Capable of holding over 200 large vessels
- **Draft Limitation:** Maximum 16 feet
- **Holding Ground:** Mud and silt bottom provides good holding

### Fleet Composition (August 2025)
**Ready Reserve Force (RRF) - 18 vessels:**
- Roll-On/Roll-Off (RO/RO) ships optimized for rapid vehicle deployment
- Core of the Beaumont fleet

**Retention Vessels:**
- MV Cape Taylor (T-AKR-113)
- MV Cape Texas (T-AKR-112)
- MV Cape Trinity (T-AKR-116)
- MV Cape Victory (T-AKR-9701)
- MV Cape Vincent (T-AKR-9666) - ROS-5 status with 10-man maintenance crew
- USNS Algol (T-AKR-287)
- USNS Antares (T-AKR-294)
- SS Denebola (T-AKR-289)

**Non-Retention (Disposal) Status:**
- MV Cape Intrepid (T-AKR-11) - Retired from RRF October 1, 2025, placed in non-retention

### Historical Context
- **Established:** 1946 by act of Congress
- **Construction:** USGS excavated 24 million cubic yards of soil to create McFadden Bend Cutoff
- **Peak Capacity:** Supported over 100 vessels during mid-20th century

### Recent Activity (2024-2025)
- **Management Contracts:** Part of $6.2 billion in contracts awarded July 2025 for RRF vessel management
- **Disposal Program:** Accelerating removal of obsolete vessels (break-bulk carriers, barges)
- **Hurricane Protection:** Cape Vincent and Cape Victory used to shelter emergency equipment during Hurricane Ike (2008)

### Strategic Importance
- Only NDRF anchorage on Gulf Coast
- Critical for rapid deployment to Latin America, Caribbean, and Gulf region operations
- Supports Military Sealift Command prepositioning requirements

## 4. SUISUN BAY RESERVE FLEET (SBRF)

### Location Details
- **Site:** Benicia, California
- **Waterway:** Northwest side of Suisun Bay
- **Common Name:** "Mothball Fleet"

### Current Fleet Status (2025)
- **Vessel Count:** 9 vessels (as of September-October 2025)
- **Historical Peak:** 324 vessels (1956)
- **Total Historic:** Approximately 1,000 ships since 1946

### Recent Activity
- **Dramatic Reduction:** From 57 obsolete vessels targeted for removal in 2009 consent decree
- **Environmental Remediation:** Seven-year program mandated phased removal by September 2017
- **Current Status:** Minimal fleet remaining after environmental compliance efforts

### Management
- **Oversight:** U.S. Maritime Administration (MARAD) military caretaker
- **Purpose:** Buffer ships for national defense and emergency purposes

## 6. KEY FINDINGS AND DISCREPANCIES

### IMO Number Discrepancies
Several vessels in the project dataset show different IMO numbers than official naval vessel registries. This requires verification:

1. **Cape Washington:** Dataset shows 8214556, official records show 7826178
2. **Cape Vincent:** Dataset shows 8214570, official records show 7729562
3. **Bob Hope Class LMSRs:** Dataset shows 914xxxx series, official records show 908xxxx series

### Status Clarifications

**Bob Hope and Fisher:**
- Both vessels were decommissioned in October 2022
- Transferred to MARAD custody
- No longer on active Naval Vessel Register
- Dataset may show outdated "Operational" or "Ready Reserve" status

**Cape Vincent Location:**
- Dataset shows "Norfolk" as home port
- Current actual location: Beaumont Reserve Fleet, Texas
- This may reflect administrative home port vs. physical layberth location

### USNS Gilliland Verification Issue
No vessel named "USNS GILLILAND" appears in official Military Sealift Command or Naval Vessel Register records. Possibilities:
1. Name spelling error
2. Confusion with USNS MENDONCA (T-AKR-303)
3. Historical vessel no longer in service
4. Dataset entry error

## 7. RECENT DEVELOPMENTS (2024-2025)

### Fleet Management
**July 30, 2025:** MARAD awarded 20 contracts totaling $6.2 billion over 10 years to seven ship management firms for Ready Reserve Force operations, focusing on:
- Accelerated crewing procedures
- Faster activation protocols (5-day target maintained)
- Enhanced maintenance and logistics support
- Improved readiness for 51 RRF vessels

### Disposal and Scrapping Activity

**James River Fleet:**
- Only 4 vessels remaining (September 2024)
- 2 designated for retention, 2 for disposal
- 1 disposal vessel already under contract for removal
- Fleet reduced to smallest size in history

**Beaumont Fleet:**
- Cape Intrepid retired October 1, 2025 and moved to non-retention status
- Focus on removing obsolete break-bulk carriers and barges
- 27 vessels currently maintained (July 2025)

**Suisun Bay Fleet:**
- Only 9 vessels remaining (October 2025)
- Successful completion of environmental remediation program
- Dramatic reduction from 324 vessels at peak

### Strategic Focus
- Emphasis on maintaining RO/RO capability for rapid military vehicle deployment
- Consolidation of retention vessels at Beaumont and Baltimore
- Phasing out older break-bulk and general cargo types
- Focus on vessels that can achieve 5-day activation standard

## 8. BUSINESS INTELLIGENCE FOR MARITIME SERVICE PROVIDERS

### Ship Agency Opportunities

**RRF Activation Events:**
- 51 vessels require crewing, provisioning, and port services for activation
- 5-10 day activation window creates intense demand for services
- Layberth ports (Baltimore, Norfolk, Portsmouth, Charleston, Beaumont) are key locations

**Maintenance and Repair:**
- Continuous maintenance contracts for RRF vessels in ROS status
- Dry-docking and inspection cycles for retention vessels
- Potential for long-term service agreements with ship management firms

### Disposal and Recycling Services
- James River fleet disposal program ongoing
- Beaumont obsolete vessel removal accelerating
- Environmental remediation requirements increase complexity and cost
- Qualified recycling facilities needed for compliant dismantling

### MSC Civilian Contractor Landscape
**Current Primary Contractors (2025):**
- Maersk Line Limited (dominant player - multiple vessel classes)
- American Steamship Company (LMSR operations)
- Crowley Maritime Corporation (ESB, expeditionary vessels)
- Intrepid Ship Management (fleet replenishment oilers)
- Austal USA (Expeditionary Fast Transport)

**Opportunities:**
- Stevedoring for RRF activation loading operations
- Ship chandlery and provisioning services at layberth ports
- Tug and linesmen services for vessel movements
- Technical services (communications, electronics, HVAC, marine engineering)
