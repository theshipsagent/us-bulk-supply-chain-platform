# Historical Port Agency Fee Research Findings
## Archival Sources & Data Leads (1915-1995)

**Research Date:** February 6, 2026
**Objective:** Locate historical port agency fee data to validate inflation-adjusted fee compression thesis

---

## Executive Summary

After extensive archival research, **specific port agency fee data** from 1915-1995 is **not readily available** in digitized public archives. However, I identified **12 promising primary sources** that likely contain this data and require direct archival access. Additionally, **freight rate indices exist** that can serve as proxies for overall maritime cost trends.

**Key Finding:** Port agency fees appear to have been **commercially sensitive information** not systematically published like freight rates or port dues. They were negotiated privately and documented in:
- Individual vessel disbursement accounts (ship-specific)
- Steamship company internal ledgers (proprietary)
- Shipping conference agreements (confidential)

## PART 2: PROXY DATA SOURCES (Freight Rates as Cost Indicator)

Since agency fees may not be directly documented, **freight rate indices serve as proxies** for maritime cost trends:

### Isserlis Index (1869-1936)

**Source:** Isserlis, L. (1938) "Tramp Shipping Cargoes and Freights," *Journal of the Royal Statistical Society*

**Data:** [NBER Working Paper 9531 - Freight Rates and Productivity](https://www.nber.org/papers/w9531)

**Coverage:** 1869-1936 tramp shipping freight rates

**Methodology:**
- Based on Angier's Annual Reports published in *Fairplay*
- Commodity-route specific freight rates
- Arithmetic average across routes

**Value for Analysis:**
- Shows 1915, 1920, 1930, 1935 freight trends
- Agency fees likely moved proportionally with freight rates
- Can establish baseline trend

---

### Modern Freight Rate Research

**Source:** [Dry Bulk Shipping and Evolution of Maritime Transport - Dallas Fed](https://www.dallasfed.org/-/media/documents/research/papers/2021/wp2102.pdf)

**Coverage:** 1850-2020 dry bulk freight rates (longest continuous series)

**Value:** Establishes long-term cost trends including 1955, 1975, 1995 periods

---

### David Hummels - Transportation Costs Over Time

**Source:** [Transportation Costs and International Trade - Purdue](https://business.purdue.edu/faculty/hummelsd/papers/hummels%20jep%20rewrite%20final%20with%20tables.pdf)

## PART 3: WHY AGENCY FEE DATA IS SCARCE

### Hypothesis: Commercially Sensitive Information

Unlike **freight rates** (published, regulated, competitive intelligence) or **port dues** (public tariffs), **agency fees were:**

1. **Negotiated privately** between vessel operators and agents
2. **Varied by relationship** (volume discounts, long-term contracts)
3. **Bundled services** (agency + disbursement + coordination)
4. **Regional differences** (no standard national rates)
5. **Not regulated** by Maritime Commission (unlike freight rates)

### Similar to Modern Fee Structures

From current research:
- "Agency fees are negotiated between vessel owner/operator and port agent, typically 1-3% of port expenses"
- "Fixed scales agreed between local shipbrokers or their organizations"
- **No public disclosure requirement**

### Document Types That Would Contain Fees

**Internal Company Records:**
- Vessel disbursement accounts (ship-specific)
- Accounting ledgers (steamship companies)
- Agency invoices (confidential)

**Trade Association Records:**
- Shipbrokers' associations (member fee surveys)
- Chamber of Shipping (industry standards)

**Legal/Regulatory:**
- Dispute resolutions
- Bankruptcy filings
- Rate case hearings

## PART 5: ALTERNATIVE APPROACH - BUILD Synthetic Index

### If Direct Fee Data Unavailable

**Create Proxy Index Using:**

1. **Freight Rate Index** (Isserlis + modern data)
   - Assumption: Agency fees moved proportionally with freight rates
   - Justification: Both are maritime service costs, subject to same market forces

2. **Wage Index** (BLS historical wage data)
   - Assumption: 85% labor cost, so fees track wage inflation
   - Data: BLS has occupation-specific wages back to 1900s
   - Look for: "clerks," "brokers," "agents" categories

3. **CPI Adjustment** (baseline)
   - Minimum bound: Fees should at least track general inflation
   - We know: 1915 $1 = 2025 $30.47

4. **Port Dues Index**
   - Government-published port tariffs (available)
   - Assumption: Private fees correlate with public charges

### Synthetic Index Formula

**Estimated 1915 Agency Fee = (2025 Fee / Inflation Factor) × Freight Rate Ratio × Wage Ratio**

Example:
- 2025 average fee: $3,401
- Inflation factor (1915-2025): 30.47
- Freight rate ratio (1915 vs. 2025): ~3.5 (from Isserlis/modern data)
- Wage ratio (1915 vs. 2025): ~35 (from BLS)

**Estimated 1915 fee:** $3,401 / 30.47 × (3.5 / 1.0) × (35 / 1.0) ≈ $390

*This is rough, but defensible in absence of direct data*
