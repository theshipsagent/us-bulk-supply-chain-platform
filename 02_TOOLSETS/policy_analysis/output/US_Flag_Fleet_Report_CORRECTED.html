2. NDRF Ghost Fleets & Ready Reserve Force

2.1 James River Reserve Fleet (Fort Eustis, Virginia)

Location: 37°07′13″N 76°38′47″W (James River at Fort Eustis)

Current Status: 4 vessels remaining (down from 800+ at Cold War peak)

Composition: 2 retention vessels, 2 designated for disposal

Historical: Oldest NDRF site (established 1925), largely dismantled due to environmental concerns

The James River Reserve Fleet, commonly known as the "Ghost Fleet," anchors in the James River off Mulberry Island near Fort Eustis. Once holding over 700 vessels after WWII, the fleet has been systematically reduced through scrapping programs. Vessels hold an estimated 13 million gallons of oil/fuel, requiring environmental monitoring.

2.2 Beaumont Reserve Fleet (Sabine-Neches River, Texas)

Location: 30°1′30″N 94°0′39″W (Neches River, McFadden Bend Cutoff)

Current Status: 27 vessels (as of July 2025)

Composition: Primarily RoRo ships in Ready Reserve Force (ROS-5 status)

Notable: Only Gulf Coast NDRF anchorage, 200+ vessel capacity

Known Vessels at Beaumont: CAPE VINCENT (T-AKR-9666), CAPE TAYLOR, CAPE TEXAS, CAPE TRINITY, USNS ALGOL, USNS ANTARES. Recent activity includes Cape Intrepid retirement (Oct 2025) and ongoing disposal of obsolete vessels through Brownsville scrapping operations.

2.3 York River Anchorages (Yorktown, Virginia)

York River serves as active Navy anchorage rather than NDRF storage site. Vessels viewable from Yorktown waterfront are typically rotational Navy units under operational control of Captain of the Port, Norfolk. Not part of ghost fleet inventory.

7. Ship Disposal & Brownsville Scrapping Operations

7.1 Why Brownsville?

Brownsville, Texas captures 85% of US Navy and MARAD ship recycling business

Three major facilities: International Shipbreaking (ISL/EMR), All Star Metals, SteelCoast

Capacity: Up to 9 vessels simultaneously, ships to 1,000 feet length

Strategic advantages: Deep draft access, Gulf location, specialized equipment, environmental compliance

Brownsville's dominance in ship recycling stems from infrastructure investments made in the 1990s. International Shipbreaking Limited (now EMR) established "the largest and most specialized facility for ship dismantling in the United States" in 1995. The Port of Brownsville's location on the Gulf Coast provides economical towing routes from both coasts and eliminates Panama Canal transit required for West Coast shipbreakers.

7.3 MARAD Ship Disposal Program

MARAD serves as the US Government's disposal agent for merchant vessels ≥1,500 GT no longer useful for defense missions. The Ship Disposal Program prioritizes vessels presenting highest environmental risk. Between 2000-2025, over 150 obsolete vessels were removed from NDRF ghost fleets through competitive bidding to qualified recyclers.

Current disposal priorities:

  • James River fleet reduction (target: complete disposal by 2030)
  • Suisun Bay environmental remediation (9 vessels remaining)
  • Beaumont fleet modernization (obsolete RoRos being replaced)
  • Decommissioned Navy vessels (carriers, cruisers, amphibs)

7.4 Business Opportunity - Ghost Fleet Towing & Preparation

Towing contracts from NDRF anchorages to Brownsville typically range $500K-$3M depending on vessel size and origin. Pre-scrapping services include fuel/oil removal, hazmat surveys, and stability certification. Maritime service providers in Norfolk/Hampton Roads positioned for James River fleet towing contracts as disposal accelerates.
