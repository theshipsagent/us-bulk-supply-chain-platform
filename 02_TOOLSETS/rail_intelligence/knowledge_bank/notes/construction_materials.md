# Construction Materials Across Class I Railroads

**Created:** 2026-02-23
**Focus:** Cement, Aggregates, Steel, Lumber, Building Materials

---

## Overview

Construction materials represent a significant commodity group for all Class I railroads, with traffic closely tied to housing starts, infrastructure spending, and commercial construction activity. This document consolidates information on cement terminals, aggregate facilities, steel distribution, and lumber handling across all six carriers.

## Cement

### Norfolk Southern
- **15 cement facilities** served
- Major cement terminals across Southeast and Mid-Atlantic
- Portland cement, bulk distribution
- Cement terminals for concrete batch plants
- Pneumatic covered hopper cars

### BNSF
- Cement terminals across western U.S.
- Transload facilities for distribution
- Unit train service for high-volume terminals
- Direct service to major metropolitan markets (LA, Phoenix, Denver, Dallas, Seattle)

### Union Pacific
- Cement plants and distribution terminals
- Western coverage (California, Arizona, Nevada, Utah, Colorado, Texas)
- Import cement via West Coast ports (Asia sources)

### CSX
- Cement facilities across eastern territory
- Serve concrete producers
- Atlantic and Gulf port access for imports

### Canadian National
- Limited cement coverage (primarily Ontario/Quebec)
- Some U.S. Midwest facilities (ex-IC territory)

### CPKC
- Canadian cement facilities (western Canada)
- Mexico cement production (single-line advantage)

**Equipment:** Pneumatic covered hopper cars (pressure discharge)
**Service:** Unit trains for large volumes, manifest for smaller shipments
**Market Drivers:** Housing starts, commercial construction, infrastructure projects

## Aggregates (Sand, Gravel, Crushed Stone)

### Norfolk Southern — MARKET LEADER
- **67 quarries** served
- **30 aggregate distribution yards**
- Largest aggregate network among Class I carriers
- Crushed stone, gravel, sand
- Railroad ballast production

**Key Markets:**
- Road construction (DOT projects)
- Concrete production (batch plants)
- Asphalt production
- Railroad maintenance (ballast)

### BNSF
- Aggregate facilities across western network
- Frac sand (Powder River Basin, Wisconsin sources to oil/gas fields)
- Construction aggregates (California, Arizona, Texas, Pacific Northwest)
- Unit train service for high-volume quarries

**Frac Sand Specialty:**
- Wisconsin sand (high-quality proppant for hydraulic fracturing)
- Unit trains to Permian Basin (Texas/New Mexico)
- Unit trains to Bakken (North Dakota)
- Peak volumes during oil/gas boom periods

### Union Pacific
- Aggregate quarries across western U.S.
- Frac sand movements (similar to BNSF)
- Construction materials to Southwest growth markets

### CSX
- Quarries and aggregate facilities
- Southeast and Mid-Atlantic coverage
- Appalachian stone quarries

### Canadian National
- Limited aggregate coverage
- Some Ontario/Quebec quarries
- Ballast for railroad use

### CPKC
- Western Canada quarries
- Some U.S. and Mexico facilities

**Equipment:** Open-top hoppers, gondolas
**Service:** Unit trains for quarry-to-distribution, manifest for local delivery
**Market Drivers:** Infrastructure spending, housing, commercial construction

## Steel Products

### Types of Steel Shipped by Rail
- **Coiled Steel:** Sheet metal, automotive steel
- **Structural Steel:** Beams, columns, construction
- **Plate Steel:** Heavy manufacturing, shipbuilding
- **Pipe:** Line pipe (oil/gas), casing, tubing
- **Rebar:** Reinforcing bar for concrete
- **Rail:** Railroad track rail
- **Slabs/Billets:** Semi-finished steel (mill-to-mill)

### Norfolk Southern — TRANSPORTATION LEADER
- **50+ steel processors** served
- **150 scrap metal locations** served
- Largest steel transportation network
- Southeast steel mini-mills (Alabama, South Carolina, Kentucky)
- Midwest access via interchange (Gary, Cleveland, Pittsburgh)

**Key Facilities:**
- Steel service centers
- Automotive steel (stamping plants)
- Scrap yards (steel recycling)

### CSX
- Steel mills and processors
- Midwest steel corridor (via Conrail Shared Assets)
- Gary, IN; Cleveland, OH; Pittsburgh, PA (legacy steel regions)
- Import steel via Atlantic ports
- Domestic mini-mills in Southeast

### Union Pacific
- Western steel service centers
- Import steel via West Coast ports
- Pipe distribution (oil/gas industry)
- Scrap export to Asia

### BNSF
- Western steel distribution
- Pipe (Texas oil/gas, wind energy)
- Import steel via West Coast ports
- Scrap export via PNW ports

### Canadian National
- Ontario/Quebec steel (Hamilton, Sault Ste. Marie)
- Import/export via Great Lakes, Atlantic ports

### CPKC
- Limited steel coverage
- Some Ontario steel
- Mexico steel (Monterrey region)

**Equipment:**
- Coil cars (for steel coils)
- Flatcars (for structural steel, plate, pipe)
- Gondolas (for scrap metal, semi-finished steel)

**Market Drivers:**
- Automotive production
- Construction activity

## Unit Train vs. Manifest Service

### Unit Trains (100+ cars, dedicated commodity)
**Common for:**
- Cement (terminal-to-terminal)
- Aggregates (quarry-to-distribution yard)
- Frac sand (Wisconsin to oil fields)
- Steel coils (mill-to-automotive plant, high-volume)

**Advantages:**
- Lower rates (higher volume)
- Faster transit (no intermediate switching)
- Dedicated equipment

### Manifest Freight (mixed commodity trains)
**Common for:**
- Lumber (reload centers, distribution)
- Steel products (service centers, smaller volumes)
- Building materials (local distribution)
- Smaller cement/aggregate shipments

**Advantages:**
- Flexibility (smaller volumes)
- Multiple origins/destinations
- No minimum volume requirements

## Construction Materials Service Characteristics

### Loading Facilities

**Cement Plants:**
- Rotary kiln production
- Pneumatic loading (covered hoppers)
- Silo storage for railcar loading

**Quarries:**
- Conveyor loading (open-top hoppers)
- Screening and sizing on-site
- Direct railcar loading or truck transload

**Steel Mills/Service Centers:**
- Overhead cranes (coil cars)
- Forklift/side-loader (flatcars for structural/plate)
- Indoor/outdoor storage

**Lumber Mills:**
- Forklift/crane loading (centerbeam cars)
- Banding and securing
- Weather protection considerations

### Unloading Facilities

**Cement Terminals:**
- Pneumatic discharge (pressure unloading)
- Silo storage
- Truck transload to concrete plants

**Aggregate Distribution Yards:**
- Rotary dumpers or bottom-dump hoppers
- Stockpile storage
- Truck loading for final delivery

**Steel Service Centers:**
- Overhead cranes
- Processing equipment (slitting, cutting)
- Warehouse storage

**Lumber Reload Centers:**
- Forklift unloading
- Repackaging for truck delivery
- Distribution to multiple customers

## Market Trends & Outlook

### Positive Drivers
- **Infrastructure Investment:** Federal infrastructure bills (roads, bridges)
- **Housing Starts:** Demand for lumber, cement, aggregates, steel
- **Nearshoring:** New manufacturing facilities (steel, cement demand)
- **Energy Transition:** Wind turbines (steel, aggregates for foundations)

### Challenges
- **Cyclical Demand:** Construction materials highly sensitive to economic cycles
- **Competition:** Trucking dominance for local/short-haul aggregates and lumber
- **Commodity Prices:** Steel imports, lumber prices volatile
- **Supply Chain:** Cement shortages, lumber mill capacity constraints

### Rail Advantages
- **Cost-Effective:** Long-haul unit trains competitive with trucking
- **Capacity:** Unit trains can move large volumes efficiently
- **Network Access:** Railroads serve quarries, mills, terminals in remote locations
- **Environmental:** Lower emissions than trucking for bulk materials
