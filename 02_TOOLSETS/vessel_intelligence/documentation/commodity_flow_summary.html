Lower Mississippi River Commodity Flow Summary

Analysis Period: 2023-2025 (3 years)
Data Source: Panjiva import manifests, USACE vessel tracking, National supply chain mapping
Generated: February 23, 2026

1. Pig Iron - Steel Mill Feedstock Flow

Volume: 8.0 million tons (3-year period)
Origins: Brazil, Ukraine, Poland
Entry Point: Lower Mississippi terminals (New Orleans area)
Final Destinations: 7 Electric Arc Furnace (EAF) steel mills
FROM: Ocean vessels from Brazil, Ukraine, Poland
TO: Lower Mississippi terminals (New Orleans area)
THEN TO: Barge transport upriver to 7 steel mills:
  • Nucor Steel Louisiana - Convent, LA (Mississippi River, RM 162)
  • Big River Steel - Osceola, AR (Mississippi River)
  • Nucor Steel Arkansas - Blytheville, AR (Mississippi River)
  • Steel Dynamics Columbus - Columbus, MS (Tennessee-Tombigbee Waterway)
  • Nucor Steel Gallatin - Ghent, KY (Ohio River)
  • Nucor Steel Tuscaloosa - Tuscaloosa, AL (Black Warrior River → Tombigbee → Mississippi)
  • Commercial Metals Company - Multiple locations on Mississippi/Arkansas rivers
WHY THIS FLOW: EAF steel mills consume pig iron as metallic charge (15-30% of total input) to produce structural steel, flat-rolled steel, and rebar. These 7 mills are located on navigable waterways specifically to receive bulk raw materials by barge. Pig iron arrives by ocean vessel at Lower Mississippi deep-draft terminals, then transfers to barges for inland distribution to mills that cannot be reached by ocean-going vessels.

2. Fertilizer - Upriver to Agricultural Regions

Volume: 26.6 million tons (3-year period)
Origins: Russia, Peru, Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Morocco
Products: Urea, Phosphates, Phosphate Rock, Potash, Sulphur
Entry Point: Lower Mississippi terminals (New Orleans area)
Transport Direction: UPRIVER to Midwest agricultural regions
FROM: Ocean vessels from Russia, Peru, Saudi Arabia, Qatar
TO: Lower Mississippi terminals (New Orleans area)
THEN TO: Barge transport UPRIVER to four major agricultural regions:
  • Illinois/Iowa Grain Belt - Via Illinois Waterway, 773 miles upriver → Chicago/Illinois River corridor (190 facilities including grain elevators, fertilizer terminals)
  • Memphis/Arkansas Delta - Via Mississippi River, 496 miles upriver → Memphis/Cincinnati corridor (292 facilities) → Missouri/Arkansas/Tennessee farmland
  • Ohio River Valley - Via Ohio River, 600-1000 miles → Ohio/Indiana/Kentucky farmland (73 facilities on Ohio River corridor)
  • Upper Mississippi - Via Mississippi River north, 1050 miles → St. Paul/Minnesota region (19 facilities) → Minnesota/Wisconsin farmland
Key Terminals: Helm Fertilizer Terminal (Memphis), Pemiscot County Port Authority (Caruthersville, MO), Mississippi Lime Terminal (Ste. Genevieve, MO), Illinois River fertilizer distribution network
WHY THIS FLOW: Corn and soybean production in the Midwest requires nitrogen, phosphate, and potash fertilizers. Ocean vessels deliver bulk fertilizer to Lower Mississippi deep-draft terminals, then barges distribute upriver to agricultural regions where crops are grown. Fertilizer moves UP the river system in spring (March-May peak for planting season), while harvested grain returns DOWN the river system in fall - creating bidirectional seasonal flows on the same barge infrastructure.

3. Grain - Downriver Export Flow (REVERSE of Fertilizer)

Products: Corn, soybeans, wheat
Origin Regions: 178 grain elevators across Midwest
Transport Direction: DOWNRIVER to export terminals
Exit Point: Lower Mississippi export terminals (New Orleans area)
Final Destination: Ocean vessels to Asia, Latin America
FROM: 178 upriver grain elevators in agricultural regions:
  • Illinois/Iowa - 68 elevators → Via Illinois Waterway, 773 miles DOWNRIVER
  • Missouri/Arkansas - 76 elevators → Via Mississippi River, 496 miles DOWNRIVER
  • Ohio River Valley - Multiple elevators → Via Ohio River to Mississippi
  • Upper Mississippi - Multiple elevators → Via Mississippi River south, 1050 miles DOWNRIVER
TO: Lower Mississippi export terminals:
  • Cargill Reserve Terminal - Reserve, LA
  • ADM Ama Terminal - Ama, LA
  • Bunge Destrehan - Destrehan, LA
  • Louis Dreyfus Company - New Orleans area
  • CHS Myrtle Grove - Myrtle Grove, LA
THEN TO: Ocean vessels to Asia, Latin America, global markets
WHY THIS FLOW: Grain production occurs in Midwest agricultural regions (Illinois, Iowa, Missouri, Arkansas, Minnesota), but export demand requires access to deep-draft ocean terminals. Grain flows DOWNRIVER (opposite direction from fertilizer) via barge from 178 inland elevators to Lower Mississippi export terminals where it transfers to ocean vessels. This creates seasonal bidirectional symmetry: fertilizer barges move UP in spring (March-May), grain barges move DOWN in fall (September-November), using the same river infrastructure in both directions.

4. Crude Oil - Two-Source Refinery Supply

Vessel Imports: 135.8 million tons (3-year period)
Origins: Mexico, Venezuela, Colombia, Canada, Kuwait, Middle East
Import Terminals: LOOP offshore terminal + 5 river terminals
Destinations: 37 Gulf Coast refineries (Louisiana, Texas, Mississippi, Alabama)
SOURCE 1 - VESSEL IMPORTS:
FROM: Ocean vessels (VLCCs, Suezmax, Aframax) from Mexico, Venezuela, Colombia, Middle East
TO: Two types of terminals:
  • LOOP (Louisiana Offshore Oil Port) - 18 miles offshore Louisiana → Pipeline to multiple refineries → Handles largest vessels (VLCCs) → 50+ million barrels storage
  • River Terminals:
    • Marathon St. James Terminal - St. James, LA (Mississippi River) → Marathon Garyville refinery
    • Shell Norco Terminal - Norco, LA (Mississippi River) → Shell Norco refinery
    • ExxonMobil Baton Rouge Terminal - Baton Rouge, LA (Mississippi River) → ExxonMobil Baton Rouge refinery
    • Phillips 66 Alliance Terminal - Belle Chasse, LA (Mississippi River) → Phillips 66 Alliance refinery
SOURCE 2 - DOMESTIC PIPELINES:
  • Permian Basin (West Texas) → EPIC, Gray Oak, Cactus II pipelines → Houston/Corpus Christi refineries
  • Eagle Ford Shale (South Texas) → Eagle Ford Pipeline → Corpus Christi refineries
  • Louisiana Production (onshore/offshore) → Local gathering systems → Louisiana refineries
FINAL DESTINATIONS: 37 Gulf Coast refineries including:
ExxonMobil Baton Rouge (502K bpd), Marathon Garyville (578K bpd), Motiva Port Arthur (607K bpd), Valero Port Arthur (335K bpd), Phillips 66 Alliance (247K bpd), Valero St. Charles (340K bpd), and 31 others in LA/TX/MS/AL region
WHY THIS FLOW: Gulf Coast refineries require crude oil feedstock to produce gasoline, diesel, jet fuel, and petrochemical feedstocks. These refineries are located on the Gulf Coast specifically to receive crude from TWO sources: (1) International vessel imports via LOOP offshore terminal and river terminals on Lower Mississippi, (2) Domestic pipeline supplies from Texas oil fields (Permian Basin, Eagle Ford Shale). The two-source model allows refineries to blend different crude types: heavy/sour crude from vessel imports (Mexico, Venezuela, Middle East) with light/sweet crude from domestic pipelines (Texas production). Products then distribute via Colonial Pipeline (gasoline/diesel to East Coast), vessel exports (Latin America/Europe), and local Gulf Coast consumption.

5. Cement - Imports Supplement Domestic Production

Volume: 19.6 million tons (3-year period)
Origins: Turkey, Vietnam, Saudi Arabia, Egypt, Algeria
Import Terminals: Gulf Coast cement terminals
End Markets: Gulf Coast construction (Houston, New Orleans) + Florida markets
FROM: Ocean vessels from Turkey, Vietnam, Saudi Arabia, Egypt
TO: Gulf Coast import terminals:
  • Argos Cement Terminal - New Orleans area (cement clinker, finished cement)
  • Cemex Distribution Terminal - Gulf Coast (Portland cement, masonry cement)
  • Lafarge/Holcim Terminals - Multiple Gulf Coast locations (clinker, cement, aggregates)
  • Holcim Vicksburg Terminal - Vicksburg, MS (Yazoo River connection to Mississippi River)
THEN TO: Three distribution patterns:
  • Truck Delivery to Gulf Coast Cities: Houston (Texas coast), New Orleans (Louisiana), Baton Rouge, Mobile (Alabama) - Construction markets within 100-mile truck radius of import terminals
  • Barge Distribution Upriver: Via Mississippi River to inland construction markets along river corridor (Memphis, St. Louis, Mississippi River cities)
  • Truck Delivery to Florida: From Gulf Coast terminals to Florida construction markets (Miami, Tampa, Jacksonville, Orlando)
DOMESTIC COMPLEMENT: 60 U.S. cement plants provide majority of supply:
  • Ohio River Valley: Ohio (10 plants), Kentucky (3 plants) - Limestone-rich geology → Barge distribution on Ohio River
  • Tennessee/Missouri: Tennessee (9 plants), Missouri (7 plants) - Limestone deposits → Barge distribution on Mississippi
  • Upper Midwest: Michigan (7 plants), Wisconsin (7 plants), Illinois (5 plants), Minnesota (4 plants)
WHY THIS FLOW: Cement production requires limestone deposits and is extremely heavy/low-value (high transport costs). Domestic cement plants are located near limestone quarries (Ohio River Valley, Tennessee, Missouri) and distribute via barge where possible. Vessel imports supplement domestic production in coastal regions where: (1) No nearby cement plants exist (Texas Gulf Coast has 0 plants), (2) Truck haul distance from nearest plant exceeds 100 miles, (3) Construction demand is high (Houston petrochemical/port construction, New Orleans infrastructure, Florida residential). Products flow from import terminals by truck (local delivery <100 miles) or barge (Mississippi River corridor distribution to inland markets).

6. Steel Products - Finished Steel Imports

Volume: 8.0 million tons (3-year period)
Origins: Brazil, Japan, South Korea, Turkey, Mexico, Vietnam
Products: Flat-rolled steel, rebar, structural beams, pipe/tube, stainless steel
Entry Point: Lower Mississippi terminals (New Orleans area)
End Markets: Gulf Coast manufacturers and construction projects
FROM: Ocean vessels from Brazil, Japan, South Korea, Turkey, Mexico, Vietnam
TO: Lower Mississippi terminals (New Orleans area)
THEN TO: Gulf Coast end markets via truck/rail:
  • Oil & Gas Sector: Tubular goods (pipe, OCTG) → Houston/Gulf Coast drilling operations, pipeline construction projects
  • Construction: Rebar, structural beams → Gulf Coast building projects, infrastructure construction (Houston, New Orleans, Baton Rouge, Mobile)
  • Manufacturing: Flat-rolled steel, sheet steel → Automotive plants, fabrication facilities, appliance manufacturers in Gulf region
  • Petrochemical: Stainless steel, specialty steel → Chemical plant construction, tank fabrication, corrosion-resistant applications
PRODUCTS BY TYPE:
  • Flat-Rolled Steel: Sheet, plate, coil → Automotive, appliances, construction cladding
  • Long Products: Rebar, structural beams, wire rod → Construction, reinforcement, infrastructure
  • Tubular Goods: Pipe, tube (OCTG, line pipe, standard pipe) → Oil/gas drilling, pipelines, structural
  • Stainless Steel: Corrosion-resistant alloy → Food processing, chemical, architectural
DOMESTIC COMPLEMENT: 55 U.S. steel mills also supply Gulf Coast markets:
  • Great Lakes Mills: Michigan (11 mills), Illinois (9 mills), Pennsylvania (8 mills) → Rail/truck distribution to Gulf Coast
  • Gulf Coast Mills: Louisiana (2 mills including Nucor Louisiana), Tennessee (6 mills), Mississippi (3 mills), Kentucky (3 mills), Alabama (3 mills) → Local supply to Gulf region
WHY THIS FLOW: Gulf Coast construction, oil/gas operations, and manufacturing require finished steel products. Vessels deliver imported steel (flat-rolled, rebar, pipe/tube, stainless) to Lower Mississippi terminals, then distribute by truck/rail to end users within the Gulf Coast region. Major consumers include: (1) Oil/gas sector - tubular goods for drilling operations and pipeline construction, (2) Construction projects - rebar and structural steel for buildings and infrastructure in Houston, New Orleans, Gulf Coast cities, (3) Petrochemical plants - stainless steel and specialty steel for chemical facilities. Vessel imports supplement domestic production from 55 U.S. steel mills (Great Lakes region + Gulf Coast regional mills).

7. Aggregates - Domestic Barge Distribution (NOT Vessel Imports)

Flow Type: DOMESTIC ONLY (vessel "imports" are misclassified)
Products: Sand, gravel, crushed stone, limestone
Origin Points: 45 aggregate terminals on river system
Destinations: Urban construction markets along waterways
TRUE FLOW (Domestic Barge Distribution):
FROM: 45 aggregate terminals along Mississippi River system:
  • Ohio River Valley: 13 terminals in Indiana (7), Pennsylvania (7), Ohio (3)
    • Mulzer Crushed Stone - Rockport, Cape Sandy, Charlestown, Tell City, New Amsterdam (IN)
    • Hanson Aggregates - Coraopolis (PA)
    • Neville Aggregates - Neville Island (PA)
    • Martin Marietta - Cincinnati (OH)
    • Osborne Concrete - Cleveland (OH)
  • Missouri River: 7 terminals in Missouri
    • Fort Bellefontaine Quarry, Central Stone Co., Leuke Hauling - Florissant area (MO)
    • Holliday Sand & Gravel - St. Joseph (MO)
    • Simpson Materials - Imperial (MO)
    • Luhr Bros. - Illmo (MO)
  • Tennessee River: 4 terminals in Tennessee
    • Vulcan Materials - Chattanooga, Memphis (TN)
    • Martin Marietta - Memphis (TN)
  • Illinois Waterway: 2 terminals in Illinois
    • Aggregate Materials - East Dubuque (IL)
    • Heidelberg Materials - Romeoville (IL)
  • Mississippi River: Louisiana (5 terminals), Mississippi (2 terminals)
  • Great Lakes: Michigan (4 terminals), Minnesota (2 terminals)
TO: Urban construction markets along waterways:
  • Chicago/Illinois River - Heidelberg Romeoville terminal → Chicago metropolitan area construction (largest aggregate market)
  • Pittsburgh/Ohio River - Multiple PA/OH terminals → Pittsburgh, Cincinnati, Louisville construction markets
  • St. Louis/Missouri River - Florissant/Fort Bellefontaine terminals → St. Louis construction
  • Memphis/Mississippi River - Memphis terminals → Mid-South construction
  • New Orleans/Lower Mississippi - Louisiana terminals → Gulf Coast construction
PRODUCTS BY TYPE:
  • Sand: River dredging, quarries → Concrete production, mortar, fill ($5-15/ton)
  • Gravel: Quarries, river deposits → Concrete aggregate, road base, drainage ($8-20/ton)
  • Crushed Stone: Limestone/granite quarries → Road base, concrete aggregate, riprap ($10-25/ton)
  • Limestone: Sedimentary rock quarries → Construction aggregate, cement raw material ($8-20/ton)
WHY THIS FLOW: Aggregates are 100% DOMESTIC commodity on the Mississippi River system - vessel imports do NOT occur for bulk construction aggregates. Reason: Transport economics make vessel imports impossible. Aggregates are extremely heavy, low-value commodities ($5-25/ton). Barge transport costs $0.01-0.02 per ton-mile, while ocean vessel + truck delivery would cost 10-20x more. Instead, aggregates flow from local quarries and river deposits to urban construction markets via barge on the Mississippi, Ohio, Illinois, and Missouri rivers. The 45 aggregate terminals load sand, gravel, crushed stone, and limestone onto barges for economical distribution to nearby construction markets (concrete plants, road projects, fill operations). River access = competitive advantage for construction in Chicago, Pittsburgh, Cincinnati, St. Louis, Memphis, and other waterway cities. NOTE: Any "imports" in vessel data are misclassified industrial minerals (TiO2 pigment from Honduras, alumina) or specialty Caribbean sand, NOT bulk construction aggregates.
