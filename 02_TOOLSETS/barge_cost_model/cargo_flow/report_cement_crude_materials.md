# Crude Materials & Cement Transport Flows on U.S. Inland Waterways

*Cargo Flow Analysis Report — February 2026*

---

## Executive Summary

Crude materials — the BTS commodity class encompassing **cement, limestone, sand & gravel, aggregate, iron ore, and other raw minerals** — account for **9.0 billion tons** of cargo moving across the U.S. inland waterway system, representing **17.0% of all waterborne freight**. At an estimated value of **$270.3 billion** (@ $30/ton), this is the second-largest commodity class by volume after petroleum.

The dominant movement pattern is **downstream**: 5.7 billion tons move downstream versus 3.3 billion upstream, producing a net downstream flow of +2.5 billion tons. This reflects the fundamental economics of bulk raw materials — extraction at upstream mining sites, delivery to downstream processing and consumption centres.

Two distinct systems carry the bulk of this traffic:

1. **Great Lakes iron ore corridor** (Lake Superior → St. Mary's River → Lake Huron → Lake Erie) — 4.1 billion tons, primarily taconite/iron ore from the Mesabi Range moving to steelmaking facilities
2. **Inland river system** (Mississippi, Ohio, Tennessee, Arkansas rivers) — 1.8 billion tons, dominated by cement, limestone, aggregate, and construction materials

## Lock Bottlenecks Affecting Crude Materials

The Mississippi River lock system is the primary infrastructure constraint for inland crude materials movement. Most upper Mississippi locks have **600-foot chambers**, requiring standard 15-barge tows (1,200 ft) to split for **double lockage** — roughly doubling transit delay.

## Key Findings

1. **Crude materials are the #2 waterway commodity** at 9.0 billion tons (17% share), behind petroleum (46%).

2. **Two distinct systems**: The Great Lakes carry iron ore downstream (84–87% crude materials concentration). The inland rivers carry cement, limestone, and aggregate in **both directions**, with net upstream flow on the Ohio and Mississippi.

3. **Cement moves upstream** on the major inland rivers — from quarries and plants located along limestone bluffs to urban consumption markets upriver.

4. **Michigan handles 38% of all crude materials** (3.4B tons) — almost entirely Great Lakes iron ore.

5. **Ohio River is the key cement corridor** at 693M tons of crude materials, with strong upstream flow (-284M net) indicating delivery to industrial Midwest.

6. **Lock capacity constrains mid-Mississippi cement movement** — 600-ft chambers on Lock & Dams 1–22 force double lockage, making long-haul upstream cement delivery increasingly uneconomic.

7. **Gulf Coast states (TX, LA) are net importers** of crude materials — construction materials arrive via barge from upriver sources.
