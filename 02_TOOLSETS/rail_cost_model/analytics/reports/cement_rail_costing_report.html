2. About the Data

Understanding how to interpret this data is essential for accurate analysis. The figures in this report come from the STB's Public Use Waybill Sample—a statistical survey, not a complete census.

What is the Waybill Sample?

The Surface Transportation Board (STB) requires Class I railroads to submit a stratified random sample of their carload waybills each year. This sample represents approximately 2-3% of all rail carload movements in the United States. Each sampled waybill includes an "expansion factor" that estimates how many actual shipments that sample record represents.

What "Expanded" Figures Mean

When this report shows "175,000 carloads," that is the statistically expanded estimate of total industry carloads based on the sample. The actual number of sampled waybill records is much smaller. This methodology is similar to political polling—a carefully designed sample is weighted to represent the entire population.

Commodity Classification

Cement is classified under STCC 32 (Stone, Clay, Glass & Concrete Products). This analysis includes Portland cement (32411), hydraulic cement (32419), and related products. These bulk commodities typically move in covered hopper cars with payloads of 100-110 tons per car.

Geographic Coverage

Origin and destination locations are coded using Bureau of Economic Analysis (BEA) Economic Areas—173 geographic zones that aggregate counties into economically meaningful regions. This report focuses on:

  • Houston (BEA 134): Primary cement production region in Texas
  • Tampa (BEA 042): Major Florida construction market
  • New Orleans (BEA 087): Gulf Coast industrial and construction hub

4. Pricing & Distance Analysis

Rail freight rates exhibit a characteristic "taper"—the rate per ton-mile decreases as distance increases. This reflects the high fixed costs of terminal operations being amortized over longer hauls.

Rate Taper Effect

The rate per ton-mile varies significantly by distance:

  • Short haul (<300 miles): $0.10-0.15 per ton-mile — Terminal costs dominate
  • Medium haul (300-600 miles): $0.07-0.10 per ton-mile — Balanced cost structure
  • Long haul (600-1000 miles): $0.05-0.07 per ton-mile — Line-haul efficiency gains
  • Very long haul (>1000 miles): $0.04-0.06 per ton-mile — Maximum taper benefit

7. URCS & R/VC Analysis

The Uniform Rail Costing System (URCS) is the STB's methodology for estimating railroad variable costs. Understanding URCS is essential for evaluating whether freight rates may be subject to regulatory review.

What is URCS?

URCS calculates the incremental variable cost a railroad incurs to move a specific shipment. This includes:

  • Line-haul costs: Fuel, crew, and locomotive costs that vary with distance
  • Car costs: Depreciation and maintenance on freight cars
  • Terminal costs: Switching, loading/unloading, and yard operations
  • Interchange costs: Costs incurred when transferring cars between carriers

URCS excludes fixed infrastructure costs (track, bridges, signals) and return on investment—it measures only the variable, avoidable cost of handling traffic.

What is R/VC Ratio?

The Revenue-to-Variable-Cost (R/VC) ratio compares freight revenue to URCS variable cost:

R/VC Ratio = (Freight Revenue / URCS Variable Cost) × 100%

Interpretation:
• R/VC = 100% → Revenue exactly covers variable cost (no contribution to fixed costs)
• R/VC = 180% → Revenue is 1.8× variable cost (STB jurisdictional threshold)
• R/VC > 180% → Shipper may petition STB for rate reasonableness review

Simplified URCS Formula

For bulk commodities like cement, variable cost can be approximated as:

Variable Cost per Ton ≈ (Distance × $0.035/ton-mile) + $5.00 terminal costs

Example (500 miles):
VC = (500 × $0.035) + $5.00 = $17.50 + $5.00 = $22.50/ton

If Rate = $40/ton: R/VC = ($40 ÷ $22.50) × 100 = 178% (just below threshold)
Regulatory Implication: Under 49 U.S.C. § 10707, the STB can review the reasonableness of rail rates that exceed 180% of variable cost, provided the shipper has no effective transportation alternatives. Short-haul captive lanes often exceed this threshold.

9. Conclusions & Recommendations

For Cement Shippers

  • Benchmark your rates against the distance-band averages in this report
  • Evaluate R/VC exposure on short-haul captive lanes where ratios exceed 200%
  • Consider truck competition on sub-300 mile lanes (break-even: ~280-320 miles)
  • Negotiate fuel surcharges separately from base rates for transparency
  • Multi-year contracts can lock in rates during low-inflation periods
  • Tampa/New Orleans multi-carrier access may provide leverage for rate negotiations

For Railroads

  • Short-haul cement provides strong margin contribution (R/VC often >200%)
  • Long-haul California routes face margin pressure from alternative sources
  • Service reliability is critical to justify premium pricing versus truck
  • Unit train opportunities exist on high-volume Houston-California lanes

A.2 Methodology Notes

Sample Expansion: All volume and revenue figures use STB expansion factors (exp_carloads, exp_tons, exp_freight_rev) which estimate actual industry totals from the sample.

URCS Approximation: Variable costs are approximated as $0.035/ton-mile plus $5.00/ton terminal costs. This simplified formula produces results consistent with published URCS outputs for bulk commodities over typical distances.

Inflation Adjustment: 2024 projections use CPI-U: 2018=251.1, 2023=304.7, 2024 (est.)=314.5. Inflation factor = 314.5 / ((251.1+304.7)/2) = 1.132.

A.3 Limitations

  • Sample variability: Low-volume lanes may have higher estimation error
  • Confidentiality masking: Some origin-destination pairs are suppressed
  • Contract vs. tariff: Waybill does not distinguish rate types
  • URCS limitations: Calculates average, not marginal, variable cost
