BEA 096 (Fargo, ND) — Cement Origin Investigation

Why does a BEA area with zero cement plants show 27 million tons of rail cement originations over 2005–2023?

Finding: BEA 096 cement originates from Lafarge/Amrize distribution terminals across North Dakota that receive cement by rail from Calgary, Alberta. The waybill codes these re-originated shipments as domestic BEA 096, masking their Canadian import origin. This is not Great Lakes laker traffic.

1. The Anomaly

BEA 096 (Fargo, ND–MN) ranks as the 3rd largest "no-plant" cement origin in the STB waybill, with 15,061 sample records representing ~27 million expanded tons over 19 years. Yet the entire EPA GHGRP database contains zero cement plants (NAICS 327310) in North Dakota or Minnesota.

3. The Explanation: Lafarge Dakota Rail Terminals

Lafarge Dakota Inc. (now Amrize, formerly Holcim/Lafarge) operates a network of rail-served cement distribution terminals across North Dakota. Cement is shipped by rail from Lafarge's plant in Exshaw/Calgary, Alberta via CPKC, stored at these terminals, and re-distributed to regional customers by rail and truck.

Supply Chain Flow

Exshaw/Calgary, AB  ⟶  CPKC rail  ⟶  US border (Portal, ND)  ⟶  Fargo terminal  ⟶  re-originated as BEA 096  ⟶  US destinations

When cement is unloaded at the Fargo terminal and reloaded into cars for onward rail shipment, the new waybill records Fargo as the origin (BEA 096). The Canadian provenance is lost — the import/export flag is either masked or coded as domestic.

4. Ruling Out the Great Lakes Laker Theory

The initial hypothesis was that Canadian cement might arrive at Fargo via Great Lakes vessel (laker) and transload to rail. This was investigated and ruled out:

Against the Laker Theory

  • Geography: Fargo is ~250 miles west of Duluth, the nearest Great Lakes port. No navigable waterway connects them.
  • BEA 098 (Duluth): Zero cement origin records in the entire waybill dataset, despite Duluth having active cement terminals.
  • Via-water flag: 95% of BEA 096 records have the flag masked, but zero show code "1" (Great Lakes). The 689 unmasked records all say "no water."
  • Laker range: St. Marys and Holcim laker fleets serve Wisconsin, Michigan, Illinois, Ohio, New York — none reach the Northern Plains.

Where Lakers DO Operate

  • St. Marys Cement: 4 barges — Charlevoix MI & Bowmanville ON → Green Bay, Milwaukee, Chicago, Manitowoc, Cleveland, Buffalo, Toledo
  • Amrize/Holcim: 2 tug-barges from Alpena MI → Duluth/Superior, Green Bay, Milwaukee, Detroit, Buffalo (~2M tons/yr)
  • CRH/Ash Grove: Duluth terminal (1100 Port Terminal Dr) receives laker cement, connected to BNSF/CN/CPKC/UP — but no evidence of rail redistribution west
  • Key pattern: Laker cement is distributed locally by truck from lakeside terminals, not re-originated onto rail

6. Implications for the Matching Model

  • Reclassify BEA 096: These 15,061 records (27M tons) should be tagged as canadian_reorigination rather than "no plant in BEA." They represent Canadian production distributed through US terminals.
  • True Canadian share: Adding BEA 096 to the explicit Canadian imports (BEA 173–183) raises the Canadian-origin share from 2.4% to 8.7% of total cement tonnage.
  • Other no-plant BEAs: Similar re-origination patterns may explain Houston TX (134), Hartford CT (013), and Boston MA (010), where cement terminals receive bulk shipments and redistribute by rail.
  • Suppression interaction: The 52% suppression rate in cement waybills likely hides additional Canadian-origin volumes, meaning the true import share could be higher still.
