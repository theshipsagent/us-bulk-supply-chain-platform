1. Methodology

The STB Public Use Waybill Sample reports cement shipment origins only as 3-digit BEA Economic Area codes (172 areas covering the continental US). To link waybill records back to specific production facilities, we assigned each of the 100 EPA-reported cement plants (NAICS 327310) to its nearest BEA area centroid using haversine distance, then joined waybill records on originbea = assigned_bea.

Where multiple plants fall within the same BEA area, tonnage and revenue are split equally (proportional allocation). Three categories of records cannot be matched:

2. Match Status Distribution

Of the 430M expanded tons of cement shipped by rail between 2005 and 2023:

  • 11.6% (50M tons) were matched to a specific plant or proportionally allocated among plants within a BEA area
  • 53% were suppressed at the source — the STB masks origins for ~52% of cement waybills, a significantly higher rate than for other bulk commodities
  • 33% originated from BEA areas with no EPA-listed cement plant, indicating these are distribution or terminal origins rather than production facilities
  • 2.4% are Canadian imports, primarily from Ontario and Quebec

4. Tonnage and Revenue Trends

Rail cement volumes declined ~28% during the 2008-2011 housing crisis, recovering to roughly pre-recession levels by 2014. The jump in record count from 2020 to 2021 (4,280 to 32,480 records) reflects a change in STB sampling methodology, not a volume surge — expanded tonnage remains stable at ~25M tons/year.

Revenue per ton has risen steadily from ~$17/ton in 2005 to ~$39/ton in 2023, reflecting both general freight rate escalation and fuel surcharge accumulation. The post-2020 acceleration aligns with broader inflationary trends in construction materials.

6. Distribution Origins Without Plants

Sixteen BEA areas show cement waybill origins but contain no EPA-listed cement plant. The largest — Fresno CA (37M tons), Houston TX (31M tons), and Fargo ND (27M tons) — are major rail distribution hubs where cement is likely re-originated after arriving by unit train from nearby producing regions. These volumes are real rail movements but represent distribution, not production geography.

7. Data Limitations

  • 52% suppression rate: Over half of cement waybill origins are masked, far exceeding the ~25% average for all STB commodities. This means matched tonnage significantly undercounts actual production-to-destination flows.
  • BEA granularity: BEA areas can span hundreds of miles. A plant "assigned" to a BEA area may not be the actual origin — particularly in large western BEAs.
  • Equal allocation: When multiple plants share a BEA (e.g., 5 plants in Allentown PA), each receives 1/N of the tonnage. In reality, plants differ in capacity.
  • No-plant BEA origins: 33% of domestic unsuppressed tonnage originates from BEA areas with no cement plant. These are legitimate rail movements but likely represent terminals, transload points, or re-origination rather than plant shipments.
  • Plant vintage: The EPA plant list uses the most recent GHGRP reporting year for each facility (mostly 2023). Plants that closed between 2005-2023 may be missed.
