BEA 134 (Houston, TX) — Cement Origin Investigation

Why does a BEA area with no cement manufacturing plants originate 31 million tons of rail cement over 2005–2023?

Finding: BEA 134 (Houston) cement rail originations are driven by Port of Houston import terminal operations. Houston is the largest U.S. waterborne cement import gateway, receiving bulk cement and clinker by vessel from Mexico (CEMEX Monterrey, GCC Chihuahua), Turkey, and Greece. Rail shipments are re-originated from port-side grinding/storage facilities into the Texas interior and Central Plains. This is port import redistribution, not domestic production.

1. The Anomaly

BEA 134 (Houston–Galveston, TX) is the 2nd largest no-plant cement origin in the STB waybill, with 4,458 sample records representing 31 million expanded tons over 19 years (2005–2023). Yet the EPA GHGRP database contains zero cement plants (NAICS 327310) within the Houston BEA area.

3. The Explanation: Port of Houston Cement Import Complex

Houston is the largest U.S. cement import port by volume, receiving bulk cement and clinker via ocean vessel. Rail distribution from port-side terminals then re-originates these shipments as domestic BEA 134, masking their foreign origin.

Supply Chain: The Re-origination Mechanism

Mexico / Turkey / Greece  →  bulk cement vessel  →  Houston Ship Channel terminal  →  grind or transfer  →  rail car loaded at port  →  waybill records BEA 134 as origin  →  Texas interior markets

Once cement is unloaded from a vessel and loaded into rail cars at the port terminal, the new waybill records Houston as the origin. The import origin is either coded with import flag 1 (a minority of records) or lost entirely through re-origination, making the full foreign share larger than the waybill import flag alone suggests.

The presence of 51+ car unit trains is diagnostic of a bulk import discharge operation: a vessel arrives, is discharged to hopper cars in unit-train lots, and moves directly to a grinding plant or large distribution depot. Fargo, by contrast, shows almost exclusively 1–5 car redistribution lots — reflecting terminal-to-customer, not vessel-to-plant.

7. Houston vs. Fargo: Two Types of No-Plant Origins

BEA 134 Houston — Port Import Gateway

  • Source: Foreign vessel (Mexico, Turkey, Greece)
  • Mechanism: Ocean vessel → port grind/storage → rail
  • Shipment pattern: Mix of unit trains + small lots
  • Import flag: Partially coded as imports (unlike Fargo)
  • Operators: CEMEX, GCC, Buzzi Unicem, Holcim
  • Rail carriers: UP (primary), BNSF (secondary)
  • Tons: 31M over 19 years (~1.6M/yr)

BEA 096 Fargo — Cross-Border Re-origination

  • Source: Canadian plant (Calgary, AB)
  • Mechanism: Domestic rail (CPKC) → border → ND terminal → re-originate
  • Shipment pattern: Almost exclusively 1–5 car redistribution
  • Import flag: Coded domestic (re-origination masks Canadian origin)
  • Operator: Lafarge / Amrize exclusively
  • Rail carriers: CPKC inbound, BNSF outbound
  • Tons: 27M over 19 years (~1.4M/yr)

Both BEAs are pass-through origins in the waybill — they receive bulk cement from outside the US economy and redistribute it under domestic origin codes. Together they account for 58M tons of cement that the raw waybill data codes as domestic US production.

8. Implications for the Matching Model

  • Reclassify BEA 134 as a port import origin: These 4,458 records should carry a new status tag — port_import — distinct from both no_plant_in_bea and canadian_import. They represent waterborne foreign cement redistributed via a US port.
  • Inferred foreign share: If BEA 134’s 31M tons are foreign-origin, total non-US-production cement in the dataset rises to 0.07B tons (Houston + Fargo + direct imports).
  • Other port candidates: Apply the same logic to Hartford CT (013, ~14M tons, Connecticut River marine terminal), Olympia WA (167), and Boston MA (010) — all are port or water-access BEAs with no EPA cement plant.
  • USACE Waterborne Commerce cross-check: Port of Houston waterborne cement receipts (USACE district data) can be used to validate the waybill tonnage estimate. If USACE shows ~1.6M tons/yr waterborne cement arriving at Houston, this analysis is confirmed.
