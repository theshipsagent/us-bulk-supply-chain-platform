1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

1.1 Overview and Purpose

The Mississippi River system represents the central artery of America's inland waterway transportation network, handling approximately 500 million tons of cargo annually and serving as a critical economic lifeline for the nation's agricultural and industrial sectors. This comprehensive analysis examines the economics of barge transportation on the Mississippi River, with particular emphasis on the Lower Mississippi region below Baton Rouge, where the waterway transitions from an inland navigation system to deep-draft port infrastructure capable of accommodating ocean-going vessels.

1.3 Geographic Significance of the Lower Mississippi

The Mississippi River system drains approximately 40 percent of the interior of the contiguous United States and about 13 percent of Canada, creating a watershed of approximately 1,245,000 square miles [📚 S97FGYWY:chunk_12]. The river stretches 2,340 miles in length and, together with its tributaries, provides over 15,000 miles of inland navigation [📚 S97FGYWY:chunk_12].

The Lower Mississippi, beginning at Cairo, Illinois, where the Ohio River joins the Mississippi, is particularly significant. New Orleans, established in 1718 approximately 90 miles upriver from the Gulf of Mexico, serves as the strategic point from which both the Mississippi River and Lake Pontchartrain can be accessed [📚 S97FGYWY:chunk_10]. The river empties into the Gulf of Mexico at an average rate of 785,000 million cubic yards annually, delivering three times as much water as the Rhine and 338 times as much as the Thames [📚 S97FGYWY:chunk_12].

1.5 Economic Elasticities and Shipper Behavior

One of the most critical findings in understanding barge transportation economics relates to how shippers respond to changes in transportation rates and transit times. The Mid-American Grain Study provides quantitative estimates of these behavioral responses, which are essential for forecasting demand and evaluating infrastructure investments.

Freight Rate Elasticity of Demand:

The elasticity of mode and origin/destination choice with respect to transportation rates varies significantly based on the magnitude of rate changes:

- 10% rate increase: Arc elasticity of 1.4 - 100% rate increase: Arc elasticity of 0.6

[📚 ZG54IV2J:chunk_11, chunk_14]

This declining elasticity pattern reflects that small rate increases induce readily mobile shippers to switch modes, while larger increases affect only more captive shippers. Importantly, 38 percent of shippers would not switch to another mode or origin/destination even if rates doubled [📚 ZG54IV2J:chunk_11].

Transit Time Elasticity:

Shippers also respond to changes in transit times, though less dramatically than to rate changes:

- 10% time increase: Arc elasticity of 0.8 - 100% time increase: Arc elasticity of 0.4

[📚 ZG54IV2J:chunk_16]

A doubling of transportation times would cause 55 percent of shippers to remain with their current mode and origin/destination, indicating substantial time insensitivity [📚 ZG54IV2J:chunk_11].

Annual Volume Elasticity:

The responsiveness of annual shipment volumes to rate and time changes is notably lower than mode choice elasticity:

- Arc elasticity for annual volume: 0.23 to 0.41 depending on magnitude of changes - Arc elasticity for 10% rate increase: 0.3

[📚 ZG54IV2J:chunk_11, chunk_16]

This suggests that while shippers may switch modes in response to cost changes, they are less likely to significantly alter their total shipping volumes.

Shipper Alternatives and Market Structure:

- 26% of surveyed shippers reported they would have to shut down if their current mode and origin/destination were not viable [📚 ZG54IV2J:chunk_13] - 14% of shippers would switch to their next-best alternative with a 10% rate increase [📚 ZG54IV2J:chunk_13] - Over 60% of shippers would not relocate existing facilities regardless of transportation cost changes [📚 ZG54IV2J:chunk_17] - However, 76% would choose locations with lower transportation costs when building new facilities [📚 ZG54IV2J:chunk_18]

These findings indicate significant market segmentation, with some shippers having substantial alternatives while others are essentially captive to their current transportation modes.

2. LOWER MISSISSIPPI GEOGRAPHIC CONTEXT

2.1 Definition and Boundaries

The Lower Mississippi River begins at Cairo, Illinois, where the Ohio River joins the Mississippi [📚 S97FGYWY:chunk_13]. This confluence marks a fundamental transition in the river's character. While the Ohio River delivers relatively clean flow, the muddy Missouri River dumps an excessively silt-laden load into the upper Mississippi, giving the river its characteristic turbidity within a mile or two below Cairo [📚 S97FGYWY:chunk_13].

From Cairo to the Gulf of Mexico, the Lower Mississippi traverses approximately 950 river miles, passing through some of the most productive agricultural and industrial regions of the United States. The region below Baton Rouge, Louisiana—approximately 230 river miles from the Gulf—represents a particularly critical segment where the waterway transitions from inland navigation to deep-draft port infrastructure.

2.2 Historical Development and Strategic Importance

The strategic location of New Orleans, established in 1718, exemplifies the geographic logic of Lower Mississippi development. The site, though "miserable" in terms of environmental conditions with snakes, mud, mosquitoes, alligators, and oppressive heat and humidity, was chosen as the only strategically located point from which both the Mississippi River and Lake Pontchartrain could be protected from foreign intrusion [📚 S97FGYWY:chunk_9-10].

Early Commerce (1700s): - First recorded cargo: 15,000 bear and deer hides rafted downstream in 1705 [📚 S97FGYWY:chunk_11] - By 1729, the Natchez Indians were supplying a significant portion of the 300,000-pound tobacco crop shipped from New Orleans [📚 S97FGYWY:chunk_15] - American deposits at New Orleans included expensive cargoes representing the sole cash crops of interior settlers [📚 S97FGYWY:chunk_23]

The strategic importance of New Orleans and the Lower Mississippi grew as American settlers pushed westward. The warehouse facilities at New Orleans bulged with tobacco, cider, and furs, with quantities growing each year, making the port "too vital to the Americans to be left in foreign hands" [📚 S97FGYWY:chunk_23].

2.3 Physical Characteristics of the Lower Mississippi

Watershed and Hydrology: - Drains approximately 40% of the contiguous United States interior - Drains approximately 13% of Canada - Total watershed area: 1,245,000 square miles - Average discharge rate: 785,000 million cubic yards per year - Water volume: 3x the Rhine River, 338x the Thames River - Fresh water supply: Enough to provide the entire U.S. with three days of water supply flows past New Orleans daily

[📚 S97FGYWY:chunk_12]

Sediment Load: The Mississippi carries approximately 50 pounds of mud (silt) per 1,000 cubic feet of water [📚 S97FGYWY:chunk_13]. While legendary as "too thick to drink and too thin to plow," this silt burden is actually modest compared to other major rivers—both the Missouri and Colorado Rivers carry approximately ten times more silt than the Mississippi [📚 S97FGYWY:chunk_14].

This sediment load creates ongoing challenges for navigation: - Requires continuous dredging to maintain channel depths - Causes shifting channels and sandbar formation - Deposits create land formations throughout the delta - Complicates port infrastructure maintenance

2.4 Deep-Draft Port Infrastructure Below Baton Rouge

The segment of the Lower Mississippi below Baton Rouge represents a unique transition zone where inland waterway navigation infrastructure meets deep-draft ocean port facilities. This convergence creates a multimodal transportation hub of exceptional strategic value.

Port Characteristics:

Deep-draft ports in this region can accommodate ocean-going vessels, creating direct international trade connections. The USACE port definitions, used for statistical consistency, recognize these ports based on legislative boundaries that align with Federal, State, and city legislative definitions [📚 BP6RKD3F:chunk_23].

Channel Depths:

The waterways below Baton Rouge maintain significantly greater depths than upper river segments: - Deep-draft channels: 40+ feet depth - Capable of accommodating large ocean-going bulk carriers - Can handle modern container ships (depending on specific port facilities) - Require continuous dredging to maintain authorized project depths

[📚 BP6RKD3F:chunk_9]

Port Governance Structures:

Port authorities in the Lower Mississippi region operate under various governance models [📚 BP6RKD3F:chunk_25-27]:

1. Landlord Ports: Build and maintain infrastructure but don't operate terminals 2. Operating Ports: Directly operate some or all terminals (example: Port of Houston Authority) 3. Jurisdictional Bodies: Oversee private terminals responsible for their own infrastructure

Some ports maintain statewide authority (Louisiana, South Carolina, Georgia) coordinating multiple facilities within State boundaries [📚 BP6RKD3F:chunk_28].

3. INFRASTRUCTURE & OPERATIONS

3.1 Overview of Navigation Infrastructure

The Mississippi River navigation system represents one of the world's most extensive and complex inland waterway networks. The system encompasses over 15,000 miles of navigable channels when including the Mississippi and its major tributaries [📚 S97FGYWY:chunk_12]. This infrastructure enables the movement of more than 500 million tons of cargo annually, providing essential transportation services for agriculture, energy, chemicals, and manufactured goods.

3.2 Lock and Dam System Configuration

Lock structures constitute the defining infrastructure elements for most of the Mississippi River system, though the Lower Mississippi below Baton Rouge operates as an open river without locks. Locks raise or lower vessels between adjacent navigation pools created by dams across the natural river channel.

Lock Function and Importance:

Lock structures "constitute the major bottlenecks in the U.S. waterway network and generate extensive queues" [📚 WUDNR4MQ:chunk_15]. This bottleneck characteristic makes lock capacity and performance critical determinants of overall system efficiency.

Lock Configuration Variations:

1. Single chamber locks: One chamber serves traffic in both directions 2. Parallel chamber locks: Two or more chambers allow simultaneous lockages 3. Unequal chambers: Parallel chambers with different dimensions or characteristics

Some locks have only one chamber, while others may have two parallel chambers whose characteristics may differ [📚 WUDNR4MQ:chunk_15].

Typical Lock Dimensions:

Historic locks on the Upper Mississippi and Illinois Rivers: - Chamber length: 600 feet (original design) - Chamber width: 110 feet - Lift: Varies by location (typically 10-40 feet)

These dimensions were established in the 1930s when typical tow sizes were significantly smaller than modern configurations. Today's standard tow configuration consists of 15 barges (3 wide × 5 long) pushed by a single towboat, creating an integrated unit approximately 1,050 feet long and 105 feet wide. This configuration requires two lockages through a 600-foot chamber, significantly increasing transit times and creating congestion.

Newer Lock Designs:

More recent lock construction incorporates larger chambers: - 1,200-foot chambers: Accommodate full 15-barge tows in single lockage - Reduced lock transit time: Approximately 50% reduction versus double lockage - Higher capacity: Can process more tows per day

3.3 Channel Characteristics and Maintenance

Navigation channels require specific depth and width dimensions to safely accommodate commercial tow traffic and ocean-going vessels.

Channel Depth Categories:

The waterway system includes eight depth categories ranging from under 8 feet to over 40 feet for commercially navigable waterways [📚 FCWL5PVF:chunk_17].

Lower Mississippi Channel Specifications:

The Lower Mississippi below Baton Rouge maintains deep-draft channel dimensions: - Authorized depth: 45 feet (some segments) - Actual (controlling) depth: May vary due to sedimentation - Width: Varies by segment, typically 500-750 feet

Channel Maintenance Requirements:

The Mississippi carries substantial sediment loads requiring continuous maintenance: - Silt burden: Approximately 50 pounds per 1,000 cubic feet of water [📚 S97FGYWY:chunk_13] - Dredging frequency: Ongoing operations to maintain project depths - Dredge disposal: Requires environmentally appropriate placement sites - Cost: Tens of millions annually for major channel segments

Controlling depths may be less than authorized project depths due to shoaling between dredging cycles [📚 FCWL5PVF:chunk_17]. This creates variability in accessible draft for vessels, particularly during low-water periods.

3.4 Lock Performance and Delay Characteristics

Lock congestion and delay represent critical operational issues affecting system performance and shipper costs.

Queuing System Characteristics:

Lock operations function as queuing systems where arriving tows wait for lockage service. The delay estimation model characterizes this as a "stochastic, microscopic, and event-scanning model" [📚 WUDNR4MQ:chunk_3].

Key Performance Variables:

1. Interarrival times: Time between successive tow arrivals 2. Service times: Duration required for lockage operation 3. Queue length: Number of tows waiting 4. Waiting time: Duration in queue before lockage begins

Service Time Components:

Total lockage time includes: - Chamber filling/emptying time - Gate operation time - Tow entry/exit time - Safety buffer periods

Volume-to-Capacity (V/C) Ratios:

Lock performance degrades non-linearly as traffic approaches capacity. The simulation model has been validated over V/C ratios from 0.04 to 0.89, showing average absolute error of only 0.54% compared to theoretical M/G/1 queuing results [📚 WUDNR4MQ:chunk_5].

At high V/C ratios (>0.8), delays increase exponentially, creating severe congestion that dramatically increases shipper costs.

Validation Against Observed Data:

The simulation model was validated against observed data at five lock sites on the Mississippi River, comparing: - Average waiting times - Chamber volumes (lockages per period) - Cut volumes (vessel passages)

Results showed the simulation model "represents the real system quite well" [📚 WUDNR4MQ:chunk_5].

3.5 Lock Interdependence and System Effects

Locks do not operate independently—performance at one lock affects traffic patterns and delays at downstream and upstream facilities.

Arrival Process Interdependence:

The variance of interarrival times at a lock depends on: - Variance of interdeparture times from adjacent upstream lock - Speed variations during travel between locks - Additional traffic entering or leaving the system between locks

[📚 WUDNR4MQ:chunk_10]

Departure Process Characteristics:

The departure process from a lock exhibits different variance characteristics than the arrival process. The departure metamodel establishes relationships among: - Coefficient of variation of interdeparture times - Coefficient of variation of interarrival times - Coefficient of variation of service times - V/C ratio

[📚 WUDNR4MQ:chunk_8]

Burke's Theorem Application:

Under certain conditions (M/M/1 queues operating below saturation), the departure process has the same statistical properties as the arrival process. The metamodel developed is "consistent with Burke's Theorem" [📚 WUDNR4MQ:chunk_9], providing theoretical validation.

System-Wide Delay Propagation:

Congestion at a single lock can propagate through the system: - Upstream delays create irregular arrival patterns at downstream locks - Downstream delays cause tows to bunch up, overwhelming upstream lock capacity - System-wide disruptions can result from localized bottlenecks

3.6 Numerical Methods for Network Analysis

Given the computational challenges of simulating complex lock networks, numerical approximation methods have been developed for rapid analysis of large combinatorial investment problems.

Two-Way Algorithm:

For systems with bi-directional traffic, an iterative alternating direction scanning procedure: 1. Scans from first to last lock 2. Then scans from last to first lock 3. Iterates until interdeparture time variances converge

[📚 WUDNR4MQ:chunk_7]

One-Way Algorithm:

For systems with primarily one-directional flow: - Single scan from first to last lock - No iteration required - Theoretically less subject to interdependence errors

[📚 WUDNR4MQ:chunk_7]

Metamodel Components:

The numerical methods rely on three key metamodels:

1. Delay Metamodel: Relates average waiting time to V/C ratio, interarrival time distribution, interdeparture time distribution, and service time distribution [📚 WUDNR4MQ:chunk_8]

2. Departures Metamodel: Estimates variance of interdeparture times based on arrival and service characteristics (standard error of 0.0058 with mean of 0.8311) [📚 WUDNR4MQ:chunk_9]

3. Arrivals Metamodel: Relates interarrival time variance to interdeparture time variance from adjacent locks, accounting for speed variations [📚 WUDNR4MQ:chunk_10]

These metamodels enable rapid screening of investment alternatives, with detailed simulation reserved for the most promising options [📚 WUDNR4MQ:chunk_14].

3.7 Capacity Constraints and Bottlenecks

System capacity is constrained by multiple factors operating at different scales:

Lock-Specific Constraints:

- Chamber dimensions: Small chambers require multiple lockages for large tows - Mechanical reliability: Equipment failures reduce effective capacity - Service times: Fixed components (filling/emptying) cannot be compressed - Safety requirements: Minimum separation and operational procedures

Channel Constraints:

- Controlling depths: Shallow segments limit draft and cargo loading - Width restrictions: Narrow channels restrict meeting and passing - Bridge clearances: Vertical restrictions may limit barge stacking - Sharp bends: Reduce safe navigation speeds and restrict tow sizes

System-Level Constraints:

- Sequential bottlenecks: Capacity is limited by weakest link - Lock interdependence: Congestion propagates through system - Seasonal variations: Low water reduces channel capacity - Ice conditions: Northern reaches experience winter closures

Identification of Critical Bottlenecks:

The numerical method enables evaluation of system-wide impacts: - Can assess benefits of expanding capacity at specific locks - Identifies which improvements yield greatest system benefits - Allows comparison of alternative investment sequences

[📚 WUDNR4MQ:chunk_14]

3.8 Operational Practices and Service Disciplines

First-In-First-Out (FIFO) Service:

The delay estimation model assumes FIFO service discipline [📚 WUDNR4MQ:chunk_4]. This reflects standard practice where tows are lockaged in order of arrival, regardless of cargo type or destination.

Priority Systems:

Some facilities may provide priority service for: - Emergency situations - Passenger vessels - Military craft - Hazardous cargo (in some cases)

However, routine commercial traffic generally operates under FIFO discipline.

Parallel Chamber Assignment:

For locks with parallel chambers of different sizes or characteristics, the chamber assignment process affects effective capacity. The current numerical method does not fully handle dissimilar parallel chambers, as "the chamber assignment process affects lock capacity" in complex ways [📚 WUDNR4MQ:chunk_12].

3.9 Tow Configuration and Fleet Characteristics

Standard Tow Configurations:

Midwestern river system: - 15-barge tow: 3 wide × 5 long (most common) - 9-barge tow: 3 wide × 3 long - 6-barge tow: 2 wide × 3 long

Lower Mississippi (open river): - 40-50 barge tows: Possible in unrestricted segments - Flexible configurations: Adjusted for cargo and conditions

Barge Specifications:

Standard jumbo hopper barge: - Length: 195 feet - Width: 35 feet - Draft (loaded): 9 feet - Capacity: ~1,500 tons

Towboat Characteristics:

- Power: 2,000 to 10,000+ horsepower - Crew: Typically 4-12 persons - Speed (loaded): 5-8 mph downstream, 3-5 mph upstream

Travel Times:

From the grain study data, average barge speeds of 4.26 miles per hour reflect loaded conditions with lock delays [📚 ZG54IV2J:chunk_28]. Actual free-running speeds in open channel can be higher.

3.10 Maintenance and Reliability

Scheduled Maintenance:

Lock facilities require periodic maintenance: - Annual closures: Typically 1-2 weeks for inspection and repair - Multi-year overhauls: Major component replacement/rehabilitation - Emergency repairs: Unplanned outages for failure response

Reliability Metrics:

- Availability: Percentage of time lock is operational - Mean time between failures (MTBF): Average operating duration before breakdown - Mean time to repair (MTTR): Average duration to restore service

The numerical method could potentially incorporate stall effects explicitly by "treating stalls as a second class of users with its own arrival and service time distributions" [📚 WUDNR4MQ:chunk_11].

Aging Infrastructure Challenges:

Many locks constructed in the 1930s face: - Deteriorating concrete and steel structures - Obsolete mechanical/electrical systems - Parts availability issues for antiquated equipment - Increasing frequency and duration of failures

3.11 Environmental and Seasonal Factors

Low Water Conditions:

During drought periods: - Draft restrictions reduce barge loading - Reduced cargo per tow increases trips required for same tonnage - Some shallow-draft tows can continue operating - Extreme events may force temporary closures

High Water/Flood Conditions:

- May increase current speeds, affecting transit times - Can inundate shore facilities - May trigger spillway operations affecting channel flows - Debris in water creates navigation hazards

Ice Conditions:

Northern segments experience: - Winter closures (typically December-March on upper reaches) - Ice formation in locks complicates operations - Reduced season for year-round traffic flow

Hurricane and Storm Events:

Gulf Coast exposure creates periodic disruptions: - Port closures during storm events - Vessel evacuations ahead of hurricanes - Post-storm recovery periods - Infrastructure damage requiring repairs

3.12 Safety and Security

Navigation Safety:

- Traffic control: Coast Guard regulates vessel operations - Aids to navigation: Buoys, lights, markers maintain channels - Vessel Traffic Services (VTS): Monitoring in congested areas - Weather monitoring: Real-time conditions for safety

Security Measures:

Post-9/11 security enhancements: - Maritime Transportation Security Act (MTSA): Port security requirements - Facility security plans: Required for port facilities - Vessel security: Inspection and monitoring protocols - HAZMAT controls: Special procedures for dangerous cargoes

3.13 Technological Enhancements

Automated Identification Systems (AIS):

Modern vessels transmit location and status data: - Enables traffic monitoring - Facilitates delay measurement - Supports safety and security - Provides data for performance analysis

The Port Performance Freight Statistics Program uses AIS data to measure container vessel dwell times, representing "the first application by BTS of automated data collection to port performance measurement" [📚 BP6RKD3F:chunk_18].

Lock Automation:

- Remote operation: Some locks operated from central locations - Automated gate controls: Computerized systems replacing manual operation - Condition monitoring: Sensors detect equipment problems early - Data collection: Automated performance tracking

Navigation Technology:

- Electronic charts: Replace paper charts - GPS positioning: Precise navigation - Radar and sonar: Obstacle detection - Integrated bridge systems: Comprehensive vessel control

3.14 Modal Interface Infrastructure

Barge-to-Ship Transfers:

Lower Mississippi ports provide infrastructure for transferring cargo from barges to ocean vessels: - Fleeting areas: Staging areas for barge accumulation - Transfer facilities: Grain elevators, bulk loaders - Temporary storage: Warehouses and tank farms buffer supply/demand variations

Rail-Barge Interfaces:

- Transloading facilities: Transfer between railcars and barges - Unit train capacity: Receiving multiple railcars simultaneously - Grain elevators: Combined rail/barge loading capability

Truck-Barge Interfaces:

- Loading docks: Direct truck-to-barge transfer - Container yards: Intermodal container handling - Roll-on/roll-off ramps: Vehicle loading facilities

3.15 Future Infrastructure Needs

Capacity Expansion Requirements:

To accommodate projected traffic growth: - New lock construction: Additional parallel chambers at bottlenecks - Lock chamber extensions: Lengthen existing chambers to 1,200 feet - Channel deepening: Increase draft capacity where economically justified

Modernization Priorities:

- Lock rehabilitation: Replace aging components before failure - Automation enhancement: Reduce operating costs and improve reliability - Environmental upgrades: Meet contemporary environmental standards - Energy efficiency: Reduce operational energy consumption

Climate Adaptation:

- Hydrological variability: Design for wider range of flow conditions - Sea level rise: Adjust channel and facility elevations - Storm resilience: Strengthen infrastructure against extreme events

Technology Integration:

- Real-time optimization: Dynamic traffic management systems - Predictive maintenance: AI/ML for failure prediction - Automated operations: Reduce labor requirements and human error - Integrated data systems: System-wide visibility and control

The infrastructure and operational analysis reveals a complex system with significant capacity constraints, particularly at lock facilities. The quantitative delay estimation models demonstrate that strategic investments in bottleneck relief can yield substantial economic benefits through reduced congestion and improved service reliability.

The Mississippi River system functions as the central conduit for bulk commodity movement in the central United States, with cargo flows exhibiting distinct directional patterns driven by agricultural exports, energy distribution, and industrial raw material requirements.

4.3 Economic Fundamentals of Barge Transportation

#### 4.3.1 Cost Structure

Barge transportation exhibits a cost structure heavily weighted toward fixed and indirect costs:

Capital Costs: - Barge fleet acquisition and financing - Towboat construction and equipment - Specialized terminal facilities

Operating Costs: - Crew labor - Fuel (diesel for towboats) - Maintenance and repairs - Insurance - Lock fees and other waterway charges

Cost per Ton-Mile:

Data from the Mid-American Grain Study provides specific cost comparisons [📚 ZG54IV2J:chunk_28]:

| Mode | Rate per Ton-Mile (cents) | Relative to Barge | |------|---------------------------|-------------------| | Barge | 1.19 | 1.0x (baseline) | | Rail | 3.16 | 2.7x | | Truck | 12.90 | 10.8x | | Multi-mode | 18.48 | 15.5x |

These dramatic cost differentials explain barge dominance for long-distance bulk commodity movements where transit time is not critical.

Distance Sensitivity:

Average shipment distances vary significantly by mode: - Barge: 863 miles - Rail: 775 miles - Truck: 123 miles - Multi-mode: 644 miles

[📚 ZG54IV2J:chunk_28]

Barge shows the longest average distance, reflecting its cost advantage for long hauls. Truck shows the shortest distance, consistent with its high cost per mile but speed and flexibility advantages for short hauls.

#### 4.3.2 Service Characteristics

Speed and Transit Time:

Transportation speed (miles per hour) varies dramatically: - Truck: 34.78 mph - Multi-mode: 28.92 mph - Rail: 8.64 mph - Barge: 4.26 mph

[📚 ZG54IV2J:chunk_28]

Barges are the slowest mode, taking days or weeks for trips that trucks complete in hours. This speed disadvantage is offset by cost advantages for commodities with low time sensitivity.

Shipment Size:

Median shipment sizes differ substantially: - Rail: 2,752 tons - Barge: 1,740 tons - Multi-mode: 27.3 tons - Truck: 25.2 tons

[📚 ZG54IV2J:chunk_28]

Barge and rail handle bulk commodity movements with large per-shipment volumes, while truck handles smaller, more frequent shipments.

Reliability:

Barge reliability is affected by: - Lock congestion and delays - Weather conditions (ice, high/low water) - Mechanical failures - Seasonal closures on northern reaches

These reliability challenges contrast with the more predictable service of truck and, to some extent, rail.

#### 4.3.3 Total Logistics Costs

Shippers evaluate transportation based on total logistics costs, not just line-haul rates:

Cost Components: - Line-haul transportation: Direct movement costs - Inventory carrying costs: Value of goods in transit - Terminal handling: Loading/unloading operations - Packaging: Mode-appropriate container/protection - Insurance: Loss and damage coverage - Administrative: Documentation and management

Time Value of Money:

Slower barge transit increases inventory carrying costs. For a $200/ton commodity at 10% annual holding cost: - 1-day delay: $0.055/ton - 1-week delay: $0.38/ton - 1-month delay: $1.64/ton

For grain (approximately $150-250/ton), these time costs are modest. For higher-value manufactured goods, time costs can exceed barge cost advantages, making faster modes preferable despite higher rates.

4.4 Demand Elasticities and Shipper Response Functions

The Mid-American Grain Study provides the most comprehensive quantitative evidence on how shippers respond to changes in transportation rates and times.

#### 4.4.1 Mode and Origin/Destination Choice Elasticity

Rate Elasticity:

Arc elasticity varies with magnitude of rate change: - 10% rate increase: 1.4 elasticity - 100% rate increase: 0.6 elasticity

[📚 ZG54IV2J:chunk_11, chunk_14]

This declining elasticity reflects shipper heterogeneity. Small rate increases induce readily mobile shippers to switch modes or O/D, while larger increases affect only more captive shippers with fewer alternatives.

Switching Behavior:

- 10% rate increase: 14% of shippers switch to next-best alternative [📚 ZG54IV2J:chunk_13] - 100% rate increase: 62% of shippers switch to next-best alternative [📚 ZG54IV2J:chunk_14] - 100% rate increase: 38% of shippers would NOT switch [📚 ZG54IV2J:chunk_11]

Time Elasticity:

Arc elasticity for transit time changes: - 10% time increase: 0.8 elasticity - 100% time increase: 0.4 elasticity

[📚 ZG54IV2J:chunk_16]

Time versus Rate Sensitivity:

"While transit time matters, it matters less than rates. Stated precisely, shippers respond less to a percent increase in transit time than they do to the same percent increase in transit rates" [📚 ZG54IV2J:chunk_16].

Time Switching Behavior:

A doubling of transportation times would cause: - 55% to maintain current mode and O/D [📚 ZG54IV2J:chunk_11] - 45% to switch to alternatives

This indicates substantial time insensitivity compared to rate sensitivity, though time remains an independent factor in mode choice [📚 ZG54IV2J:chunk_10].

#### 4.4.2 Annual Volume Elasticity

Shippers' annual shipment volumes also respond to rate and time changes, though less dramatically than mode choice.

Volume Response:

Arc elasticities for annual volume component: - Range: 0.23 to 0.41 depending on magnitude - 10% rate increase: 0.3 elasticity [📚 ZG54IV2J:chunk_16]

"Shippers change their volumes considerably less than they change their modes and O/Ds" [📚 ZG54IV2J:chunk_17].

This finding indicates that while transportation costs affect shipment routing, they have more modest effects on total production and marketing volumes.

#### 4.4.3 Facility Location Elasticity

Location decisions exhibit dramatically different elasticities in short versus long run.

Existing Facilities:

Over 60% of surveyed shippers would not relocate existing facilities regardless of transportation cost changes [📚 ZG54IV2J:chunk_17].

This reflects the high fixed costs of facility relocation, including: - Real estate acquisition and construction - Equipment installation - Workforce relocation/recruitment - Lost production during transition - Customer relationship disruption

New Facilities:

When choosing locations for new facilities, 76% of shippers would select locations with lower transportation costs but higher investment costs (within the ranges considered) [📚 ZG54IV2J:chunk_18].

Short versus Long Run:

"Facility location is fairly insensitive to transportation costs and times in the short run, but in the long run, when new facilities are built, the location choice is highly sensitive to transportation costs" [📚 ZG54IV2J:chunk_18].

This implies waterway infrastructure improvements can have long-term regional development impacts as new facilities locate to exploit transportation advantages, even though existing facilities show limited mobility.

#### 4.4.4 Captive Shippers and Alternatives

No Alternative Shippers:

26% of surveyed shippers reported they would have to shut down if their current mode and O/D were not viable [📚 ZG54IV2J:chunk_13].

These "captive" shippers have no economically viable alternatives and would cease operations rather than switch modes or locations.

Alternative Structures:

Among shippers with alternatives: - 57.7%: Would use different mode at same origin/destination - 15.6%: Would use different origin or destination locations - 26.8%: Would shut down (no alternatives)

[📚 ZG54IV2J:chunk_30]

This distribution shows that mode switching is far more common than location switching when shippers face transportation cost changes.

4.5 Value of Time and Service Reliability

#### 4.5.1 Transit Time Value

The independent significance of transit time (separate from its impact on rates) reflects several factors:

Inventory Carrying Costs: - Interest on capital tied up in goods in transit - Warehouse and storage expenses - Obsolescence and deterioration risks - Insurance costs

Market Responsiveness: - Ability to respond to price signals - Seasonal market windows (particularly for agriculture) - Competitive delivery commitments

Working Capital: - Cash flow implications of slow payment cycles - Credit and financing costs

#### 4.5.2 Reliability Value

Service reliability has value beyond average transit time:

Schedule Certainty: - Predictable arrival enables efficient inventory management - Reduces safety stock requirements - Facilitates just-in-time manufacturing processes

Congestion Costs:

Lock delays create both direct and indirect costs: - Direct: Barge and crew time costs during waiting - Indirect: Inventory carrying costs, missed delivery windows, market opportunity losses

The delay estimation models show that waiting times increase non-linearly with V/C ratios [📚 WUDNR4MQ:chunk_5], meaning congestion costs escalate rapidly as facilities approach capacity.

4.6 Market Structure and Competition

#### 4.6.1 Barge Industry Structure

The barge transportation industry is characterized by:

Carrier Types: - Common carriers: Provide service to general public on published tariffs - Contract carriers: Operate under specific contracts with shippers - Private carriers: Shippers operating their own barge fleets

Market Concentration: - Multiple large carriers (American Commercial Barge Lines, Ingram Barge Company, Kirby Corporation, others) - Numerous small and medium operators - Competitive markets for most commodity movements

Entry Barriers: - Capital requirements for fleet acquisition - Terminal infrastructure access - Expertise and reputation - Regulatory compliance

#### 4.6.2 Rate Determination

Barge freight rates are determined by market forces:

Supply Factors: - Barge and towboat availability - Fuel costs - Labor costs - Lock congestion and delays

Demand Factors: - Commodity production volumes (harvest sizes, industrial output) - Export demand - Alternative mode availability and pricing - Seasonal patterns

Competitive Dynamics: - Rail rates provide competitive ceiling for many movements - Truck rates generally too high for effective barge competition - Pipeline rates compete for petroleum movements

The Mississippi River system supports: - Crude oil movements to refineries - Petroleum product distribution from Gulf Coast refineries - Regional fuel supply chains

Chemical Industry:

4.8 Infrastructure Investment Economics

#### 4.8.1 Benefit-Cost Analysis Framework

Lock expansion and rehabilitation projects can be evaluated using benefit-cost analysis:

Benefits: - Delay reduction (time savings valued at shipper cost of waiting) - Reliability improvements (variance reduction in transit times) - Capacity expansion (accommodation of traffic growth) - Avoided modal diversion (maintaining traffic on lower-cost mode)

Costs: - Construction capital expenditures - Project design and engineering - Environmental mitigation - Lost navigation capacity during construction - Ongoing operations and maintenance

Discount Rate:

Federal water resources projects typically use discount rates in the 3-7% range, with current OMB guidance for 2024-2026 specifying rates based on Treasury securities of comparable maturity.
