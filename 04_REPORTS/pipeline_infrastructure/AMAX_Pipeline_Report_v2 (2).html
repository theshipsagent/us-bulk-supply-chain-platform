01 — Executive Overview

The Gulf Coast's Most Active Energy Infrastructure Corridor

The AMAX Port Nickel Terminal occupies 387 acres on the East Bank of the Mississippi River at Braithwaite, Plaquemines Parish, Louisiana (River Mile ~80 AHP). The site features deep-draft river access, Norfolk Southern Class I rail service, and substantial upland acreage suitable for industrial redevelopment.

Key Finding

Southeast Louisiana is experiencing the largest build-out of natural gas infrastructure in a generation, driven by LNG export growth (+12.3 Bcf/d under construction), data center power demand (Meta $10B investment), and industrial expansion. Three pipeline systems pass within 5–25 miles of the AMAX site. Plaquemines LNG — a Venture Global facility in the same parish — is now pulling 3+ Bcf/d of feedgas and has announced a 30+ MTPA brownfield expansion (FERC CP26-27, filed November 2025) that would make it the largest LNG export complex in North America at 58+ MTPA total peak capacity. This creates a direct load-balancing opportunity for gas storage development at the AMAX site.

02 — Site-Proximate Infrastructure

Pipeline Infrastructure Near the AMAX Site

Three pipeline systems have been identified within approximately 25 miles of the AMAX property. These systems demonstrate existing right-of-way infrastructure in the immediate vicinity and confirm the technical feasibility of pipeline interconnection from the AMAX site.

Pipelines Within 5 Miles

Harvest CAM Crude Oil System<5 mi
Operator: Harvest MidstreamProduct: Crude OilStatus: Operational

Nearest confirmed major pipeline to the AMAX site. Collects offshore and nearshore crude oil production and delivers to New Orleans-area refineries including PBF Chalmette (190,000 BPD, East Bank, ~15–20 mi). Connects to Belle Chasse Terminal (3,200 acres, 1 million barrels storage, two crude oil loading docks) — the former Phillips 66 Alliance Refinery, converted to terminal operations in 2022 after Hurricane Ida damage, acquired by Harvest Midstream in 2023. While direct crude integration is limited for the AMAX site, Harvest CAM's proximity demonstrates existing pipeline right-of-way infrastructure in the immediate corridor and confirms midstream operational presence.

Pipelines Within 10–25 Miles

Gulf South Pipeline10–25 mi (est.)
Operator: Boardwalk Pipeline PartnersProduct: Natural GasCapacity: ~3 Bcf/d

Extensive Louisiana intrastate natural gas network — the most likely interconnection option for the AMAX site. As a Louisiana-focused intrastate system, Gulf South offers operational flexibility not available with interstate systems subject to FERC tariffs. Recent Kosci Junction reconfigurations have optimized flow patterns for evolving Louisiana gas market dynamics. Exact route through Plaquemines Parish requires GIS verification with Louisiana DNR data, but the system's dense Southeast Louisiana coverage makes it the primary interconnection candidate.

Cayenne NGL Pipeline10–20 mi (est.)
Operator: AMID / Targa JVProduct: Y-grade NGLsCapacity: 40,000–50,000 BPD

Transports unfractionated natural gas liquids from the Venice Gas Processing Plant in lower Plaquemines Parish (~75 miles south) to the Toca Fractionation Facility in Ascension Parish (~50 miles northwest). The pipeline crosses the Mississippi River via horizontal directional drill (HDD) estimated within 10–20 miles of the AMAX site. This river crossing demonstrates technical feasibility for pipeline construction in the corridor and confirms NGL infrastructure presence in the immediate regional vicinity.

Pipelines Within 25–75 Miles

Gator Express Pipeline50–75 mi south
Operator: Venture GlobalProduct: Natural GasStatus: Operational

Two 42-inch diameter pipeline laterals, approximately 26.8 miles combined, delivering natural gas to Plaquemines LNG from interconnections with Tennessee Gas Pipeline (TGP) and Texas Eastern Transmission (TETCO) located offshore southwest of the terminal. Segment 1: ~15-mile Southwest Lateral TGP. Segment 2: ~12-mile Southwest Lateral TETCO (looped section). Each segment designed to deliver approximately 1.9 Bcf/d (~3.8 Bcf/d combined design capacity). FERC dockets CP17-66/CP17-67, authorized September 2019. Entirely within Plaquemines Parish. Pipeline routing from Haynesville to Plaquemines LNG potentially traverses a corridor near the AMAX site, creating additional interconnection possibility. Engineered by Gulf Companies; includes HDD in shallow water, DirectPipe crossing at LNG terminal entrance, pipe bridge over levee, SCADA, and cathodic protection.

Transco (Williams)20–50 mi
Operator: Williams CompaniesProduct: Natural GasCapacity: ~15 Bcf/d

Largest U.S. natural gas transmission system. Runs parallel to Mississippi River through East Bank parishes. Serves Cameron LNG, proposed CP2, and Gillis hub convergence point. Zone 3 (Gulf Coast) provides direct access to Southeast Louisiana market. The system connects to numerous LNG feed pipelines including LEG, which began service July 2025.

Colonial Pipeline (Lines 1 & 2)15–30 mi
Operator: Colonial Pipeline Co.Product: Refined ProductsCapacity: ~2.5M BPD

Major refined products corridor (gasoline, diesel, jet fuel) on East Bank, running from Houston to East Coast markets. Combined capacity approximately 2.5 million barrels per day across both lines.

Collins Pipeline15–20 mi
Operator: Collins Pipeline Co.Product: Refined ProductsCapacity: 65,000 BPD

Refined products from PBF Chalmette Refinery (East Bank) to Collins, Mississippi. Terminus approximately 15–20 miles upriver from AMAX site.

Critical Data Gap — Resolved

The original analysis identified Gator Express as a critical data gap — not found in RBN Energy's 180-pipeline database. Research has now confirmed Gator Express consists of two co-located 42" laterals (FERC CP17-67), engineered by Gulf Companies, with combined design capacity of ~3.8 Bcf/d. The pipeline is a FERC-certificated facility pipeline (not common carrier) dedicated to Plaquemines LNG. Remaining unknown: exact route geometry relative to AMAX site — requires Louisiana DNR GIS overlay or direct Venture Global inquiry.

2025 Haynesville Takeaway Milestone

With LEG (operational July 2025) and NG3 (operational October 2025), approximately 3.5 Bcf/d of new Haynesville takeaway capacity entered service in 2025 — the largest single-year expansion in the basin's history. Haynesville-to-Gulf Coast flows averaged 5.7 Bcf/d through June 2025 at ~89% utilization. LEG ramped to 850+ MMcf/d within weeks of startup; NG3 commissioned at 250–300 MMcf/d with ramp ongoing. Both systems connect to Transco at or near the Gillis hub.

CP2 LNG — New Development (January 2026)

Venture Global announced FID and closed $15.1 billion in project financing for CP2 LNG Phase 1 in Cameron Parish — the largest standalone project financing ever. CP2 will have peak production capacity of 28 MTPA with first LNG expected in 2027. The associated CP Express Pipeline will require additional takeaway capacity from Haynesville. This is Venture Global's fourth FID in less than 6 years, with over $80 billion in capital markets transactions. Combined with Plaquemines expansion, Venture Global alone could operate 100+ MTPA of LNG capacity.

07 — Data Center & Industrial Demand

Emerging Gas Demand Beyond LNG

Meta Richland Parish Data Center

Meta Platforms has committed $10 billion to a hyperscale data center in Richland Parish, northeast Louisiana — one of the largest private investments in state history. The project requires massive new natural gas infrastructure: initial power demand of 2,250 MW (up to 5 GW at full buildout), supplied by three Entergy CCGT plants with ISD 2028–2029. Gas demand scales from 0.4–0.5 Bcf/d initially to 0.8–1.0 Bcf/d at 5 GW, requiring a 50–100 mile lateral from existing interstate systems. Additional hyperscale data centers are proposed across the Gulf Coast driven by AI and cloud computing, each requiring 500–2,000 MW (0.1–0.4 Bcf/d per facility).

Industrial & Petrochemical Corridor

Southeast Louisiana's chemical plant and refinery corridor (Baton Rouge to New Orleans) consumes 2–3 Bcf/d of natural gas for process heating, hydrogen production, and power generation. Three refineries within 20–50 miles of the AMAX site have combined capacity exceeding 900,000 BPD. Growth in petrochemical capacity — ethylene crackers, ammonia plants, methanol facilities — adds incremental gas demand beyond the LNG and data center drivers.

Gas Storage Expansion

The convergence of LNG export growth, data center power demand, and industrial expansion is driving new gas storage development. Louisiana's salt dome geology provides ideal conditions for rapid-cycle salt cavern storage. Existing Louisiana storage capacity exceeds 500 Bcf, with 100–200 Bcf of potential additions announced or under construction. Key drivers include LNG load balancing (daily and seasonal), Haynesville production management, industrial demand variability, and market arbitrage.

08 — Regional Gas Flow Patterns

Supply, Demand & Directional Flow

Supply Sources

Haynesville Shale (15+ Bcf/d production, 150–250 miles north of Port Nickel) — primary source, connected via multiple systems including LEG, NG3, LEAP, and Gulf Run. Offshore Gulf of Mexico (2–3 Bcf/d) — connects via onshore Louisiana gathering. Interstate imports — gas from Appalachia and Midwest flows through Louisiana to LNG facilities via Transco, Texas Eastern, and others.

Demand Centers

LNG Export (primary driver): 15+ Bcf/d and growing — dominant demand source. Industrial/Petrochemical: 3–5 Bcf/d (refineries, chemical plants, power generation). Interstate Export: 2–4 Bcf/d to Southeast states. Net flow pattern: Gas flows south and east through Louisiana toward LNG terminals, creating north-south pipeline corridors through Southeast Louisiana — with Port Nickel positioned directly along these flow paths.

Flow Disruption from Plaquemines LNG

The Plaquemines LNG ramp has already shifted flows on regional pipelines. Midcontinent Express Pipeline (MEP) started delivering gas to TGP to meet Plaquemines demand, while volumes to Transco declined. This reallocation of capacity to support LNG demand demonstrates how new load centers reshape pipeline economics — and why strategically located storage and interconnection facilities gain value.

09 — Strategic Implications

Pipeline Network Integration Opportunity

The AMAX site's combination of proximity to existing pipeline systems, Plaquemines LNG co-location, deep-draft river access, Class I rail service, and 341 acres of upland creates a viable foundation for natural gas storage facility development.

Site Advantages

Gulf South Pipeline (10–25 mi) — most likely natural gas interconnection option, with intrastate regulatory flexibility. Plaquemines LNG same parish (~50–75 mi south) — direct load-balancing value proposition for 3+ Bcf/d facility, growing to 58+ MTPA. Gator Express feed pipeline potentially traverses nearby corridor, creating additional interconnection possibility. 341 acres upland minimizes wetland mitigation costs versus coastal greenfield sites. Mississippi River deep-draft access for construction equipment and materials. Norfolk Southern Class I rail for ongoing operational logistics. Existing industrial infrastructure (dock, rail, laydown) reduces capital requirements versus greenfield. East Bank location provides direct access to major pipeline corridor without river crossings.

Key Challenges

Geological Uncertainty

Salt dome presence and suitability in Plaquemines Parish requires detailed subsurface assessment before cavern storage development commitment.

Capital Intensity

$200–600M total project cost: salt cavern development + surface infrastructure + pipeline lateral to Gulf South or other system.

Regulatory Timeline

FERC approval required if interstate connection (12–24 months); Louisiana intrastate approvals faster. Environmental review adds complexity.

Operator Partnership

AMAX likely needs experienced midstream partner — Boardwalk (Gulf South), Williams, or Venture Global — for development and operations.

Market Competition

Existing Louisiana storage facilities with established commercial relationships. Belle Chasse Terminal (3,200 acres) is a competing large-scale facility upriver.

Policy Risk

Federal LNG export policy uncertainty affects post-2028 timelines. Commodity price volatility impacts storage economics. CP2 DOE review uncertainty.

10 — Recommendations & Next Steps

Phased Development Pathway

Phase 1: Infrastructure Mapping & Feasibility (Q1–Q2 2026)

Obtain Louisiana DNR pipeline GIS data for 25-mile radius. Request Gulf South Pipeline system maps from Boardwalk Pipeline Partners. Identify Gator Express route via Venture Global inquiry or Louisiana DNR. Commission geological assessment of salt dome presence in Plaquemines Parish. Market demand study for storage capacity needs, competitive analysis, and revenue projections. Interconnection engineering: route analysis, permitting assessment, capital cost estimate. Budget: $250K–$500K.

Phase 2: Interconnection Feasibility & Operator Outreach

Engineering assessment of pipeline connection options. Regulatory requirements evaluation (FERC if interstate, Louisiana PSC if intrastate). Preliminary discussions with potential partners: Boardwalk (Gulf South), Williams (Transco), Venture Global (Plaquemines LNG). Cost estimates for connection infrastructure. Pursue GIS analysis for precise distance measurements.

Phase 3: Commercial Development

Define specific use case for pipeline access (gas supply, storage interconnection, etc.). Develop commercial framework with pipeline operators. Secure experienced midstream partner. Pursue necessary permits and approvals. Target FID upon confirmation of all four prerequisites: geological feasibility, market demand, operator partnership, and regulatory pathway.

Decision Framework

Proceed to full development only if Phase 1 confirms all four prerequisites: geological feasibility (salt dome suitable for cavern storage), market demand (storage justified by LNG load balancing or industrial demand), operator partnership (experienced midstream partner committed), and regulatory pathway (FERC or Louisiana approvals achievable within acceptable timeline).

Market Timing

Near-term (2025–2027): LNG construction peak; LEG and NG3 operating; Plaquemines LNG ramping to full capacity; CP2 FID and construction commencing. Mid-term (2028–2030): Meta data center CCGT plants operational; Golden Pass, Port Arthur, Rio Grande commissioning; Louisiana LNG (Woodside) potential ISD; Plaquemines expansion construction. Long-term (post-2030): Potential ~40+ Bcf/d total Gulf Coast LNG capacity; multiple new storage facilities for load balancing; ongoing pipeline network densification.

Annex A: Gator Express Pipeline — Technical Detail

FERC Dockets: CP17-66 (Plaquemines LNG) and CP17-67 (Gator Express Pipeline)

Authorization: September 30, 2019, by Federal Energy Regulatory Commission

Configuration: Two co-located 42-inch diameter natural gas pipeline laterals sharing one right-of-way corridor for the majority of their routes.

Segment 1 (Southwest Lateral TGP): ~15.1-mile, 42" pipeline from Tennessee Gas Pipeline interconnection to Plaquemines LNG terminal.

Segment 2 (Southwest Lateral TETCO): ~11.7-mile, 42" looped pipeline from Texas Eastern Transmission platform to terminal.

Combined Length: ~26.8 miles

Design Capacity: Each segment ~1.9 Bcf/d (~3.8 Bcf/d total design capacity)

Engineering: Gulf Companies — HDD in shallow water, DirectPipe crossing at LNG terminal entrance, pipe bridge over levee, SCADA, and cathodic protection systems.

Location: Entirely within Plaquemines Parish, Louisiana. West bank of Mississippi River, approximately 20 miles south of Belle Chasse.

Status: Both phases operational. Phase 2 gas introduction authorized by FERC April 2024. Facility pipeline (not common carrier) — dedicated to Plaquemines LNG.

Feed Sources: TGP, TETCO, and Columbia Gulf via interconnections. Plaquemines LNG feedgas averaged 2.68 Bcf/d in July 2025, reaching a peak of 2.92 Bcf/d on July 13, 2025. Averaged 3+ Bcf/d by August 2025.

Plaquemines Expansion Impact: Venture Global filed for Plaquemines LNG brownfield expansion (FERC CP26-27, November 2025) — 30+ MTPA additional capacity requiring an associated Kosciusko Junction Pipeline Project. This expansion would require additional feed gas infrastructure and could create new interconnection opportunities in Plaquemines Parish.

Annex C: Glossary of Terms

Bcf/d — Billion cubic feet per day (natural gas capacity). Standard unit for large-scale gas transmission.

kbd / BPD — Thousand barrels per day / Barrels per day (crude oil capacity). One barrel = 42 U.S. gallons.

MMcf/d — Million cubic feet per day. 1,000 MMcf/d = 1 Bcf/d.

MTPA — Million tonnes per annum (LNG capacity). Approximately 0.13 Bcf/d per MTPA.

LNG — Liquefied Natural Gas. Cooled to ~-260°F for volume reduction and ocean transport.

NGL — Natural Gas Liquids. Hydrocarbon components: ethane, propane, butane, natural gasoline.

AHP — Above Head of Passes. Mississippi River mile marker system. Port Nickel at Mile 80 AHP.

FERC — Federal Energy Regulatory Commission. Regulates interstate pipelines.

Interstate Pipeline — Crosses state boundaries; regulated by FERC.

Intrastate Pipeline — Operates within single state; regulated by state commission (Louisiana PSC).

Common Carrier — Pipeline offering services to multiple shippers on non-discriminatory basis.

Dedicated Facility Pipeline — Built to serve specific facility; may not offer common carrier service. Gator Express is this type.

HDD — Horizontal Directional Drill. Trenchless construction method for pipeline crossings.

Haynesville Shale — Major natural gas producing region in North Louisiana/East Texas. 15+ Bcf/d production.

Gillis Hub — Major natural gas trading and pipeline convergence point in Beauregard Parish, Louisiana.

LOOP — Louisiana Offshore Oil Port. Deepwater crude terminal in Gulf of Mexico.

Y-Grade — Raw mixed NGL stream before fractionation into individual components.

CCGT — Combined Cycle Gas Turbine. Power generation technology requiring natural gas fuel.
