# Chapter 10: Infrastructure Investment and Capacity Outlook

## Executive Summary

US bulk supply chain infrastructure is at an inflection point, with the largest federal investment in decades -- anchored by the $2.5 billion IIJA allocation for inland waterway construction -- beginning to address a maintenance and capacity backlog that has accumulated over half a century of underinvestment. Key projects including the Chickamauga Lock replacement, Kentucky Lock addition, and LaGrange Lock and Dam rehabilitation will reduce transit delays on critical waterway corridors, while port expansion projects such as the Houston Ship Channel widening (Project 11) and New Orleans terminal modernization will increase vessel accommodation capacity. Rail infrastructure investment remains robust at $25--28 billion annually across Class I carriers, though capacity allocation increasingly favors intermodal over bulk carload traffic. Pipeline infrastructure continues to expand in the Gulf Coast and Permian Basin corridors, with $14--18 billion in active construction reshaping energy commodity logistics. Simultaneously, the sector faces structural challenges including an aging maritime workforce, climate-driven disruption to waterway operations, and the capital demands of decarbonization. This chapter assesses the investment pipeline, identifies remaining infrastructure gaps, and projects the capacity implications for bulk supply chains through 2035.

## 10.1 IIJA Waterway Investment

### Funding Overview

The Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA, P.L. 117-58) appropriated approximately $2.5 billion for inland waterway construction through the US Army Corps of Engineers (USACE), supplementing the existing Inland Waterways Trust Fund (IWTF) cost-share mechanism. This represents a step change in federal waterway investment, which had averaged only $500--700 million annually in the decade prior.

Key funding provisions:

- **$2.5 billion** in direct appropriations for inland waterway lock and dam construction
- **Modified cost-share formula:** 65% general Treasury / 35% IWTF (established by WRRDA 2014, reinforced by IIJA)
- **Acceleration of project timelines** through advance funding authority, enabling multi-year construction contracts without annual appropriation uncertainty

The IWTF itself has maintained a healthy balance in recent years, supported by the $0.29/gallon diesel fuel tax on commercial waterway operators. FY 2025 IWTF receipts were approximately $130 million, enabling the 35% cost-share obligation to be met for multiple concurrent projects.

### Priority Construction Projects

#### Chickamauga Lock Replacement (Tennessee River, TN)

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Waterway | Tennessee River, Mile 471 |
| Existing lock | 60 ft x 360 ft (built 1940) |
| New lock | 110 ft x 600 ft |
| Total project cost | $893 million |
| IIJA allocation | $459 million |
| Completion target | 2027 |
| Capacity impact | Eliminates double-lockage requirement for standard 15-barge tows; reduces average transit delay from 5+ hours to <1 hour |

The Chickamauga Lock is the most significant single bottleneck on the Tennessee River system. The existing 60-foot-wide chamber requires tows to break apart and transit in multiple cuts, adding hours of delay per lockage. The replacement 110-foot chamber will accommodate full tows in a single pass. As of early 2026, construction is approximately 75% complete, with concrete placement for the new chamber walls nearing the final pour stages.

#### Kentucky Lock Addition (Tennessee River, KY)

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Waterway | Tennessee River, Mile 22.4 |
| Existing lock | 110 ft x 600 ft (built 1944) |
| New lock | 110 ft x 1,200 ft |
| Total project cost | $2.4 billion (revised estimate) |
| IIJA allocation | $465 million (partial) |
| Completion target | 2029 (revised) |
| Capacity impact | Doubles throughput capacity at the gateway to the Tennessee River; reduces congestion affecting Ohio-Tennessee interchange traffic |

Kentucky Lock is the first lock on the Tennessee River from its confluence with the Ohio. As the gateway to the entire Tennessee River system, it processes all commercial traffic entering or exiting the waterway. The 1,200-foot addition will allow simultaneous processing of two tows. The project has experienced significant cost escalation since original authorization (initially estimated at $775 million in 1996 dollars), reflecting the challenges of concurrent construction alongside an active navigation channel.

#### LaGrange Lock and Dam (Illinois Waterway, IL)

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Waterway | Illinois Waterway, Mile 80.2 |
| Existing lock | 110 ft x 600 ft (built 1939) |
| Project scope | Major rehabilitation, not replacement |
| Total project cost | $227 million |
| IIJA allocation | $112 million |
| Completion target | 2026 |
| Capacity impact | Addresses structural deficiencies that risk unscheduled closures; maintains reliability on the Chicago-to-Gulf corridor |

The Illinois Waterway connects the Great Lakes (via the Chicago Sanitary and Ship Canal) to the Mississippi River system and is the primary corridor for grain and bulk commodities moving from the upper Midwest to Gulf export facilities. LaGrange rehabilitation is approximately 90% complete as of early 2026, with remaining work focused on gate machinery replacement and dam face repairs.

## 10.2 Port Expansion Projects

### Houston Ship Channel Improvement Project (Project 11)

The Houston Ship Channel (HSC) is the busiest waterway in the United States by tonnage and serves as the primary gateway for bulk commodity imports and petrochemical exports in the Gulf region.

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Current channel dimensions | 45 ft deep x 530 ft wide (Galveston Bay reach) |
| Improved dimensions | 46.5 ft deep x 700 ft wide (select reaches); wider passing areas |
| Total project cost | $1.6 billion (federal + local) |
| Federal IIJA/USACE allocation | $537 million |
| Local sponsor (Port Houston) | $540 million + |
| Completion target | 2028 (phased) |
| Capacity impact | Enables two-way traffic for Suezmax/Aframax vessels; reduces transit delays currently averaging 3--5 hours for inbound deep-draft vessels |

For bulk supply chains, the HSC widening will:
- Reduce vessel waiting times that currently add $30,000--$75,000 per day in demurrage and off-hire costs
- Allow simultaneous passage of bulk carriers and tankers in expanded reaches
- Support deeper draft loading of Panamax bulk carriers, increasing per-voyage cargo capacity by 5--10%

### New Orleans Terminal Modernization

The Port of New Orleans and associated lower Mississippi River terminals are undergoing multiple modernization projects:

- **Napoleon Avenue Container Terminal expansion:** $118 million investment to increase capacity, with intermodal connector improvements benefiting adjacent bulk terminals
- **Inland port connector dredging:** Maintaining the Mississippi River Ship Channel at 50 feet (deepened from 45 feet under a separate USACE project completed in 2023)
- **Louisiana International Terminal (LIT):** New $1.8 billion terminal in St. Bernard Parish, primarily container-focused but including bulk-capable berths and improved river-to-rail connectivity
- **Midstream buoy operations:** Continued investment in ship-to-barge transfer capabilities that enable bulk operations without dedicated berth infrastructure

## 10.3 Rail Capacity Investment

### Class I Capital Expenditure Trends

The seven Class I railroads collectively invest $25--28 billion annually in capital expenditures, of which approximately 75--80% is directed toward maintenance of way and structures (track, bridges, tunnels) and the remainder toward equipment, technology, and expansion.

| Year | Total Class I Capex ($B) | Maintenance of Way ($B) | Equipment ($B) | Technology/Other ($B) |
|------|-------------------------|------------------------|----------------|---------------------|
| 2020 | $22.8 | $15.1 | $4.9 | $2.8 |
| 2021 | $24.1 | $16.2 | $5.0 | $2.9 |
| 2022 | $26.3 | $17.8 | $5.2 | $3.3 |
| 2023 | $27.1 | $18.4 | $5.3 | $3.4 |
| 2024 | $26.8 | $18.1 | $5.2 | $3.5 |
| 2025 (est.) | $27.5 | $18.6 | $5.1 | $3.8 |

### Positive Train Control (PTC) and Beyond

PTC implementation was substantially completed by the 2020 federal deadline (with extensions), at a total industry cost exceeding $15 billion. The PTC infrastructure now provides a digital backbone for further capacity and efficiency enhancements:

- **Enhanced scheduling optimization** using PTC data to reduce headway between trains
- **Dynamic block signaling** that increases line capacity by 15--25% on equipped corridors
- **Predictive maintenance** using sensor data to reduce unplanned track outages
- **Energy management systems** that optimize locomotive throttle and braking for fuel efficiency

### Intermodal vs. Bulk Capacity Allocation

A structural tension exists in Class I railroad capital allocation between intermodal franchise growth and bulk/carload maintenance:

- **Intermodal traffic** generates higher revenue per train-mile and is growing at 3--5% annually, attracting the majority of capacity expansion investment (new intermodal terminals, double-stack clearance projects, dedicated intermodal corridors).
- **Bulk/carload traffic** is flat or declining in volume for most commodity categories (coal especially), leading railroads to rationalize rather than expand bulk-serving infrastructure.
- **Implications for bulk shippers:** Service quality (transit time reliability, car supply consistency) may erode on corridors where bulk traffic competes with intermodal for capacity, particularly during peak shipping seasons.

### Short-Line Railroad Infrastructure

The 600+ short-line and regional railroads that provide first/last-mile rail service for many bulk shippers represent a critical but underfunded segment of the rail network:

- **CRISI grants:** The Consolidated Rail Infrastructure and Safety Improvements program has allocated approximately $2.1 billion since 2017, though annual demand exceeds available funding by 4--5x
- **Railroad Rehabilitation and Improvement Financing (RRIF):** Federal credit assistance program providing low-interest loans for track rehabilitation, with approximately $35 billion in lending authority
- **State programs:** 38 states operate rail assistance programs, collectively investing $400--600 million annually in short-line infrastructure

### Crude Oil and Refined Products

- **Permian Basin takeaway:** Total pipeline capacity from the Permian to Gulf Coast refining and export hubs exceeds 7.5 million bbl/d, with additional expansions planned as production approaches 6.5 million bbl/d
- **Refined product distribution:** Colonial Pipeline (2.5 million bbl/d) and Plantation Pipeline (660,000 bbl/d) remain the backbone of Gulf-to-East Coast refined product delivery
- **Pipeline reversal and repurposing:** Several legacy crude oil pipelines are being repurposed for NGL or CO2 service as production patterns shift

### Pipeline-Waterway Interaction

Pipeline and waterway infrastructure interact at several critical junctures:

- **Pipeline river crossings:** Over 3,500 pipeline crossings of navigable waterways require ongoing maintenance and inspection coordination with USACE and USCG
- **Terminal connectivity:** Major bulk liquid terminals along the Lower Mississippi River and Houston Ship Channel serve as pipeline-to-vessel transfer points, with combined throughput exceeding 4 billion barrels annually
- **Competing modal share:** For liquid bulk commodities, pipeline expansion can reduce barge volumes on parallel corridors, as seen on the Ohio River where refined product barge traffic has declined 15--20% over the past decade as pipeline capacity increased

### Carbon Capture Pipeline Infrastructure

An emerging category of pipeline infrastructure supports carbon capture, utilization, and storage (CCUS):

- **Class VI well permitting:** EPA has issued 8 Class VI permits for CO2 injection wells as of Q1 2026, with over 100 applications pending
- **CO2 pipeline proposals:** Multiple projects totaling over 3,000 miles of new CO2 pipeline have been proposed across the Gulf Coast, Midwest, and Great Plains
- **Regulatory challenges:** State-level siting authority disputes and landowner opposition have delayed several major CO2 pipeline projects, including Summit Carbon Solutions' Midwest corridor

## 10.5 Supply Chain Resilience

### Lessons from COVID-19 Disruptions (2020--2022)

The COVID-19 pandemic and its aftermath exposed several vulnerabilities in US bulk supply chains:

1. **Labor shortages:** Crew quarantine requirements and workforce attrition caused vessel and facility operational delays. The maritime industry lost an estimated 8--12% of its experienced workforce through early retirement and career changes during 2020--2022.
2. **Equipment imbalances:** Container and railcar dislocations created cascading shortages. Bulk shippers competing for the same railcar fleet as intermodal traffic faced car supply shortages and extended transit times.
3. **Port congestion:** Though primarily a container phenomenon, bulk terminals experienced indirect congestion as port resources (tugs, pilots, labor) were diverted to address container backlogs.
4. **Supply chain visibility gaps:** Many bulk shippers lacked real-time visibility into inventory positions, vessel locations, and infrastructure status, limiting their ability to respond to disruptions.

### Climate Adaptation

Climate change is altering the operational parameters of bulk supply chain infrastructure:

- **Low water events:** The 2022 Mississippi River low-water crisis reduced barge loading capacity by 25--40% for over two months, adding $15--30/ton to barge freight on affected corridors. Climate models project increasing frequency and severity of low-water events, with NOAA projections suggesting a 30--50% increase in drought severity on the Lower Mississippi by 2050.
- **Flooding:** Conversely, high-water events cause lock closures and navigation restrictions. The 2019 flood season closed portions of the Upper Mississippi and Missouri River systems for months, diverting an estimated 20 million tons of bulk cargo to rail.
- **Hurricane exposure:** Gulf Coast ports and waterway infrastructure face increasing hurricane intensity, with post-storm recovery times of 1--4 weeks becoming a regular operational risk. Hurricane preparedness plans now routinely include pre-positioning of emergency dredging assets and rapid-deployment navigation aids.
- **Temperature extremes:** Rail track heat restrictions during extreme heat events reduce train speeds and line capacity by 15--25%, while winter freeze events close Great Lakes navigation (typically December--March) and restrict river operations.
- **Sea level rise:** Gulf Coast bulk terminals face long-term elevation challenges, with USACE projections of 1.5--4.5 feet of sea level rise by 2100 along the Louisiana and Texas coasts requiring phased facility elevation and flood protection investments.

### Resilience Investment Priorities

- **Redundant routing capability:** Developing alternative modal options (barge/rail/truck) for critical bulk supply chains, with particular focus on identifying trigger points for modal switching
- **Inventory buffer strategies:** Pre-positioning inventory to hedge against 30--60 day disruption scenarios, with cost-benefit analysis of buffer stock investment versus disruption risk
- **Real-time monitoring:** Deploying AIS, IoT, and satellite-based tracking for waterway, rail, and truck segments to enable proactive rerouting
- **Infrastructure hardening:** Flood walls, pump stations, and hurricane-rated facility upgrades at critical bulk terminals, particularly along the Gulf Intracoastal Waterway

## 10.6 Workforce Challenges

### Aging Maritime Workforce

The US maritime workforce faces a demographic crisis:

| Workforce Segment | Average Age | Projected Retirement (by 2030) | Replacement Pipeline |
|-------------------|-------------|-------------------------------|---------------------|
| Licensed deck officers | 52 | 35--40% | Maritime academy graduates (~1,200/year) |
| Licensed engineers | 54 | 40--45% | Maritime academy + hawsepipe (~800/year) |
| Inland towboat operators | 48 | 25--30% | Apprenticeship programs expanding |
| Harbor pilots | 56 | 30--35% | 7--10 year training pipeline; extremely limited |
| Longshoremen/stevedores | 50 | 30--35% | Union hiring halls; varies by port |

The pipeline of replacement workers is insufficient to offset projected retirements. Maritime academy enrollment has been flat for a decade, and the profession faces competition from higher-paying shoreside logistics and energy sector jobs. The six state maritime academies and the US Merchant Marine Academy at Kings Point collectively graduate approximately 1,200 licensed officers per year, against a projected annual replacement need of 1,800--2,200.

### Jones Act Crew Requirements

The Jones Act requirement that 75% of crew on coastwise vessels be US citizens creates an additional constraint:

- US-citizen mariner wages are 2.5--3.5x higher than international equivalents
- The qualified labor pool is limited, creating chronic staffing challenges for Jones Act operators
- Crew availability constraints can delay vessel departures and reduce fleet operational days
- USCG credential processing backlogs of 6--12 months for new mariners compound the shortage

### Mitigation Strategies

- **Increased academy capacity:** Federal appropriations for expanded enrollment at the US Merchant Marine Academy and six state maritime academies, with a target of increasing annual graduates to 1,600 by 2030
- **Apprenticeship programs:** Industry-funded programs for inland waterway operators (e.g., Ingram Barge, American Commercial Barge Line, Kirby Inland Marine)
- **Regulatory flexibility:** Potential USCG waivers for credentialing timelines (politically difficult given labor union opposition)
- **Compensation increases:** Inland waterway operators have raised entry-level wages 20--35% since 2020 to compete with trucking and energy sector employment
- **Automation:** Longer-term reduction in crew requirements through autonomous vessel technology (see Section 10.7)

## 10.7 Technology Trends

### Autonomous and Semi-Autonomous Vessels

Autonomous vessel technology is advancing rapidly in controlled environments:

- **Inland waterways:** Semi-autonomous towboat navigation systems are in pilot testing by multiple operators, with initial applications in straight-channel transit segments. Companies including Sea Machines Robotics and Buffalo Automation have demonstrated supervised autonomous operation on designated river segments. Full autonomy remains distant (2035+) due to the complexity of lock approaches, fleeting operations, and traffic density.
- **Short-sea shipping:** Autonomous coastal vessels (e.g., Yara Birkeland in Norway, Suzaku in Japan) have demonstrated feasibility for fixed-route, short-distance bulk movements. US application faces Jones Act crew requirements and USCG regulatory approval hurdles.
- **Deep-sea:** Autonomous ocean-going bulk carriers are at the research stage, with remote monitoring and reduced-crew concepts more likely than zero-crew operations in the medium term. NYK Line and MOL are piloting remote bridge monitoring systems on trans-Pacific routes.

### Digital Lock Infrastructure

USACE is investing in the modernization of lock operations through digital technology:

- **Electronic Logbook (ELOG) system:** Fully deployed across the inland waterway system, replacing paper-based lock reporting with digital transit records accessible in real-time
- **Lock Queue Management Systems:** AIS-based vessel tracking integrated with lock scheduling algorithms to optimize queue management and reduce wait times. Pilot programs on the Ohio River have reduced average wait times by 15--20%
- **Remote lock operation:** USACE has demonstrated remote operation capability for low-traffic locks, enabling a single operator to manage multiple lock sites simultaneously. Operational deployment is planned for 10--15 sites by 2028.
- **Predictive maintenance sensors:** Vibration, strain, and water quality sensors deployed on lock gates, valves, and dam structures enable condition-based maintenance that reduces unscheduled closure risk

### Precision Navigation

The precision navigation initiative represents a paradigm shift in waterway management:

- **High-definition channel surveys:** Multi-beam sonar surveys updated monthly (vs. annually) on critical reaches, with real-time depth data transmitted to vessel operators
- **Dynamic channel marking:** GPS-corrected navigation buoy positions updated in real-time to reflect changing channel conditions, replacing static charting
- **Under-keel clearance optimization:** Real-time water level and draft monitoring that enables operators to maximize cargo loading while maintaining safe under-keel clearance, potentially increasing per-barge payload by 3--8% on draft-constrained reaches
- **eNavigation integration:** USCG eNavigation initiative linking AIS, electronic charting, and real-time environmental data into integrated bridge displays for towboat operators

### Digital Twin Infrastructure

Digital twin technology -- creating real-time virtual replicas of physical infrastructure -- is being applied to bulk supply chain assets:

- **Lock and dam digital twins:** USACE is piloting digital twin models for major lock complexes, enabling predictive maintenance scheduling that reduces unplanned closures by an estimated 20--30%.
- **Port digital twins:** Major ports (Houston, NOLA, Savannah) are developing comprehensive digital twin platforms that model vessel traffic, berth allocation, and equipment deployment to optimize throughput.
- **Terminal operations:** Bulk terminal operators are implementing digital twin models that simulate stockpile management, conveyor routing, and loading/unloading sequences to maximize throughput and minimize demurrage.

### AI-Driven Route Optimization

Artificial intelligence and machine learning are being deployed across bulk supply chain planning:

- **Barge fleet optimization:** AI-powered routing algorithms that incorporate real-time water levels, lock queue data, and weather forecasts to optimize tow compositions and departure timing. Early adopters report 5--12% fuel savings and 8--15% improvement in on-time performance.
- **Rail network optimization:** Class I railroads are using machine learning for train dispatching, crew scheduling, and locomotive distribution, with reported efficiency gains of 3--7% in network velocity.
- **Predictive analytics:** AI models that forecast demand patterns, infrastructure disruptions, and commodity flow shifts, enabling proactive supply chain adjustment rather than reactive response.
- **Cargo matching platforms:** Digital freight marketplaces for barge and rail capacity that use algorithmic pricing and matching to improve fleet utilization and reduce empty repositioning.

## 10.9 Infrastructure Gaps and Unfunded Needs

Despite the IIJA's historic investment, significant infrastructure gaps remain:

### Inland Waterways

- **Unfunded backlog:** USACE estimates $13--17 billion in unmet inland waterway capital needs beyond IIJA funding
- **Lock 25 (Upper Mississippi):** The system's worst bottleneck remains in the study phase, with construction funding not yet secured. Estimated replacement cost: $1.5--2.0 billion. Average delay exceeds 6 hours during the fall harvest shipping peak.
- **Small-scale locks:** Numerous 600-foot locks on the Upper Mississippi and Ohio River tributaries lack funding for 1,200-foot extensions, perpetuating double-lockage delays that add $1,500--$3,000 per lockage in tow operating costs
- **Aging dam infrastructure:** Over 60% of USACE navigation dams exceed their 50-year design life, with rehabilitation needs competing with lock investment for limited funding
- **Channel maintenance dredging:** Annual maintenance dredging requirements average $1.2--1.5 billion but have been chronically underfunded, leading to channel dimension deficiencies on 15--20% of commercially navigable miles

### Ports

- **Landside connectivity:** Many bulk ports lack adequate rail and highway connections to efficiently move cargo inland. The "last mile" problem -- congested port-area roads and insufficient rail switching capacity -- constrains throughput even when waterside capacity is adequate.
- **Resilience investment:** Post-hurricane and climate adaptation infrastructure (elevated facilities, hardened utilities, backup power) remains largely unfunded at most Gulf and Atlantic coast bulk terminals.
- **Draft limitations:** Several major bulk ports (including portions of the Mississippi River above Baton Rouge) operate at depths below what modern bulk carrier designs require for full loading.
- **Berth modernization:** Aging berth infrastructure at publicly owned bulk terminals requires an estimated $3--5 billion in renovation over the next decade to maintain structural integrity and accommodate larger vessels.

### Rail

- **Short-line railroad deferred maintenance:** The 600+ short-line and regional railroads that provide first/last-mile rail service for many bulk shippers face a cumulative capital backlog of $12--15 billion. Federal grant programs (CRISI, RDP) address only a fraction of this need.
- **Grade crossing safety:** Over 130,000 at-grade rail crossings remain, creating safety risks and slowing both rail and truck traffic. Elimination costs far exceed available funding.
- **Capacity bottleneck corridors:** Chicago (the nation's busiest rail hub), Houston, and New Orleans area rail networks face chronic congestion that delays bulk rail traffic by 12--48 hours. The CREATE program in Chicago has completed 30 of 70 planned projects, with remaining work requiring an additional $3+ billion.

## 10.11 Implications for Commodity Analysis

The infrastructure investment and capacity outlook has direct implications for commodity-specific supply chain analysis. When extending this framework to individual commodities, the following considerations should guide the assessment:

1. **Project completion timing matters for cost projections.** Lock improvements at Chickamauga (2027) and Kentucky (2029) will reduce barge transit costs on the Tennessee River by eliminating delay-driven costs quantified in Chapter 9. Commodity analyses for products moving on these corridors should model pre- and post-completion cost scenarios.

2. **Port deepening changes vessel economics.** The Norfolk deepening to 55 feet and Mobile deepening to 50 feet will enable larger bulk vessels to load fully, reducing per-ton ocean freight and port costs. Commodity analyses should assess whether the relevant supply chain can capitalize on these improvements.

3. **Rail capacity allocation trends affect service quality.** Class I railroads' prioritization of intermodal investment over bulk/carload infrastructure suggests that bulk rail service reliability may not improve materially. Commodity analyses dependent on rail should incorporate a service quality risk assessment and evaluate barge or truck alternatives where feasible.

4. **Pipeline expansion reshapes liquid bulk logistics.** Increasing pipeline capacity on Gulf Coast corridors will continue to draw liquid bulk volumes away from barge and rail, potentially improving capacity availability for dry bulk commodities on shared waterway and rail infrastructure.

5. **Workforce constraints may limit capacity utilization.** Even as physical infrastructure improves, workforce shortages in piloting, towboat operations, and terminal labor may prevent full utilization of new capacity. Commodity analyses should consider labor availability as a potential bottleneck.

6. **Climate risk requires scenario modeling.** The increasing frequency of extreme weather events (low water, flooding, hurricanes) necessitates supply chain resilience analysis for each commodity. At minimum, commodity analyses should model a 30-day disruption scenario on the primary transportation corridor and identify mitigation strategies.

7. **Energy transition reshapes long-term demand patterns.** Coal-dependent corridors will see declining volumes, potentially improving capacity availability for other bulk commodities on shared infrastructure. Conversely, corridors serving renewable energy material supply chains may face new congestion as demand grows.

8. **Technology adoption timelines vary by mode.** Autonomous vessel and digital lock technologies will deliver efficiency gains incrementally over the 2026--2035 period. Commodity analyses should not assume near-term technology-driven cost reductions but should flag corridors where pilot programs are underway.

9. **Unfunded infrastructure gaps represent persistent risk.** The Lock and Dam 25 replacement on the Upper Mississippi remains the system's most critical unfunded need. Commodity analyses for products transiting this corridor should incorporate delay cost scenarios that assume no improvement before 2035 at the earliest.
