# Chapter 8: Regulatory Environment

## Executive Summary

The US bulk supply chain operates within a multi-layered regulatory framework that imposes significant cost and operational constraints on shippers, carriers, and terminal operators. The most consequential near-term development is the USTR's Section 301 maritime shipping fees targeting Chinese-built vessels, which will add $16--33 per ton to bulk shipments on affected trades when fully phased in by 2027. Legacy regulations including the Jones Act, Surface Transportation Board rate oversight, EPA emission and discharge standards, and USCG security mandates collectively shape the competitive landscape, influencing modal choice, port selection, and total landed cost. This chapter provides a commodity-agnostic assessment of the regulatory environment, establishing the compliance cost baseline that will be applied to individual commodity analyses in subsequent volumes.

## 8.1 Section 301 Maritime Shipping Fees (USTR 2025)

### Background and Legal Authority

In April 2025, the Office of the United States Trade Representative (USTR) finalized an investigation under Section 301 of the Trade Act of 1974, concluding that China's dominance in global shipbuilding constitutes an actionable practice that burdens US commerce. The resulting action imposes per-voyage fees on Chinese-built vessels calling at US ports, representing the first use of Section 301 authority to target maritime shipping infrastructure rather than imported goods.

### Fee Structure for Bulk Carriers

The fee schedule distinguishes between vessel types, with dry bulk and liquid bulk carriers subject to the following framework:

| Phase | Effective Date | Fee per Voyage (Bulk Carriers) | Basis |
|-------|---------------|-------------------------------|-------|
| Phase 1 | October 2025 | $500,000 | Per net ton of vessel capacity, minimum floor |
| Phase 2 | April 2026 | $750,000 | Escalation with adjusted per-ton multiplier |
| Phase 3 | April 2027 | $1,000,000 | Full implementation rate |

Fees apply to any vessel where the hull was constructed at a Chinese shipyard, regardless of current flag state, ownership, or operating company. Vessels that have undergone major conversion outside China may qualify for partial exemptions, though the criteria remain under regulatory refinement.

### Per-Ton Cost Impact

For a standard Supramax bulk carrier (approximately 55,000 DWT) loading a full cargo:

- **Phase 1:** $500,000 / 55,000 tons = **~$9.09/ton**
- **Phase 2:** $750,000 / 55,000 tons = **~$13.64/ton**
- **Phase 3:** $1,000,000 / 55,000 tons = **~$18.18/ton**

For a Handysize vessel (approximately 30,000 DWT), the per-ton impact is more severe:

- **Phase 1:** $500,000 / 30,000 tons = **~$16.67/ton**
- **Phase 3:** $1,000,000 / 30,000 tons = **~$33.33/ton**

The $16--33 per-ton range reflects the spread across vessel sizes and phases. This cost is expected to be passed through to cargo interests via charter party terms or freight rate adjustments.

### Fleet Exposure

Approximately 50--55% of the global dry bulk fleet (by number of vessels) was built in Chinese shipyards, with the proportion higher among newer Supramax and Ultramax classes. This concentration means that avoiding Chinese-built tonnage will constrain available capacity on US trades and likely produce a bifurcation in charter rates between Chinese-built and non-Chinese-built vessels.

### Industry Response and Litigation

Multiple industry groups, including the World Shipping Council and individual carrier associations, have filed legal challenges arguing that Section 301 authority does not extend to per-voyage service fees as opposed to tariffs on goods. As of early 2026, preliminary injunction requests have had mixed outcomes, and the Phase 1 fee remains in effect while litigation proceeds.

## 8.2 Jones Act (Merchant Marine Act of 1920)

### Cabotage Requirements

Section 27 of the Merchant Marine Act of 1920 (the Jones Act) requires that all merchandise transported by water between US ports be carried on vessels that are:

1. **Built in the United States** at a US shipyard
2. **Owned by US citizens** (at least 75% ownership)
3. **Flagged under the US registry**
4. **Crewed by US citizens or permanent residents** (at least 75% of crew)

These requirements apply to all coastwise trade, including movements between mainland ports, between the mainland and noncontiguous states/territories (Alaska, Hawaii, Puerto Rico, Guam), and all inland waterway transportation.

### Domestic Fleet Constraints

The Jones Act-eligible fleet is substantially smaller and older than the global fleet:

| Metric | Jones Act Fleet | Global Fleet |
|--------|----------------|--------------|
| Total vessels (self-propelled, 1,000+ DWT) | ~99 | ~12,500+ |
| Average vessel age | 20+ years | ~12 years |
| Newbuild cost premium vs. foreign yard | 5--8x | Baseline |
| Annual crew cost (comparable vessel) | $3.5--5.0M | $1.0--1.5M |

The constrained supply of Jones Act vessels creates a structural capacity limitation for domestic waterborne movements, particularly for bulk commodities moving between Gulf Coast, East Coast, and Great Lakes ports.

### Cost Premium Analysis

Multiple economic studies have estimated the cost premium of Jones Act compliance on domestic shipping. The Government Accountability Office (GAO) and Congressional Research Service (CRS) analyses suggest:

- **Jones Act vessel operating costs** are approximately **2.5--4.0x** higher than comparable foreign-flag vessels, driven primarily by crew costs, insurance, and maintenance in US shipyards.
- **Freight rates** for Jones Act-eligible movements are estimated at **80--100% higher** than a hypothetical scenario where foreign-flag vessels were permitted in coastwise trade.
- **For bulk commodities**, the effective premium translates to approximately **$5--15/ton** on typical Gulf-to-East Coast movements, depending on cargo type and vessel size.

The cost premium is most acute for commodities where domestic waterborne transport competes with rail or truck alternatives, as it narrows or eliminates the inherent cost advantage of water transportation.

### Reform Prospects

Legislative efforts to reform or repeal the Jones Act have been introduced repeatedly but have never advanced past committee. The domestic shipbuilding industry, maritime labor unions, and national security advocates constitute a durable coalition supporting the status quo. For planning purposes, the Jones Act should be treated as a permanent feature of the US regulatory landscape.

## 8.3 Surface Transportation Board (STB)

### Jurisdiction and Rate Oversight

The Surface Transportation Board is an independent federal agency with economic regulatory authority over freight railroads. Its jurisdiction is most relevant to bulk supply chains in the following areas:

- **Rate reasonableness complaints** filed by shippers alleging that rail rates exceed competitive levels
- **Common carrier obligations** requiring railroads to provide service upon reasonable request
- **Rail merger and acquisition review**
- **Service adequacy** oversight and emergency service orders

### Uniform Rail Costing System (URCS)

The URCS is the STB's cost accounting methodology used to estimate the variable costs a railroad incurs in providing a specific movement. Key elements include:

- **Variable cost estimation** based on movement characteristics (distance, weight, car type, train type)
- **Revenue-to-Variable-Cost (R/VC) ratio** calculation, which compares the rate charged to the URCS-estimated variable cost
- **Regulatory threshold:** A movement with an R/VC ratio above 180% is considered "revenue adequate" and may be subject to rate challenge; below 180%, the railroad is presumed to lack market dominance

For bulk commodities, R/VC ratios typically range from 150% to 350%, with captive shippers (those without competitive transportation alternatives) generally paying higher ratios.

### Reciprocal Switching Proposal

In 2023--2024, the STB proposed a final rule on reciprocal switching (also called competitive switching), which would require a railroad serving a shipper's facility to switch traffic to a competing railroad at a regulated rate under defined conditions. Key provisions:

- Applies where a shipper has access to only one Class I railroad (captive shipper)
- The interchange would occur at a nearby junction point
- The switching fee would be set based on STB-determined costs

The rule has faced significant opposition from Class I railroads and remains in a contested regulatory posture. If implemented, it would provide competitive rate relief for captive bulk shippers, potentially reducing rail rates by 10--20% on affected lanes.

## 8.4 EPA Regulations

### Tier 4 Marine Engine Standards

The EPA's emissions standards for marine compression-ignition engines regulate NOx, PM, HC, and CO emissions in tiered phases:

| Tier | Applicability | NOx Reduction vs. Uncontrolled |
|------|--------------|-------------------------------|
| Tier 1 | Engines manufactured 2004+ | ~20% |
| Tier 2 | Engines manufactured 2009+ | ~40% |
| Tier 3 | Engines manufactured 2014+ (Category 1 & 2) | ~60% |
| Tier 4 | Engines manufactured 2017+ (Category 1 & 2) | ~90% |

For inland towboats and barges, the transition to Tier 4 engines has increased capital costs by $500K--$1.5M per vessel for selective catalytic reduction (SCR) and diesel particulate filter (DPF) systems. These costs are being amortized over the fleet as older vessels are repowered or retired.

### Ballast Water Management

The EPA's Vessel General Permit (VGP) and the USCG's ballast water discharge standard (33 CFR 151) require vessels to install ballast water management systems (BWMS) that meet numeric discharge limits. Compliance costs include:

- **BWMS installation:** $1--5M per vessel depending on size and system type
- **Ongoing operating costs:** $50K--150K/year for consumables and maintenance
- **Reporting and record-keeping** obligations under the VGP

### Facility Environmental Compliance

Bulk terminal operators must comply with:

- **Stormwater permits** (NPDES) for runoff containing commodity residue
- **Fugitive dust regulations** under the Clean Air Act, requiring dust suppression systems for open stockpile operations
- **RCRA** requirements for any hazardous material handling
- **Spill Prevention, Control, and Countermeasure (SPCC)** plans for petroleum and chemical bulk facilities

Compliance costs are embedded in terminal operating expenses and passed through to users via wharfage and handling charges.

## 8.5 US Coast Guard (USCG)

### Port State Control

The USCG conducts port state control (PSC) examinations on foreign-flag vessels entering US waters to verify compliance with international conventions (SOLAS, MARPOL, STCW, MLC). Vessels found deficient may be detained until deficiencies are corrected, imposing direct costs (repair, delay) and indirect costs (off-hire, demurrage disputes).

The USCG's targeting matrix prioritizes inspections based on flag state performance, classification society, vessel age, and prior detention history. Bulk carriers are among the most frequently inspected vessel types due to their structural risk profile.

### Vessel Safety

Key USCG vessel safety requirements affecting bulk operations include:

- **Structural surveys** and enhanced survey programs for bulk carriers
- **Cargo securing** requirements and cargo information documentation
- **Navigation safety** regulations in congested waterways (Vessel Traffic Services)
- **Towing vessel inspection regulations** (Subchapter M) for inland towboats, which transitioned from a voluntary to a mandatory inspection regime beginning in 2018

### Maritime Transportation Security Act (MTSA)

MTSA requires:

- **Facility Security Plans (FSP)** for port facilities handling bulk cargo
- **Vessel Security Plans (VSP)** for vessels operating in US waters
- **Transportation Worker Identification Credential (TWIC)** for all personnel requiring unescorted access to secure areas of MTSA-regulated facilities
- **MARSEC level** compliance protocols that can restrict operations during elevated threat conditions

TWIC compliance alone costs approximately $125 per credential (valid 5 years) and imposes administrative burden for workforce management at bulk terminals.

## 8.6 Customs and Border Protection (CBP)

### Customs Clearance

All imported bulk commodities are subject to CBP entry procedures, including:

- **Entry documentation** (CBP Form 7501, commercial invoice, bill of lading)
- **Harmonized Tariff Schedule (HTS) classification** for duty determination
- **Merchandise Processing Fee (MPF):** 0.3464% of declared value, minimum $31.67, maximum $614.35 per entry
- **Harbor Maintenance Fee (HMF):** 0.125% of cargo value for imports
- **Automated Commercial Environment (ACE)** electronic filing requirements

For high-volume bulk imports, the MPF cap of $614.35 per entry means the effective ad valorem rate is negligible, but the HMF at 0.125% of cargo value can be material for high-value commodities.

### UFLPA Forced Labor Compliance

The Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act (UFLPA), effective June 2022, creates a rebuttable presumption that goods mined, produced, or manufactured wholly or in part in the Xinjiang Uyghur Autonomous Region of China are made with forced labor and are prohibited from US importation under 19 USC 1307.

For bulk commodities, UFLPA compliance is relevant for:

- **Silica-based materials** (polysilicon, quartz, silica sand) with Chinese supply chain exposure
- **Cotton** and agricultural products
- **Certain metals and minerals** with Xinjiang processing connections (aluminum, lithium, rare earths)
- **Tomato products** and processed agricultural goods

CBP has issued Withhold Release Orders (WROs) and has detained shipments pending documentation of supply chain provenance. Importers must maintain detailed supply chain mapping and be prepared to provide clear and convincing evidence that goods were not produced with forced labor.

## 8.7 US Army Corps of Engineers (USACE)

### Inland Waterways Trust Fund and Waterway Use Tax

Commercial users of the inland waterway system pay a fuel tax of **$0.29 per gallon** on diesel consumed by vessels operating on designated inland waterways. Revenue is deposited into the Inland Waterways Trust Fund (IWTF), which cost-shares construction of new navigation infrastructure with general Treasury appropriations (typically at a 65% federal / 35% IWTF split following the 2014 WRRDA reforms).

For a typical 15-barge tow consuming approximately 7,000--10,000 gallons per day, the waterway use tax adds approximately $2,000--$2,900 per operating day to barge transportation costs.

### Lock Operations

USACE operates and maintains 236 lock chambers at 193 lock sites on the inland waterway system. Operational considerations include:

- **No direct user fee** for lock transits (funded through fuel tax and federal appropriations)
- **Lock capacity constraints** at aging infrastructure, with average lock age exceeding 60 years
- **Scheduled and unscheduled closures** for maintenance, which can add days to transit times
- **Lockage delays** averaging 1--3 hours at major bottleneck locations (e.g., Lock 25 on the Upper Mississippi, Markland on the Ohio)

### Channel Maintenance Funding

USACE maintains authorized channel depths through dredging programs funded by the Harbor Maintenance Trust Fund (HMTF) for coastal ports and general appropriations for inland channels. Chronic underfunding has resulted in:

- Multiple inland waterway segments operating below authorized depth
- Draft restrictions that reduce barge loading capacity by 10--25% during low-water periods
- Deferred maintenance backlogs estimated at $8--12 billion system-wide

## 8.8 Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA) Impacts

The IIJA (P.L. 117-58, November 2021) authorized and appropriated significant funding for transportation infrastructure relevant to bulk supply chains:

| Program | IIJA Funding | Relevance to Bulk Supply Chain |
|---------|-------------|-------------------------------|
| Inland waterway construction | $2.5B | Lock rehabilitation and replacement |
| Port infrastructure (PIDP grants) | $2.25B | Terminal modernization, intermodal connectors |
| RAISE/INFRA grants | $15B+ | Multimodal freight projects |
| Bridge replacement | $40B | Removal of navigation-restricting bridges |
| Rail crossing elimination | $3B | Grade separation reducing truck/rail conflicts |
| Army Corps construction | $11.6B | Channel deepening, ecosystem restoration |

The IIJA represents the largest single infusion of federal infrastructure investment in decades. For bulk supply chains, the most impactful allocations are the inland waterway lock construction funding and port infrastructure grants, which address long-standing capacity bottlenecks. Detailed project-level analysis is provided in Chapter 10.

## 8.10 Implications for Commodity Analysis

The regulatory environment creates differentiated cost impacts across commodity types, supply chain configurations, and trade lanes. When extending this framework to individual commodity analyses, the following considerations should guide the assessment:

1. **Section 301 sensitivity** varies by commodity depending on the share of Chinese-built vessels in the relevant trade lane fleet and the commodity's price elasticity. Low-value, high-volume bulk commodities (aggregates, coal, grain) face the largest relative impact, as the $16--33/ton fee represents a higher percentage of delivered commodity value.

2. **Jones Act relevance** is commodity-specific based on the extent of domestic waterborne movements in the supply chain. Commodities with significant coastwise or inland waterway legs (petroleum products, construction aggregates, grain) bear the full Jones Act premium, while commodities moving primarily in international trade are affected only at the port-to-destination inland segment.

3. **STB rate oversight** matters most for rail-captive bulk shippers. Commodities with limited modal alternatives (e.g., coal from Powder River Basin, grain from interior elevators served by a single railroad) should be analyzed with explicit R/VC ratio benchmarks.

4. **Environmental compliance costs** are largely embedded in carrier and terminal operating costs but should be tracked as they escalate, particularly for commodities handled at terminals facing stringent air quality or stormwater requirements.

5. **UFLPA exposure** requires commodity-specific supply chain mapping for any commodity with Chinese processing or sourcing connections.

Each subsequent commodity volume will include a dedicated regulatory impact section that applies these factors to the specific supply chain configuration under analysis.
