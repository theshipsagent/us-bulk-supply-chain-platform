# Chapter 6: US Vessel Fleet and Maritime Operations

## Executive Summary

The vessel fleet serving US bulk supply chains operates under a bifurcated regulatory structure that fundamentally shapes logistics costs and capacity constraints. The Jones Act mandates that all waterborne cargo moving between US ports be carried on vessels that are US-built, US-owned, US-flagged, and US-crewed — a requirement that inflates domestic waterborne transport costs by a factor of three to five relative to international benchmarks, while simultaneously limiting available capacity to an aging and numerically constrained fleet. For import-facing supply chains, foreign-flag vessels of varying size classes call at US ports under no such restrictions, but their deployment patterns are governed by global charter markets, port draft limitations, and commodity-specific vessel design requirements. Understanding these two fleets — domestic Jones Act and foreign-flag import — is essential for any commodity-level analysis of landed cost, transit time, and supply chain resilience across the US bulk transportation network.

## 6.1 The Jones Act: Regulatory Framework for Domestic Waterborne Trade

The Merchant Marine Act of 1920, commonly known as the Jones Act (46 U.S.C. § 55102), establishes four requirements for any vessel transporting merchandise between points in the United States:

1. **US-Built**: The vessel must be constructed in a US shipyard. Major repairs and rebuilds must also occur domestically.
2. **US-Owned**: At least 75% of the ownership interest must be held by US citizens or entities.
3. **US-Flagged**: The vessel must be documented under the US flag and subject to US Coast Guard inspection standards.
4. **US-Crewed**: The vessel must be crewed by US citizens or permanent residents, in compliance with US labor and manning regulations.

These requirements apply to all commercial cargo movements between US ports, including coastwise movements between the continental US, Alaska, Hawaii, Puerto Rico, Guam, and the US Virgin Islands. The law does not apply to cargo arriving from or departing to foreign ports, which is why the import fleet operates under entirely different economics.

### Legislative Rationale and Ongoing Debate

The Jones Act was enacted to maintain a merchant marine capable of serving as a naval auxiliary in time of war, to support domestic shipbuilding capacity, and to protect US maritime labor. Critics argue that the Act functions as a protectionist barrier that raises domestic shipping costs, limits fleet renewal, and forces cargo onto less efficient surface transportation modes (truck and rail). Proponents counter that it sustains a strategic shipbuilding industrial base, provides employment for approximately 650,000 workers in the maritime sector, and ensures that domestic waterborne commerce is not dependent on foreign-flag capacity during national emergencies.

Periodic legislative proposals to modify or repeal the Jones Act — particularly the US-build requirement — have been introduced but have not advanced, reflecting the entrenched political coalition of shipbuilders, maritime unions, and national security advocates that supports the current framework.

## 6.2 Domestic Fleet Composition

The Jones Act-qualified fleet is segmented into several major vessel categories, each serving distinct roles within the domestic bulk supply chain.

### 6.2.1 Inland Barges

The inland barge fleet comprises approximately **27,000 active vessels** operating on the inland waterway system (Mississippi River and tributaries, Ohio River, Illinois Waterway, Gulf Intracoastal Waterway, and others). Barges are unpowered and move in multi-unit tows pushed by towboats. The fleet includes:

- **Covered hopper barges**: For grain, fertilizer, and dry bulk commodities requiring weather protection.
- **Open hopper barges**: For coal, aggregates, sand, and other bulk commodities tolerant of open-air transport.
- **Tank barges**: For petroleum products, chemicals, and liquid bulk commodities.
- **Deck barges**: For project cargo, oversized equipment, and construction materials.

A standard inland barge measures approximately 195 feet in length and 35 feet in beam, carrying roughly 1,500 tons of cargo at a 9-foot draft. A typical 15-barge tow on the Lower Mississippi moves approximately 22,500 tons — the equivalent of 225 rail cars or 900 truck loads — making barge transport the most fuel-efficient and cost-effective mode for bulk commodity movements along navigable waterways.

### 6.2.2 Coastal Tankers

Approximately **95 coastal tankers** operate under Jones Act qualification, transporting petroleum products, crude oil, and chemicals between US ports. This fleet serves critical supply chains including:

- Refined product movements from Gulf Coast refineries to East Coast demand centers.
- Crude oil movements from Gulf and West Coast terminals to refinery complexes.
- Chemical and petrochemical feedstock distribution along the Gulf Coast.

The coastal tanker fleet has experienced significant renewal in recent years driven by the OPA 90 (Oil Pollution Act of 1990) mandate for double-hull construction, which phased out all single-hull tank vessels from US waters.

### 6.2.3 Container Ships

Approximately **65 container ships** operate under Jones Act qualification, serving the non-contiguous trades:

- **Mainland–Hawaii**: The largest Jones Act container trade by volume.
- **Mainland–Alaska**: Serving consumer goods and supply chain requirements for the state.
- **Mainland–Puerto Rico**: A significant trade lane for consumer products and manufacturing inputs.
- **Mainland–Guam**: A smaller but strategically important trade.

Major operators in this segment include Matson Navigation, Pasha Hawaii, and TOTE Maritime. These vessels are generally smaller than their international counterparts, reflecting the trade lane volumes and port constraints of the non-contiguous routes.

### 6.2.4 Ocean-Going Bulk Carriers

Only approximately **5 ocean-going dry bulk carriers** currently operate under Jones Act qualification. This extremely limited fleet reflects the prohibitive economics of building large bulk carriers in US shipyards at three to five times the cost of equivalent vessels from Asian yards. The few remaining vessels serve niche trades, primarily transporting coal and aggregates in coastwise service.

### 6.2.5 Tugs and Towboats

Approximately **5,200 tugs and towboats** operate under Jones Act qualification, performing essential functions across the domestic maritime network:

- **Line-haul towboats**: Pushing barge tows on the inland waterway system (typically 2,000–10,500 HP).
- **Harbor tugs**: Assisting vessel docking and undocking at ports (typically 3,000–6,000 HP).
- **Offshore tugs**: Supporting coastwise barge tows and offshore operations.
- **Articulated tug-barge (ATB) units**: A growing segment combining a dedicated tug with a notched barge for coastwise liquid and dry bulk transport, offering Jones Act-compliant capacity at lower cost than conventional self-propelled vessels.

## 6.3 The Jones Act Cost Premium

The economic impact of Jones Act requirements manifests primarily through two cost drivers: vessel construction costs and crew costs.

### 6.3.1 Construction Cost Differential

US shipyard construction costs are estimated at **3–5x the cost of equivalent vessels built in foreign yards** (primarily South Korea, China, and Japan for large commercial vessels). This premium reflects:

| Cost Factor | US Shipyard | Foreign Yard (Asia) |
|---|---|---|
| Labor cost per hour | $35–65 | $8–20 |
| Throughput efficiency | Lower | Higher |
| Supply chain for steel and components | Domestic sourcing requirements | Global sourcing |
| Yard capacity and specialization | Limited, general-purpose | Large-scale, vessel-type-specialized |
| Regulatory compliance burden | US environmental, labor, safety | Local standards |

A Handysize bulk carrier (30,000 DWT) that might cost $25–30 million at an Asian yard could cost $80–150 million at a US shipyard — assuming a US yard with the capacity and willingness to bid on the project exists. This cost differential has effectively eliminated new-build Jones Act ocean-going bulk carriers from commercial viability, explaining the fleet's contraction to approximately 5 vessels.

### 6.3.2 Crew Cost Differential

US crew costs are estimated at **3–4x the cost of foreign crews** on equivalent vessels. This premium reflects:

- Higher base wages mandated by US labor law and collective bargaining agreements.
- US benefits packages (health insurance, retirement, leave provisions).
- US Coast Guard manning requirements that may exceed international (STCW) minimums.
- Training and certification costs under the US system.

A Jones Act vessel operating with a crew of 20–25 may incur annual crew costs of $4–6 million, compared to $1–2 million for a comparable foreign-flag vessel crewed under international labor standards.

### 6.3.3 Net Impact on Domestic Freight Rates

The combined construction and crewing premium translates directly into higher daily charter rates and per-ton freight rates for Jones Act vessels relative to international benchmarks. For commodities where waterborne transport competes with rail or truck, this premium can divert cargo to surface modes, particularly for shorter-distance movements where the inherent fuel efficiency advantage of water transport is insufficient to overcome the Jones Act cost burden.

## 6.4 Foreign-Flag Import Fleet

Vessels calling at US ports to discharge imported bulk commodities operate under foreign flags and are not subject to Jones Act requirements. These vessels are built, owned, and crewed internationally, with freight rates set by global charter markets (Baltic Exchange indices).

### 6.4.1 Bulk Carrier Size Classes

The following size classes are most relevant to US bulk import operations:

| Vessel Class | Typical DWT Range | LOA (m) | Beam (m) | Laden Draft (m) | Cargo Capacity (MT) | Primary Commodities |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Capesize | 150,000–210,000 | 280–300 | 43–50 | 17.0–18.5 | 150,000–200,000 | Iron ore, coal |
| Panamax | 65,000–85,000 | 225–245 | 32.2 | 13.5–14.5 | 65,000–82,000 | Grain, coal, minerals |
| Supramax | 50,000–65,000 | 190–200 | 32.2 | 12.0–13.5 | 50,000–62,000 | Minerals, steel, fertilizer |
| Handysize | 25,000–40,000 | 150–180 | 27–32 | 9.5–11.5 | 25,000–38,000 | Steel, forest products, minerals |
| Handymax | 40,000–50,000 | 180–190 | 32 | 11.5–12.5 | 40,000–50,000 | Grain, minerals, fertilizer |
| Mini-Bulk | 5,000–15,000 | 100–140 | 18–22 | 6.0–8.0 | 5,000–14,000 | Specialty minerals, breakbulk |

**Table 6.1**: Bulk carrier size classifications relevant to US port operations.

For US bulk imports, **Supramax, Panamax, and Handysize** classes dominate trade flows. Capesize vessels are limited to deep-draft ports (Hampton Roads, New Orleans, Mobile) due to their laden draft requirements. The New Panamax (Neopanamax) class, enabled by the expanded Panama Canal locks (completed 2016), allows vessels up to approximately 120,000 DWT to transit the canal, expanding the range of vessel sizes accessible to US Gulf and East Coast ports via the Pacific trade.

### 6.4.2 Other Vessel Types Calling US Ports

Beyond dry bulk carriers, US ports receive:

- **Product tankers and crude carriers**: Ranging from Medium Range (MR, ~45,000 DWT) to Very Large Crude Carriers (VLCC, ~300,000 DWT) at the Louisiana Offshore Oil Port (LOOP).
- **Container ships**: From feeder vessels (~1,000 TEU) to Ultra Large Container Vessels (ULCV, 24,000+ TEU) at major gateway ports.
- **Ro-Ro vessels**: For automobiles, heavy equipment, and rolling stock.
- **LNG/LPG carriers**: At dedicated gas terminals along the Gulf Coast and East Coast.
- **General cargo/multipurpose vessels**: For project cargo, breakbulk, and neo-bulk commodities.

## 6.5 Vessel Classification: Panjiva Pipeline Methodology

Accurate identification and classification of vessels within trade flow data is essential for commodity-level supply chain analysis. The project's Panjiva data pipeline employs a **7-phase classification methodology** to process and enrich vessel records across 800,000+ bill of lading records.

### 6.5.1 Classification Phases

| Phase | Process | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Phase 1 | Raw data ingestion and field standardization | Normalize vessel name, IMO number, carrier SCAC codes across source records |
| Phase 2 | White noise filtering | Remove non-cargo records (ballast movements, repositioning, ship stores) |
| Phase 3 | Carrier exclusion rules | Identify and flag known container carriers vs. bulk/breakbulk operators |
| Phase 4 | Vessel name resolution and IMO matching | Resolve variant spellings, abbreviations, and errors to canonical vessel identities |
| Phase 5 | Vessel type assignment | Classify each vessel by type (bulk carrier, tanker, container, general cargo, etc.) using registry data |
| Phase 6 | DWT and dimensional enrichment | Append deadweight tonnage, LOA, beam, and draft from ship registry databases |
| Phase 7 | Fleet segmentation and trade lane assignment | Assign vessels to trade lane and fleet segments for analytical aggregation |

**Table 6.2**: Seven-phase vessel classification pipeline.

### 6.5.2 Processing Scale

The pipeline has processed over **800,000 bill of lading records**, resolving vessel identities across:

- Multiple transliterations of vessel names (e.g., Cyrillic-to-Latin conversion for Russian-flag vessels).
- Name changes following vessel sales and reflagging.
- Duplicate and erroneous IMO entries in source data.
- Carrier-assigned voyage identifiers that do not correspond to standard vessel registries.

## 6.6 Ship Registry Database

The project maintains a ship registry database containing **52,035 vessels** with the following core attributes:

| Attribute | Description | Coverage |
|---|---|---|
| IMO Number | Unique 7-digit International Maritime Organization identifier | 100% of ocean-going vessels |
| Vessel Name | Current registered name | 100% |
| Vessel Type | Classification (bulk carrier, tanker, container, general cargo, etc.) | 100% |
| DWT (Deadweight Tonnage) | Maximum cargo + fuel + stores capacity in metric tons | 98% |
| LOA (Length Overall) | Total vessel length in meters | 95% |
| Beam | Maximum vessel width in meters | 95% |
| Maximum Draft | Deepest laden waterline in meters | 93% |
| Flag State | Country of registration | 100% |
| Year Built | Construction completion year | 97% |
| Builder | Shipyard of construction | 90% |
| Gross Tonnage (GT) | Volumetric measure of enclosed spaces | 99% |

**Table 6.3**: Ship registry database attributes and coverage.

This registry enables the enrichment of bill of lading records with physical vessel characteristics, supporting analyses such as:

- Estimating cargo parcel sizes from vessel DWT and draft observations.
- Identifying port accessibility constraints based on vessel draft vs. channel depth.
- Tracking fleet age and renewal patterns by vessel type and trade lane.
- Cross-referencing vessel movements with AIS (Automatic Identification System) data for transit time analysis.

## 6.7 Vessel Intelligence Toolset

The project's vessel intelligence capabilities extend beyond static registry data to include active analytical tools:

### 6.7.1 Entity Resolution

Vessel entity resolution addresses the challenge of linking disparate references to the same physical vessel across multiple data sources. The toolset employs:

- **IMO-based deterministic matching**: Where IMO numbers are present and valid, exact matching provides high-confidence linkage.
- **Fuzzy name matching**: For records lacking IMO numbers, probabilistic matching algorithms (Levenshtein distance, Jaro-Winkler similarity, token-based matching) resolve vessel identity from name strings.
- **Contextual disambiguation**: Vessel type, DWT range, flag state, and trade lane history provide additional signals to resolve ambiguous matches (e.g., multiple vessels sharing a common name).

### 6.7.2 HS Code Classification

The toolset supports classification of cargo descriptions from bill of lading records to Harmonized System (HS) codes at the 4-digit and 6-digit levels. This capability enables:

- Commodity-level aggregation of trade flows that arrive as free-text cargo descriptions.
- Alignment of customs data (which uses HS codes natively) with shipping data (which often uses narrative descriptions).
- Identification of misclassified or ambiguously described cargoes through cross-referencing vessel type, trade lane, and shipper identity.

### 6.7.3 Carrier and Operator Identification

The toolset resolves carrier identities from SCAC codes, bill of lading prefixes, and vessel operator databases, enabling analysis of:

- Market share by carrier within commodity-specific trade lanes.
- Service patterns and schedule reliability for liner services.
- Chartering patterns for tramp (spot) bulk and tanker movements.

## 6.8 Military Sealift Command and Strategic Reserve Fleet

### 6.8.1 Military Sealift Command (MSC)

The Military Sealift Command operates approximately **125 civilian-crewed vessels** that provide ocean transportation, fleet support, and special mission capabilities for the Department of Defense. MSC vessels are US government-owned or chartered and are crewed by civil service mariners and/or contract mariners. The MSC fleet includes:

- **Combat logistics force ships**: Replenishment oilers, ammunition ships, and combat stores ships supporting deployed naval forces.
- **Sealift ships**: Large, medium-speed, roll-on/roll-off (LMSR) vessels and other cargo ships for military equipment and supply transport.
- **Prepositioning ships**: Forward-deployed vessels carrying military equipment and supplies at strategic locations worldwide.
- **Special mission ships**: Oceanographic survey vessels, submarine support ships, and missile range instrumentation ships.

### 6.8.2 National Defense Reserve Fleet (NDRF)

The Maritime Administration (MARAD) maintains the **National Defense Reserve Fleet** at anchorages in James River (Virginia), Beaumont (Texas), and Suisun Bay (California). This fleet comprises approximately 45 vessels in varying states of readiness:

- **Ready Reserve Force (RRF)**: Approximately 40 vessels maintained in 5- to 20-day readiness status for activation in support of military sealift requirements.
- **Non-retention vessels**: Older vessels awaiting disposal through scrapping or artificial reef programs.

### 6.8.3 Strategic Implications

The MSC fleet and NDRF represent the nation's surge sealift capacity for military contingencies. However, the commercial Jones Act fleet also serves as a strategic reserve in the broader sense — the US-citizen mariners who crew Jones Act vessels constitute a pool of qualified seafarers available for military sealift activation. The declining size of the Jones Act fleet has raised concerns about the adequacy of this mariner pool, with industry estimates suggesting that the current pool of approximately 11,500 qualified oceangoing mariners may be insufficient to sustain both commercial operations and a major military sealift mobilization simultaneously.

## 6.10 Implications for Commodity Analysis

The vessel fleet structure creates several analytical imperatives for any commodity-specific supply chain study built on this framework:

1. **Domestic vs. import cost asymmetry**: The Jones Act premium means that domestic waterborne redistribution of imported commodities (e.g., transferring imported steel from a Gulf Coast port to an East Coast destination via coastwise shipping) is significantly more expensive than the original ocean freight from a foreign origin. Commodity analysts must account for this cost asymmetry when modeling total landed cost at inland or secondary-port destinations.

2. **Port-vessel compatibility constraints**: Not all US ports can accommodate all vessel size classes. Channel depth, air draft (bridge clearance), berth length, and cargo handling equipment determine which vessels can call at which ports. Commodity flows are constrained by these physical matchups — a Capesize iron ore carrier cannot call at most US ports, concentrating iron ore imports at a handful of deep-draft facilities.

3. **Vessel type as commodity signal**: In the Panjiva classification pipeline, vessel type serves as a powerful signal for commodity identification. A Supramax bulk carrier discharging at a port with bulk handling infrastructure is almost certainly carrying dry bulk cargo, even if the bill of lading description is ambiguous. This vessel-commodity correlation is a core element of the classification methodology.

4. **Fleet capacity as supply constraint**: The limited number of Jones Act ocean-going bulk carriers (approximately 5) means that domestic coastwise bulk movements are effectively capacity-constrained. For commodities where coastwise shipping would be the lowest-cost mode, this constraint forces cargo onto rail or truck, altering the competitive dynamics of the supply chain.

5. **Charter rate volatility**: Import commodities transported on foreign-flag vessels are subject to global charter rate volatility (Baltic Dry Index for bulk, Worldscale for tankers). Commodity supply chain cost models must incorporate freight rate variability as a significant component of total landed cost, particularly for low-value, high-volume commodities where freight represents a large share of delivered price.

6. **Strategic and regulatory risk**: The MSC fleet requirements, potential Jones Act modifications, and evolving environmental regulations (IMO 2020 sulfur cap, potential carbon levies) all represent risk factors that can alter vessel availability, fleet economics, and trade flow patterns for bulk commodities over the medium to long term.
