# Chapter 2: US Inland Waterway System

## Executive Summary

The US inland waterway system comprises approximately 25,000 miles of navigable channels serving as the lowest-cost mode of freight transportation for bulk commodities, handling over 600 million short tons of cargo annually across interconnected river systems, canals, and intracoastal waterways. The network is anchored by the Mississippi River System—itself exceeding 12,000 miles of commercially navigable waterway—and is maintained by the US Army Corps of Engineers (USACE) through 239 lock chambers at 192 sites, more than half of which have exceeded their 50-year engineered design life. While the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA) of 2021 authorized $2.5 billion for inland waterway construction, the system faces persistent capacity constraints at aging lock sites that impose significant delay costs on shippers and ripple through bulk commodity supply chains nationwide.

---

## 2.1 System Overview and Major Waterway Corridors

The US inland waterway system is organized around several principal corridors, each serving distinct commodity flows and geographic markets. The following sections describe the major waterways in terms of their physical characteristics, traffic volumes, and strategic importance to bulk freight logistics.

### 2.1.1 Mississippi River System

The Mississippi River System is the backbone of inland waterway transportation in the United States, encompassing the Upper Mississippi River (UMR), the Lower Mississippi River (LMR), and major tributaries including the Ohio, Missouri, Illinois, Arkansas, and Tennessee Rivers. In aggregate, the system provides over 12,000 miles of commercially navigable waterway connecting the agricultural heartland, industrial Midwest, and energy-producing regions to deepwater port facilities on the US Gulf Coast (USACE, Waterborne Commerce Statistics Center [WCSC], 2023).

**Lower Mississippi River (LMR).** The segment from Cairo, Illinois, to the Gulf of Mexico—approximately 954 statute miles—is an open-river reach requiring no locks and maintaining a minimum 45-foot channel depth from Baton Rouge to the Gulf (12-foot authorized channel depth above Baton Rouge). The LMR handles the highest tonnage of any single waterway segment in the nation, with USACE WCSC data reporting over 500 million short tons annually in recent years, driven by petroleum products, grain, coal, chemicals, and construction aggregates (USACE WCSC, Waterborne Commerce of the United States [WCUS], Part 2, 2022).

**Upper Mississippi River (UMR).** Extending approximately 858 statute miles from Minneapolis, Minnesota, to the confluence with the Ohio River at Cairo, Illinois, the UMR is controlled by a series of 29 lock and dam structures maintaining a 9-foot navigation channel. Annual tonnage on the UMR ranges from 80 to 100 million short tons, with grain (principally corn and soybeans) comprising the dominant commodity class (USACE WCSC, WCUS Part 2, 2022).

### 2.1.2 Ohio River System

The Ohio River extends approximately 981 statute miles from Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania, to its confluence with the Mississippi at Cairo, Illinois, and is the most heavily trafficked tributary in the system by tonnage. Navigation is maintained through 20 locks and dams operated by USACE. The Ohio River and its principal tributaries—the Monongahela, Allegheny, Kanawha, and Tennessee Rivers—collectively handle approximately 220 to 250 million short tons annually, with coal, aggregates, petroleum products, and chemicals as leading commodity classes (USACE WCSC, WCUS Part 2, 2022; USACE Navigation Data Center [NDC], Lock Performance Monitoring System [LPMS], 2023).

### 2.1.3 Illinois Waterway

The Illinois Waterway connects the Great Lakes (via the Chicago Sanitary and Ship Canal) to the Mississippi River at Grafton, Illinois, spanning approximately 327 statute miles through eight locks. It serves as a critical link for grain exports moving from the Midwest to Gulf export elevators. Annual tonnage ranges from 40 to 55 million short tons, with corn and soybeans comprising a dominant share during harvest season (August through November). The LaGrange Lock on this system is a frequently cited bottleneck (USACE WCSC, WCUS Part 2, 2022).

### 2.1.4 Gulf Intracoastal Waterway (GIWW)

The Gulf Intracoastal Waterway extends approximately 1,100 statute miles along the Gulf Coast from St. Marks, Florida, to Brownsville, Texas, providing a protected, shallow-draft channel (12-foot authorized depth) for barge traffic. The GIWW is critical for petrochemical and petroleum product movements, connecting refinery and chemical plant complexes along the Texas and Louisiana coasts. Annual tonnage on the GIWW exceeds 110 million short tons (USACE WCSC, WCUS Part 5, 2022).

### 2.1.5 Columbia-Snake River System

The Columbia-Snake River System in the Pacific Northwest provides approximately 465 statute miles of navigable waterway from the Pacific Ocean to Lewiston, Idaho, controlled by eight locks (four on the Lower Snake River, four on the Lower Columbia). The system handles approximately 40 to 55 million short tons annually, with wheat and other agricultural products, petroleum, and wood products as principal commodity classes. The four Lower Snake River dams have been the subject of ongoing policy debate regarding dam removal for salmon recovery, introducing long-term uncertainty into this corridor (USACE, Northwestern Division, 2023).

## 2.2 Lock Infrastructure: Condition, Capacity, and Age Profile

### 2.2.1 System Inventory

The USACE operates and maintains 239 lock chambers at 192 sites across the inland waterway system. These structures range from small, single-chamber recreational locks to high-volume commercial navigation locks handling thousands of lockages per year. The majority of commercial lock chambers are 600 feet or 1,200 feet in length, with 110-foot widths as the prevailing standard. A standard 15-barge tow on the Mississippi or Ohio River system is configured to fit a 1,200-foot chamber in a single lockage; at 600-foot chambers, tows must be broken into two cuts, effectively doubling transit time (USACE, Institute for Water Resources [IWR], Inland Navigation Condition Report, 2022).

### 2.2.2 Age and Structural Condition

The age profile of US lock infrastructure presents one of the most significant risk factors in the bulk supply chain. As of 2023, **54% of lock chambers have exceeded their original 50-year engineered design life**, with some structures dating to the 1930s. The median age of commercially significant lock chambers is approximately 60 years. The aging infrastructure manifests in increased rates of unscheduled closures, longer maintenance outages, and reduced reliability (USACE IWR, Inland Navigation Condition Report, 2022; Congressional Research Service [CRS], Inland Waterways: Recent Proposals and Issues for Congress, R45430, updated 2023).

The USACE employs a Facilities Condition Index (FCI) and a Lock Reliability Index to assess structural and mechanical condition. Many high-volume lock sites have been rated as having "significant" or "critical" maintenance needs in USACE Capital Investment Strategy (CIS) assessments.

### 2.2.3 Design Life Exceedance and Risk Implications

Lock chambers beyond their 50-year design life are subject to increasing rates of unscheduled mechanical and structural failures, including miter gate deformation, valve system failures, concrete deterioration, and electrical/control system obsolescence. The USACE has documented a correlation between lock age and the frequency of unscheduled outages, which impose direct delay costs on barge operators and indirect supply chain disruption costs on shippers and receivers (USACE, Inland Marine Transportation Systems [IMTS] Capital Investment Strategy Report, 2020).

## 2.3 Waterway Tonnage and Traffic Patterns

### 2.3.1 USACE Waterborne Commerce Statistics

The USACE Waterborne Commerce Statistics Center (WCSC), located in New Orleans, Louisiana, is the authoritative source for US domestic and foreign waterborne commerce data. The WCSC publishes the annual *Waterborne Commerce of the United States* (WCUS) report series, organized into five parts by geographic region. Supplementary traffic data is available through the Maritime River Information Services (MRTIS) system, which provides near-real-time vessel tracking and lock transit data.

Key tonnage figures for the inland waterway system include:

- **Total inland waterway system:** Approximately 600 to 640 million short tons annually (all commodity classes, all waterway segments).
- **Mississippi River System (all segments):** Over 500 million short tons annually, representing approximately 80% of total inland waterway tonnage.
- **Ohio River mainstem:** 220 to 250 million short tons annually.
- **Illinois Waterway:** 40 to 55 million short tons annually.
- **GIWW:** 110 to 120 million short tons annually.

*(Sources: USACE WCSC, WCUS Parts 1–5, 2022; USACE NDC, Tonnage for Selected US Ports and Waterways, 2023.)*

### 2.3.2 Commodity Mix

The inland waterway system handles a diverse commodity mix, though a small number of commodity classes account for the majority of tonnage. Based on USACE WCSC data (WCUS, 2022), the approximate commodity distribution is:

### 2.3.3 Seasonal Traffic Patterns

Inland waterway traffic exhibits pronounced seasonal variation driven by agricultural harvest cycles, heating fuel demand, and construction activity:

- **Grain season (September–December):** Corn and soybean harvest drives a 30–50% surge in downbound traffic on the UMR and Illinois Waterway. Lock delays at UMR Lock 27 and LaGrange Lock on the Illinois Waterway peak during this period.
- **Coal movements (October–March):** Utility coal movements increase in the fall and winter heating season, particularly on the Ohio River system.
- **Construction aggregates (April–October):** Sand, gravel, and crushed stone shipments peak during the construction season, concentrated on the Lower Ohio and LMR.
- **Low-water season (August–November):** The LMR periodically experiences low-water conditions that restrict channel depth and tow sizes, particularly in the Memphis to Vicksburg reach. In severe drought years (e.g., 2022), draft restrictions have limited tow loadings by 20–30%, effectively increasing per-ton transportation costs.

## 2.4 Lock Performance Metrics

### 2.4.1 NDC Lock Performance Monitoring System (LPMS)

The USACE Navigation Data Center (NDC) maintains the Lock Performance Monitoring System (LPMS), which records detailed operational data for every lockage at every USACE-operated lock site. LPMS data includes:

- **Processing time:** The time from a vessel's arrival at the lock to departure from the chamber (includes approach, entry, chamber filling/emptying, and exit).
- **Delay time:** The time a vessel waits in queue before beginning the lockage process.
- **Lockage count:** The number of individual lockages (chamber cycles) per day, month, and year.
- **Tow cuts:** The number of cuts required when a tow exceeds chamber capacity, requiring multiple lockages for a single tow passage.

### 2.4.2 Average Delay Times at High-Volume Locks

Delay times at inland waterway locks vary enormously by site, season, and direction of travel. At well-maintained, high-capacity (1,200-foot) locks with moderate traffic, average delays may be under one hour. At congested, undersized (600-foot), or aging locks, average delays routinely exceed 4 to 8 hours, with peak-season delays exceeding 24 hours at the most constrained sites.

### 2.4.3 Cost of Lock Delays

The economic cost of lock delays is borne by barge operators (in the form of increased fuel consumption and crew costs) and ultimately passed through to shippers. The USACE and the Transportation Research Board (TRB) have estimated the aggregate annual cost of lock delays on the inland waterway system at $400 million to $700 million, depending on methodology and traffic levels. At individual lock sites, delay costs can be estimated using the USACE's Tow Cost Model, which applies vessel operating cost rates to recorded delay hours (USACE IWR, Economic Impact of Inland Waterway Lock Delays, 2021; TRB, Special Report 315, 2015).

## 2.5 Barge Fleet Characteristics

### 2.5.1 Fleet Inventory

The US inland barge fleet comprises approximately **27,000 cargo-carrying barges** and **5,200 towboats (pushboats)**, according to data from the USACE, Waterways Council, Inc. (WCI), and the American Waterways Operators (AWO). The fleet is predominantly privately owned, with major operators including Ingram Barge Company, American Commercial Barge Line (ACBL), Kirby Corporation, and Canal Barge Company.

### 2.5.2 Barge Types and Capacities

Inland barges are purpose-built, unpowered vessels designed for specific commodity types:

### Table 2.5: Standard Inland Barge Types and Capacities

| Barge Type | Typical Dimensions (L x W, feet) | Capacity (short tons) | Draft Loaded (feet) | Primary Commodities |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Open hopper barge | 195 x 35 | 1,500–1,750 | 9.0 | Coal, aggregates, ores, scrap |
| Covered hopper barge | 195 x 35 | 1,500–1,750 | 9.0 | Grain, fertilizer, cement, minerals |
| Tank barge | 195–297 x 35–54 | 1,500–3,000 | 9.0 | Petroleum products, chemicals, liquid fertilizer |
| Deck barge | 195 x 35 | 1,200–1,500 | Varies | Steel, project cargo, equipment |
| Open hopper jumbo barge | 200 x 35 | 1,750–2,000 | 9.0 | Coal, aggregates |

*Source: AWO, American Inland Waterways Industry Facts, 2023; WCI, Value to the Nation, 2023.*

### 2.5.3 Typical Tow Configurations

Towboat-and-barge configurations ("tows") vary by waterway segment, lock dimensions, and channel conditions:

- **Lower Mississippi River (open river):** Tows of 30 to 40 barges are common, pushed by line-haul towboats of 4,000 to 10,500 horsepower. A 30-barge tow of covered hopper barges carries approximately 52,500 short tons—the cargo equivalent of 2,100 trucks or 525 rail cars.
- **Upper Mississippi River (9-foot channel, 1,200-foot locks):** Tows typically comprise 15 barges (3 wide x 5 long), requiring a single cut at 1,200-foot locks.
- **Upper Mississippi River (600-foot locks):** Tows of 15 barges must be split into two cuts, with each half locked through separately.
- **Ohio River:** 15-barge tows are standard on the mainstem; smaller configurations (6–9 barges) are used on tributaries.
- **Illinois Waterway:** Tow sizes range from 9 to 15 barges, depending on lock dimensions and bridge clearances.
- **GIWW:** Tow sizes are limited to 2–4 barges abreast, with total tow lengths constrained by lock and bridge restrictions.

### 2.5.4 Fleet Age and Renewal

The average age of the inland barge fleet is approximately 18 to 22 years, with significant variation by barge type. The hopper barge fleet has undergone gradual renewal, with new-build activity concentrated among the larger operators. Tank barges are subject to additional regulatory requirements (USCG Subchapter D and O) that have driven accelerated replacement of older single-hull vessels. Towboat construction has been relatively limited in recent years, contributing to an aging towing fleet with an average age exceeding 25 years (Informa Economics / IHS Markit, Inland Barge Fleet Profile, 2022).

## 2.6 Infrastructure Investment: IIJA and the Inland Waterways Trust Fund

### 2.6.1 Inland Waterways Trust Fund (IWTF)

The Inland Waterways Trust Fund, established by the Inland Waterways Revenue Act of 1978, is funded by a $0.29 per gallon fuel tax on commercial vessels operating on designated inland waterways. Historically, the IWTF has provided a 50% cost share for inland waterway construction projects, with the remaining 50% appropriated from general Treasury revenues. The IWTF has been chronically underfunded relative to the capital investment needs of the system, with annual revenues of approximately $100 to $120 million against a multi-billion-dollar backlog of authorized construction projects (CRS, Inland Waterways Trust Fund, IF11132, updated 2023).

### 2.6.2 IIJA Funding for Inland Waterways

The Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA), enacted in November 2021, authorized **$2.5 billion in direct appropriations** for inland waterway construction, separate from and in addition to the IWTF cost-share mechanism. This represented the largest single federal investment in inland waterway infrastructure in decades. Priority projects receiving IIJA funding include:

### 2.6.3 Remaining Capital Backlog

Despite the IIJA investment, the USACE's estimated capital backlog for inland waterway navigation infrastructure remains in excess of $8 to $12 billion, reflecting decades of deferred maintenance and delayed construction. The backlog includes major lock replacement projects on the UMR (Navigation and Ecosystem Sustainability Program, or NESP), additional Ohio River lock rehabilitations, and GIWW channel improvements. The 50-year design life exceedance rate is projected to increase to over 70% by 2040 absent sustained investment (USACE IWR, IMTS Capital Investment Strategy, 2020; WCI, Capital Spending Plan, 2023).

## 2.7 Key Bottlenecks and Constraint Points

### 2.7.1 Lock 27 (Chain of Rocks), Upper Mississippi River

Lock 27, located near Granite City, Illinois, is the last lock on the Upper Mississippi River before the open-river LMR reach and handles the convergence of UMR and Missouri River traffic. Despite having a 1,200-foot chamber, Lock 27 is one of the highest-volume locks in the system, with over 10,500 commercial lockages annually. Average delays of 3.5 to 5.0 hours and peak-season delays exceeding 12 hours make it a persistent bottleneck for grain exports and other bulk commodities moving to Gulf ports (USACE NDC, LPMS, 2022).

### 2.7.2 LaGrange Lock, Illinois Waterway

LaGrange Lock, approximately 80 statute miles above the mouth of the Illinois River, has a 600-foot chamber that requires 15-barge tows to make two cuts. During the grain export season (September–December), delays at LaGrange routinely exceed 5 hours and can reach 15 to 20 hours during peak weeks, creating a chokepoint for corn and soybean exports bound for Gulf elevators. The IIJA-funded major rehabilitation is intended to improve reliability but does not expand chamber capacity (USACE NDC, LPMS, 2022; CRS R45430, 2023).

### 2.7.3 Chickamauga Lock, Tennessee River

Chickamauga Lock, located near Chattanooga, Tennessee, is the most severely constrained commercial navigation lock in the USACE system. Its 360-foot by 60-foot chamber—built in 1940—is the smallest lock on the Tennessee River and requires large tows to make three or more cuts, with average processing times of 2 to 3.5 hours and average delays of 5 to 10 hours. The lock has experienced structural cracking and reliability issues that have prompted emergency repairs and periodic closures. The IIJA-funded replacement (a new 600-foot by 110-foot chamber) is under construction, with completion expected in the late 2020s (USACE Nashville District, Chickamauga Lock Replacement Project Fact Sheet, 2023).

### 2.7.4 Other Constraint Points

Additional bottleneck locations include:

- **Lock 25 (Upper Mississippi River):** A 600-foot chamber handling heavy grain traffic, with average delays of 4 to 7 hours during harvest season.
- **Inner Harbor Navigation Canal (IHNC) Lock (New Orleans):** Connects the Mississippi River to the GIWW and the Mississippi River-Gulf Outlet; subject to high traffic volumes and scheduled closures for bridge operations.
- **Bayou Sorrel Lock (GIWW, Louisiana):** Connects the GIWW to the Atchafalaya River; subject to congestion during high-water events.
- **Lower Snake River Locks:** Subject to ongoing policy uncertainty regarding potential dam removal, which would eliminate navigation above the lowest lock.

## 2.8 Modal Cost Comparison: Inland Waterway vs. Rail and Truck

The cost advantage of inland waterway transportation relative to rail and truck modes is the fundamental economic driver of barge traffic. While precise cost comparisons are commodity- and lane-specific, general benchmarks are widely cited:

### Table 2.7: Approximate Freight Cost by Mode (Bulk Commodities)

| Transport Mode | Cost per Ton-Mile (US cents) | Cargo Capacity per Unit | Fuel Efficiency (ton-miles per gallon) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Inland barge (15-barge tow) | 0.5–1.5 | 22,500–26,250 short tons | 514–576 |
| Rail (100-car unit train) | 2.0–4.0 | 10,000–12,000 short tons | 470–500 |
| Truck (single tractor-trailer) | 8.0–20.0 | 25 short tons | 130–150 |

*Sources: Texas Transportation Institute (TTI), A Modal Comparison of Domestic Freight Transportation Effects on the General Public, 2023 update; USACE IWR, Inland Waterway Transportation Value to the Nation, 2020; AWO fact sheets.*

The cost advantage of barge transportation is most pronounced for low-value, high-bulk commodities (coal, aggregates, grain, fertilizer) over distances exceeding 250 statute miles, where the fixed costs of terminal handling and barge loading/unloading are amortized over sufficient volume.

## 2.9 Implications for Commodity Analysis

The inland waterway system's infrastructure condition, capacity constraints, and investment trajectory have direct and material implications for commodity-specific supply chain analysis:

1. **Commodity routing decisions are lock-dependent.** For any bulk commodity moving by barge, the specific lock sites on the route—and their current and projected performance characteristics—determine transit time variability, which directly affects inventory carrying costs and delivery reliability. Commodity analysts must identify the specific locks on each barge route and assess current delay profiles using USACE NDC/LPMS data.

2. **Seasonal congestion patterns interact with commodity cycles.** The grain export season (September–December) produces predictable congestion peaks on the UMR and Illinois Waterway that affect not only grain shippers but all commodities sharing those waterways. Coal, cement, fertilizer, and chemical shipments on the same corridors experience increased transit time variability during these periods.

3. **Infrastructure investment timelines affect long-term supply chain planning.** The IIJA-funded projects (Chickamauga, Kentucky Lock, Montgomery Lock) will improve capacity over a 5- to 15-year horizon, but the remaining capital backlog means that many constraint points will persist or worsen. Commodity supply chain models should incorporate scenario analysis for both improved and degraded lock performance at key sites.

4. **Low-water risk on the LMR is a systemic supply chain risk.** The 2022 drought demonstrated that extreme low-water events on the Lower Mississippi River can reduce barge loadings by 20–30%, effectively increasing per-ton transport costs and reducing throughput at Gulf export terminals. Commodity analyses should incorporate hydrological risk, particularly for grain, coal, and petroleum products.

5. **Barge fleet capacity is finite and cyclically constrained.** With approximately 27,000 barges and 5,200 towboats, the fleet operates near capacity during peak seasons. Commodity demand surges (e.g., a large grain harvest coinciding with high coal demand) can tighten barge availability and increase spot freight rates by 30–50% or more. Fleet utilization rates and barge-building trends should be monitored as leading indicators of freight cost pressure.

6. **Modal diversion thresholds are commodity-specific.** When barge delays or rate premiums exceed commodity-specific thresholds, shippers divert to rail or, less commonly, truck. Understanding these diversion thresholds—and the rail system's capacity to absorb diverted traffic—is essential for accurate commodity flow modeling.
