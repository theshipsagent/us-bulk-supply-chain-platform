## Purpose

This annex catalogs all maps and visualizations referenced throughout the US Bulk Supply Chain Report. Maps are organized by chapter reference and thematic category. Each entry includes the map identifier, title, description, primary data sources, and the chapter(s) in which it is referenced.

Maps are designed for publication at standard US Letter (8.5 x 11 in) and A3 (11.7 x 16.5 in) formats. Digital versions are available in the platform's `03_OUTPUTS/maps/` directory in PDF, PNG (300 dpi), and GeoJSON formats where applicable.

## Detailed Map Descriptions

### MAP-01: US Inland Waterway System Overview

**Category:** System Overview
**Chapter Reference:** Ch. 01 (Executive Overview), Ch. 02 (Inland Waterway System)
**Scale:** Continental US, 1:6,000,000
**Projection:** Albers Equal-Area Conic

**Description:**
Comprehensive map of the US commercially navigable inland waterway system, depicting all USACE-maintained waterways with commercial traffic. The map distinguishes between waterway classes by annual tonnage: heavy-traffic corridors (>50M tons/year) shown in dark blue, moderate-traffic corridors (10--50M tons) in medium blue, and light-traffic corridors (<10M tons) in light blue. The 12,000-mile Mississippi River System is highlighted as the network backbone, with tributary connections to the Ohio, Missouri, Illinois, Tennessee, Cumberland, and Arkansas river systems. The Gulf Intracoastal Waterway (GIWW) is shown as the east-west coastal connector from Brownsville, TX to Apalachee Bay, FL. Great Lakes connecting channels and the Columbia/Snake River system are included as separate navigable networks.

**Key Features Depicted:**
- All commercially navigable waterways (25,000 miles)
- Lock and dam locations (192 sites, 239 chambers)
- Major port cities and bulk terminal clusters
- Waterway mile markers at 100-mile intervals on principal corridors
- Connection points between waterway systems (e.g., Chicago Sanitary and Ship Canal, Tennessee-Tombigbee Waterway)

**Data Sources:** USACE National Waterway Network (NWN), Navigation Data Center, National Transportation Atlas Database (NTAD)

### MAP-02: Mississippi River System Lock Locations

**Category:** Lock Infrastructure
**Chapter Reference:** Ch. 02 (Inland Waterway System), Ch. 10 (Infrastructure Outlook)
**Scale:** Mississippi River System, 1:3,000,000
**Projection:** Albers Equal-Area Conic

**Description:**
Detailed map of all lock and dam sites on the Mississippi River System, including the Upper Mississippi (Minneapolis to Cairo), Lower Mississippi (Cairo to Head of Passes), Ohio River (Pittsburgh to Cairo), Illinois Waterway (Chicago to Grafton), Tennessee River, Cumberland River, and Arkansas River (McClellan-Kerr Navigation System). Each lock site is symbolized by condition status: green (operational, within design life), yellow (operational, beyond design life, rehabilitation planned), red (operational, beyond design life, no rehabilitation funded). IIJA-funded construction projects are marked with a distinct symbol indicating active construction.

**Key Features Depicted:**
- 192 lock sites with chamber dimensions annotated
- Lock age and condition status (color-coded)
- IIJA-funded project locations (Chickamauga, Kentucky, LaGrange, Montgomery, Emsworth, Brandon Road)
- Average delay times at major bottleneck locks (annotated callouts)
- River mile markers
- Tributary confluence points

**Data Sources:** USACE Lock Performance Monitoring System (LPMS), IIJA project fact sheets, Navigation Data Center

### MAP-03: Ohio River Lock and Dam Inventory

**Category:** Lock Infrastructure
**Chapter Reference:** Ch. 02 (Inland Waterway System)
**Scale:** Ohio River basin, 1:2,000,000
**Projection:** Albers Equal-Area Conic

**Description:**
Focused map of the Ohio River navigation system from Pittsburgh, PA to Cairo, IL (981 miles), including the 20 lock and dam sites that maintain the 9-foot navigation channel. Each lock site includes an inset data panel showing chamber dimensions, year built, annual tonnage, and average delay time. The Olmsted Lock and Dam (completed 2018, replacing obsolete Locks 52 and 53) is highlighted as the system's newest and highest-capacity facility. Tributary connections to the Kanawha, Monongahela, Allegheny, Muskingum, and Kentucky rivers are shown with their respective lock systems.

**Key Features Depicted:**
- 20 Ohio River main stem lock and dam sites
- Tributary lock systems (14 additional sites)
- Pool stages and dam locations
- Major river ports (Pittsburgh, Huntington, Cincinnati, Louisville)
- Coal loading and industrial terminal locations
- Montgomery and Emsworth rehabilitation project status

**Data Sources:** USACE Ohio River Division, LPMS, Pittsburgh and Huntington District reports

### MAP-04: Gulf Coast Port Infrastructure

**Category:** Port Infrastructure
**Chapter Reference:** Ch. 03 (Port Infrastructure), Ch. 07 (Trade Flow Analysis)
**Scale:** US Gulf Coast (Brownsville, TX to Mobile, AL), 1:2,500,000
**Projection:** Lambert Conformal Conic

**Description:**
Comprehensive map of bulk commodity port infrastructure along the US Gulf Coast, the region handling approximately 65% of US waterborne bulk throughput. The map depicts all deep-water port facilities with bulk handling capability, including berth locations, channel depths, and anchorage areas. The Lower Mississippi River complex (Baton Rouge to Head of Passes) is shown in expanded detail, illustrating the concentration of grain elevators, petroleum terminals, chemical docks, and midstream transfer operations. The Houston Ship Channel, Sabine-Neches Waterway, Calcasieu Channel, and Mississippi River Ship Channel are depicted with authorized and maintained channel dimensions.

**Key Features Depicted:**
- All bulk-capable port terminals with commodity type annotations
- Channel depths and widths (authorized vs. maintained)
- Anchorage areas and offshore lightering zones
- Pilot boarding stations
- GIWW intersection points with deep-water channels
- Houston Ship Channel Project 11 improvement areas
- Louisiana International Terminal (LIT) planned location
- Mississippi River mile markers (Baton Rouge to Head of Passes)

**Data Sources:** USACE entrance/clearance data, NOAA nautical charts, individual port authority facility maps, USCG waterway data

**Category:** Railroad Network
**Chapter Reference:** Ch. 04 (Railroad Network)
**Scale:** Western US (west of Mississippi River), 1:6,000,000
**Projection:** Albers Equal-Area Conic

**Description:**
Map of the BNSF Railway and Union Pacific railroad networks in the western United States, illustrating the duopoly market structure that defines Class I rail service west of the Mississippi. Main line routes are distinguished by carrier ownership (BNSF = orange, UP = blue) with traffic density indicated by line weight. Key bulk commodity corridors are labeled, including the Powder River Basin coal lines, Pacific Northwest grain corridors, and Gulf Coast petrochemical routes. Terminal and classification yard locations are marked.

**Key Features Depicted:**
- BNSF and UP main line networks with traffic density
- Trackage rights and joint operations segments
- Major classification yards and intermodal terminals
- Bulk commodity loading facilities (grain elevators, coal mines, chemical plants)
- CPKC (formerly KCS) cross-border corridor through Kansas City to Mexico
- CN US operations (Chicago to New Orleans via Jackson, MS)

**Data Sources:** STB Annual Report, NTAD National Rail Network (NARN), AAR Railroad Facts, FRA GIS data

### MAP-10: Commodity Flow Density — Waterways

**Category:** Commodity Flow
**Chapter Reference:** Ch. 07 (Trade Flow Analysis)
**Scale:** US inland waterway system, 1:5,000,000
**Projection:** Albers Equal-Area Conic

**Description:**
Heat-map style visualization of waterborne commodity flow density across the US inland waterway system. Line width and color intensity are proportional to annual tonnage on each waterway segment, revealing the dominant traffic patterns: the heavy concentration on the Lower Mississippi and Ohio Rivers, the seasonal grain-driven peaks on the Upper Mississippi and Illinois Waterway, and the petrochemical-dominated flows on the GIWW and Houston Ship Channel. Directional flow arrows distinguish northbound from southbound traffic where directional volumes differ significantly.

**Key Features Depicted:**
- Annual tonnage by waterway segment (graduated line width)
- Directional flow arrows (northbound vs. southbound)
- Commodity composition pie charts at major junctions
- Lock throughput volumes
- Seasonal variation indicators for grain corridors
- Import/export gateway points

**Data Sources:** USACE Waterborne Commerce Statistics Center (WCSC), LPMS lock throughput data, USDA Grain Transportation Report

### MAP-12: Section 301 Impact Zones

**Category:** Regulatory
**Chapter Reference:** Ch. 08 (Regulatory Environment), Ch. 09 (Cost Benchmarking)
**Scale:** Continental US with global inset, 1:6,000,000 (US) / 1:100,000,000 (global)
**Projection:** Albers Equal-Area Conic (US panel), Winkel Tripel (global panel)

**Description:**
Two-panel map illustrating the geographic impact of Section 301 maritime shipping fees on US bulk supply chains. The US panel identifies ports most affected by the fee structure, with graduated symbols showing the proportion of calling vessels that are Chinese-built and therefore subject to fees. Gulf Coast ports with high Chinese-built vessel exposure are highlighted. The global inset panel shows the major trade routes affected, including China-to-US Pacific routes, China-to-US Gulf routes via Suez/Panama, and triangulated routes where Chinese-built vessels carry third-country cargo to US ports.

**Key Features Depicted:**
- US ports sized by Chinese-built vessel call frequency
- Fee exposure classification (high/medium/low) by port
- Global trade route lines with vessel type annotation
- Alternative routing options to avoid fee-exposed vessels
- Commodity-specific impact zones (bulk imports most affected)
- Timeline overlay showing fee phase-in schedule by category

**Data Sources:** USTR Section 301 Federal Register notices, USCG vessel entry data, Panjiva import manifests, IHS Markit vessel database, MARAD vessel registry

### MAP-15: Climate Vulnerability Zones

**Category:** Resilience
**Chapter Reference:** Ch. 10 (Infrastructure Outlook)
**Scale:** Continental US, 1:6,000,000
**Projection:** Albers Equal-Area Conic

**Description:**
Map identifying climate vulnerability zones for bulk supply chain infrastructure, based on NOAA climate projections, USACE hydrological modeling, and historical disruption data. The map overlays three primary risk categories: drought/low-water risk zones (affecting barge loading and waterway navigation), flood/high-water risk zones (affecting lock operations and port access), and hurricane/storm surge risk zones (affecting Gulf and Atlantic coast port infrastructure). Infrastructure assets within high-vulnerability zones are highlighted.

**Key Features Depicted:**
- Drought vulnerability zones (Lower Mississippi, Ohio River, Missouri River)
- Flood vulnerability zones (Upper Mississippi, Missouri River, Ohio River tributaries)
- Hurricane/storm surge vulnerability zones (Gulf Coast, South Atlantic)
- Heat restriction zones for rail operations
- Ice closure zones (Great Lakes, Upper Mississippi, northern tributaries)
- Historical disruption events annotated (2019 floods, 2022 low water, major hurricanes)
- Sea level rise projection contours for Gulf Coast port areas

**Data Sources:** NOAA Climate Projections, USACE Institute for Water Resources, FEMA flood maps, National Hurricane Center historical tracks

## Map Production Notes

All maps in this report are produced using the following standards:

- **Coordinate reference system:** NAD83 / Conus Albers (EPSG:5070) for continental US maps; WGS 84 (EPSG:4326) for global insets
- **Base data:** USGS National Map, Census TIGER/Line, Natural Earth (for global context)
- **Transportation networks:** NTAD National Waterway Network, NTAD National Rail Network, NHPN (highways)
- **Symbology:** Consistent across all maps; see legend specifications in `03_OUTPUTS/maps/legend_spec.json`
- **Resolution:** 300 dpi minimum for print publication; 150 dpi for digital distribution
- **Formats available:** PDF (vector), PNG (raster), GeoJSON (spatial data), SVG (scalable vector)
- **Accessibility:** All maps include text descriptions for screen reader compatibility
