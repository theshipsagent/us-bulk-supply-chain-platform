# Chapter 5: Intermodal Connectivity and Transfer Infrastructure

## Executive Summary

Efficient bulk commodity supply chains rarely rely on a single transportation mode from origin to destination; instead, they leverage the comparative advantages of barge, rail, truck, and pipeline through strategically located intermodal transfer points. This chapter examines the infrastructure, economics, and operational characteristics of mode-to-mode transfers --- with particular emphasis on barge-to-rail facilities along the Mississippi River System, major inland intermodal terminals, truck drayage for last-mile delivery, and the pipeline network for liquid and gaseous commodities. Modal cost differentials are substantial (barge at $0.008--$0.015 per ton-mile, rail at $0.025--$0.045, and truck at $0.10--$0.25), and the selection and sequencing of modes across a supply chain can reduce total delivered cost by 30--50% compared to single-mode alternatives, making intermodal connectivity a critical determinant of commodity competitiveness.

## 5.2 Barge-to-Rail Transfer Points Along the Mississippi River System

The Mississippi River System --- comprising the Mississippi, Ohio, Missouri, Illinois, Tennessee, and Arkansas Rivers --- intersects with the Class I railroad network at dozens of points, creating opportunities for barge-to-rail (and rail-to-barge) commodity transfers. These transfer facilities are particularly important for:

- **Grain** --- Upper Midwest grain moves by rail to river elevators, is transferred to barges, and floats to Gulf export terminals. The reverse flow brings imported fertilizer upstream by barge for rail distribution to inland markets.
- **Coal** --- Appalachian coal moves by rail to Ohio River transload points for barge delivery to riverside power plants and industrial users.
- **Aggregates and construction materials** --- Sand, gravel, and crusite move by barge to transfer points near construction markets, then by truck or rail to job sites.
- **Chemicals and petroleum products** --- Gulf Coast chemical production moves by barge up the Mississippi/Ohio system, with rail transfer at inland terminals.

### Key Barge-to-Rail Transfer Locations

|---|---|---|---|---|
| New Orleans / Lower Mississippi (mile 0--235) | Mississippi | Grain (export), coal, petroleum coke, chemicals | BNSF, UP, NS, CSX, CN, CPKC | 500+ million tons (total port complex) |
| Baton Rouge, LA | Mississippi | Chemicals, petroleum products, grain | UP, BNSF, CN | 75+ million tons |
| Memphis, TN | Mississippi | Grain, steel, chemicals, forest products | BNSF, UP, NS, CSX, CN | 15+ million tons |

| Cincinnati / Northern KY | Ohio | Coal, chemicals, steel, aggregates | CSX, NS | 40+ million tons |
| Pittsburgh, PA | Ohio / Allegheny / Monongahela | Coal, steel, aggregates, chemicals | NS, CSX | 30+ million tons |

| Paducah / Calvert City, KY | Tennessee / Ohio confluence | Chemicals, coal, aggregates | CSX, NS, BNSF (via interchange) | 10+ million tons |

### Transfer Facility Design

Barge-to-rail transfer typically involves:

- **Marine terminals** with fleeting areas (barge staging), mooring cells, and dock infrastructure
- **Material handling equipment** --- Clamshell cranes, conveyor systems, pneumatic unloaders (for grain and cement), or pipeline connections (for liquid bulk)
- **Rail loop tracks or ladder tracks** for spotted cars during loading/unloading
- **Storage capacity** --- Silos (grain), stockpiles (coal, aggregates), tank farms (liquid), or covered warehouses (bagged product)
- **Truck scales and load-out** for onward truck distribution

Throughput rates at modern barge-to-rail facilities range from 1,000 to 10,000 tons per day, depending on commodity, equipment, and the number of simultaneous loading/unloading positions.

## 5.3 Major Inland Intermodal Terminals

Several inland hub cities serve as critical nodes where multiple transportation modes converge, enabling efficient commodity routing and transfer.

### Chicago, IL

Chicago is the single most important intermodal hub in North America:

- **Six of seven Class I railroads** operate in the Chicago terminal area (BNSF, UP, CSX, NS, CN, CPKC)
- **Railroad interchange** --- Approximately 25% of all US rail freight passes through Chicago, with an average transit time of 24--48 hours through the terminal area (historically longer during congestion periods)
- **CREATE Program** --- The Chicago Region Environmental and Transportation Efficiency program is a public-private partnership investing in grade separations, junction improvements, and yard upgrades to reduce rail congestion
- **Barge connectivity** via the Illinois Waterway (linking Lake Michigan to the Mississippi River System)
- **Truck access** via Interstates 80, 90, 94, 55, 57, and 65

For bulk commodities, Chicago functions primarily as a **railroad interchange and classification point** rather than a traditional intermodal lift facility. Grain, coal, chemicals, and metals are frequently reclassified or transferred between carriers at Chicago-area yards.

### Memphis, TN

Memphis sits at the intersection of five Class I railroads (BNSF, UP, NS, CSX, CN) and the Mississippi River:

- **Intermodal container/trailer terminals** operated by multiple carriers
- **Bulk transfer** between barge and rail at riverside terminals
- **Trucking hub** --- Memphis is a top-ten trucking center, with extensive warehousing and distribution infrastructure
- **Proximity to agricultural production** --- Major grain origination and distribution point for the Mid-South

### St. Louis, MO

Located at the confluence of the Mississippi and Missouri Rivers:

- **Four Class I railroads** (BNSF, UP, NS, CSX) plus regional carriers
- **Barge-to-rail grain transfer** --- Numerous river elevators handle export and domestic grain flows
- **Chemical and petroleum transload** --- Facilities serve Gulf Coast and Midwest chemical markets
- **Gateway function** --- St. Louis connects Eastern and Western railroad territories

### Kansas City, MO/KS

A major railroad hub where Eastern and Western carriers converge:

- **Five Class I railroads** (BNSF, UP, NS, CSX via Meridian Speedway, CPKC)
- **Grain and agricultural products** --- Central position in the winter wheat belt
- **Automotive and general merchandise** intermodal lift facilities
- **Bulk transload** for chemicals, building materials, and petroleum products

### Houston, TX

The largest petrochemical complex in the United States:

- **Three Class I railroads** (BNSF, UP, CPKC) plus extensive industrial switching carriers
- **Houston Ship Channel** --- One of the busiest port complexes globally, handling crude oil, refined products, chemicals, and LNG
- **Rail-to-ship and barge-to-rail** transfer for bulk chemicals, plastics, and petroleum products
- **Pipeline hub** --- Convergence point for crude oil, natural gas, and refined product pipelines (see Section 5.7)
- **Truck drayage** --- Extensive short-haul trucking connects petrochemical plants, rail terminals, port facilities, and distribution centers

## 5.4 Transfer Costs and Handling Rates

Intermodal transfer introduces additional costs beyond line-haul transportation. These costs vary significantly by commodity, facility design, and transfer mode pair.

### Table 5.2: Representative Intermodal Transfer Costs

| Transfer Type | Commodity Category | Estimated Cost per Ton | Key Cost Drivers |
|---|---|---|---|
| Barge-to-rail (dry bulk) | Grain, coal, aggregates | $2.00 -- $5.00 | Crane/conveyor throughput rate, storage duration, demurrage |
| Barge-to-rail (liquid bulk) | Chemicals, petroleum | $1.50 -- $4.00 | Pump rates, tank car spotted time, heating requirements |
| Rail-to-truck (transload, dry) | Grain, cement, minerals | $3.00 -- $8.00 | Pneumatic transfer or front-end loader, storage, truck load-out |
| Rail-to-truck (transload, liquid) | Chemicals, fuels | $2.00 -- $6.00 | Pump-over, tank storage, truck loading rack |
| Barge-to-truck (direct) | Aggregates, sand | $2.00 -- $5.00 | Crane unload, stockpile, truck load-out |
| Rail-to-ship (export terminal) | Grain, coal, petroleum coke | $3.00 -- $8.00 | Conveyor/dumper systems, vessel loading rate, berth time |
| Truck-to-rail (reload) | Various dry bulk | $4.00 -- $10.00 | Truck dump, storage, rail car loading |

**Note:** Costs are approximate and vary substantially by facility, throughput volume, commodity characteristics, and contract terms. Costs include handling labor, equipment operation, and short-term storage but exclude line-haul transportation and long-term storage fees.

### Cost Optimization Considerations

- **Dwell time minimization** --- Every hour a barge, rail car, or truck sits idle at a transfer point incurs opportunity cost and, potentially, demurrage or detention charges. Modern facilities are designed for continuous-flow operations.
- **Equipment matching** --- Barge-to-rail transfers must match barge unloading capacity with rail car loading rates. A mismatch creates bottlenecks and increases per-ton handling costs.
- **Seasonal surge capacity** --- Grain harvest and coal stockpiling create seasonal demand peaks that can overwhelm transfer facility capacity, leading to delays and cost premiums.
- **Double handling avoidance** --- Each transfer adds $2--$10 per ton in handling costs. Supply chain design should minimize the number of mode transfers unless the line-haul savings more than offset handling costs.

## 5.5 Truck Drayage for Last-Mile Bulk Delivery

Despite the cost advantages of barge and rail for long-haul bulk movement, the majority of final delivery to end-use facilities depends on truck transportation. This "last-mile" segment frequently represents the highest per-ton-mile cost in the entire supply chain.

### Drayage Economics

| Distance Range (miles) | Estimated Cost per Ton (25-ton load) | Cost per Ton-Mile | Typical Application |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 -- 25 | $3.00 -- $8.00 | $0.12 -- $0.32 | Port/terminal to nearby plant or jobsite |
| 25 -- 75 | $6.00 -- $15.00 | $0.08 -- $0.20 | Rail transload to regional distribution |
| 75 -- 150 | $12.00 -- $30.00 | $0.08 -- $0.20 | Regional distribution from hub terminal |
| 150 -- 300 | $25.00 -- $60.00 | $0.08 -- $0.20 | Direct truck delivery (no rail/barge) |

### Equipment and Regulatory Considerations

Bulk truck drayage uses specialized equipment:

- **Pneumatic (dry bulk) trailers** --- For cement, fly ash, flour, plastic pellets. Capacities of 22--26 tons.
- **End-dump and belly-dump trailers** --- For aggregates, sand, coal. Capacities of 22--26 tons (higher with state-permitted overweight corridors).
- **Liquid tanker trailers** --- For chemicals, fuels, food-grade liquids. Capacities of 5,500--9,500 gallons depending on product density and DOT classification.
- **Hopper-bottom trailers** --- For grain, fertilizer, animal feed. Capacities of 22--26 tons.

Federal gross vehicle weight limits (80,000 lbs on Interstate highways) constrain payload to approximately 22--26 tons per truckload, depending on tractor and trailer tare weight. Some states permit higher weights on designated corridors or with special permits, which can reduce per-ton drayage costs.

### Driver Availability and Market Dynamics

Truck drayage costs are sensitive to driver availability and hours-of-service (HOS) regulations. The chronic shortage of commercial drivers in the US (estimated at 60,000--80,000 drivers) exerts upward pressure on per-mile rates, particularly for short-haul drayage work, which involves more loading/unloading time relative to driving time and is therefore less attractive to drivers compensated on a per-mile basis.

## 5.6 Multimodal Routing Optimization

Designing least-cost bulk commodity supply chains requires simultaneous consideration of:

1. **Line-haul modal costs** --- As established in Table 5.1, barge is the lowest-cost mode for long-haul bulk, but is constrained to navigable waterways. Rail provides the next-lowest cost on a per-ton-mile basis, with far greater geographic reach. Truck is the most expensive but offers door-to-door flexibility.

2. **Transfer costs** --- Each mode change adds $2--$10 per ton (Table 5.2). A routing with two mode transfers must achieve sufficient line-haul savings to offset $4--$20 per ton in aggregate handling costs.

3. **Transit time and reliability** --- Barge transit is slow (5--12 mph) and subject to seasonal disruptions (ice, low water, flood closures). Rail provides intermediate speed and reliability. Truck is the fastest and most schedule-flexible. Time-sensitive commodities (perishable goods, just-in-time production inputs) may warrant higher-cost modal choices.

4. **Inventory carrying costs** --- Slower transit modes require more pipeline inventory, increasing working capital requirements. For high-value commodities, the inventory cost of slow barge transit may exceed the line-haul savings.

5. **Terminal access and capacity** --- Not all origins and destinations are served by all modes. The availability and capacity of intermodal transfer facilities constrains routing options.

### Optimization Framework

A least-cost routing optimization for bulk commodities typically involves:

- **Network representation** --- A graph with nodes (origins, destinations, transfer terminals) and edges (modal links with distance, cost, time, and capacity attributes)
- **Objective function** --- Minimize total delivered cost (line-haul + transfer + inventory + risk)
- **Constraints** --- Modal capacity, terminal throughput, seasonal availability, equipment availability, customer delivery windows
- **Scenario analysis** --- Sensitivity to fuel price changes, waterway closures, railroad service disruptions, and demand fluctuations

The commodity-specific chapters in this report series will apply this framework with commodity-relevant parameters.

## 5.7 Pipeline Network Overview

Pipelines constitute a distinct transportation mode for liquid and gaseous bulk commodities, operating on fundamentally different economics than barge, rail, or truck.

### Petroleum Pipelines

The US petroleum pipeline network comprises approximately:

- **190,000+ miles of liquid petroleum pipelines** (crude oil, refined products, NGLs)
- Regulated by the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC) for interstate movements and state commissions for intrastate
- Major systems include Colonial Pipeline (Gulf Coast to East Coast refined products), Keystone Pipeline System (Canadian crude to US refineries), DAPL (Bakken crude to Midwest/Gulf), and the Permian Basin gathering and takeaway systems

### Natural Gas Pipelines

- **300,000+ miles of interstate natural gas transmission pipelines**
- Regulated by FERC under the Natural Gas Act
- Major corridors move Appalachian (Marcellus/Utica) production to demand centers in the Northeast, Southeast, and Gulf Coast for LNG export
- Capacity constraints on Appalachian takeaway have periodically depressed Marcellus basis differentials, illustrating the direct link between pipeline infrastructure and commodity pricing

### CO2 Pipelines

- Approximately **5,000 miles of CO2 pipelines** in the US, primarily in the Permian Basin region
- Used for enhanced oil recovery (EOR) operations
- Growing interest in CO2 pipeline infrastructure for carbon capture, utilization, and storage (CCUS) projects, with multiple new pipeline proposals under federal and state review

### Pipeline Economics

| Attribute | Typical Value |
|---|---|
| Cost per ton-mile | $0.01 -- $0.03 (crude oil/refined products); varies for natural gas |
| Throughput capacity | 100,000 -- 3,000,000+ barrels per day (large-diameter crude lines) |
| Construction cost | $3 -- $8 million per mile (new construction, terrain-dependent) |
| Operating cost | Low marginal cost per unit volume once constructed |
| Permitting timeline | 2 -- 7+ years for interstate pipelines (FERC/NEPA process) |
| Commodity limitation | Liquids and gases only; no dry bulk capability |

Pipelines are relevant to bulk supply chain analysis primarily as **competitive alternatives** to rail and barge for crude oil, refined products, and NGL movements. When pipeline capacity is available, it typically offers the lowest-cost and most reliable transportation for these commodities. Pipeline capacity constraints or construction delays frequently redirect liquid bulk volumes to rail and barge, creating cross-modal market dynamics that affect rates and equipment availability across the transportation network.

## 5.8 Infrastructure Gaps and Capacity Constraints

Despite the extensive US intermodal network, several structural gaps and capacity constraints limit the efficiency of bulk commodity supply chains.

### Lock and Dam System Aging

- The US Army Corps of Engineers operates 236 lock chambers at 193 lock sites on the inland waterway system
- Average age exceeds **60 years**, well beyond the 50-year design life
- Unscheduled lock closures cause barge delays averaging 2--4 hours per lockage during peak periods, with some facilities experiencing 8--12 hour average delays
- The Inland Waterways Trust Fund (IWTF) and congressional appropriations have historically underfunded rehabilitation, though the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA) of 2021 provided incremental funding

### Chicago Rail Bottleneck

- Rail transit through Chicago averages 24--48 hours but can exceed 72 hours during peak congestion
- The CREATE Program has completed some grade separations but remains underfunded relative to the full project scope
- Congestion at Chicago imposes systemwide costs estimated at billions of dollars annually in delayed freight

### Gulf Coast Port Capacity

- The Lower Mississippi River port complex and Houston Ship Channel face periodic congestion during grain harvest season and hurricane recovery periods
- Draft restrictions on the Mississippi River (periodically below 45 feet at Southwest Pass) limit vessel loading to less than full capacity, increasing per-ton ocean freight costs
- Proposed deepening of the Mississippi River Ship Channel to 50 feet would improve vessel economics but faces environmental and funding challenges

### Intermodal Terminal Underinvestment

- Many barge-to-rail transfer facilities on the inland waterway system were built in the mid-20th century and lack modern material-handling equipment
- Short-line and regional railroads connecting barge terminals to the Class I network often have deferred maintenance, reducing reliability and speed
- Truck access roads to river terminals and rural transload facilities are frequently in poor condition, increasing drayage costs and safety risks

### Pipeline Permitting and Construction Delays

- New interstate pipeline construction faces extended permitting timelines (often 3--7 years) and legal challenges
- As a result, crude oil and NGL production growth in the Permian Basin, Bakken, and Appalachian Basin has periodically outpaced pipeline takeaway capacity, forcing volumes onto rail and truck at higher cost
- The regulatory environment for CO2 pipelines (for CCUS) remains uncertain, potentially delaying deployment of carbon capture supply chains

### Workforce Constraints

- All modes face workforce challenges: truck driver shortages, skilled lock operator retirements, railroad crew availability, and marine pilot/deckhand recruitment
- Workforce constraints limit the ability of the intermodal network to scale capacity in response to demand surges

## Implications for Commodity Analysis

The intermodal connectivity analysis in this chapter provides the following foundations for commodity-specific supply chain modeling:

1. **Modal selection logic** --- The cost-per-ton-mile differentials documented in Table 5.1 (barge $0.008--$0.015, rail $0.025--$0.045, truck $0.10--$0.25) establish clear economic preferences for long-haul bulk movement: barge where waterway access exists, rail for inland corridors, and truck for last-mile delivery. Each commodity chapter will evaluate the available modal options for its specific origin-destination pairs.

2. **Transfer cost budgets** --- The handling costs in Table 5.2 ($2--$10 per ton per transfer) must be incorporated into total delivered cost models. A two-transfer supply chain (e.g., rail-to-barge-to-truck) adds $4--$20 per ton in handling costs, which is justifiable only when line-haul savings are substantial.

3. **Critical transfer nodes** --- The Mississippi River System barge-to-rail network and the five major inland hubs (Chicago, Memphis, St. Louis, Kansas City, Houston) are the primary intermodal connection points for bulk commodities. Commodity chapters will identify which of these nodes are relevant to their specific supply chains.

4. **Capacity constraints as cost drivers** --- Lock delays, Chicago rail congestion, Gulf Coast port draft restrictions, and pipeline permitting bottlenecks all impose costs that vary by commodity and corridor. Commodity chapters will quantify the impact of these constraints on specific supply chains.

5. **Pipeline competition** --- For liquid and gaseous commodities (crude oil, refined products, NGLs, natural gas, CO2), pipeline availability fundamentally alters the competitive dynamics of rail and barge. Commodity chapters covering petroleum and chemical products will explicitly model pipeline vs. rail vs. barge economics.

6. **Seasonal and disruption risk** --- Barge-dependent supply chains face ice closures (December--March on the Upper Mississippi), low-water events (late summer/fall), and flood disruptions (spring). Commodity chapters will incorporate seasonal availability windows and disruption risk premiums into their cost models.

7. **Last-mile cost significance** --- Truck drayage at $0.10--$0.25 per ton-mile means that a 100-mile last-mile segment can cost $10--$25 per ton --- often comparable to or exceeding the entire long-haul barge or rail cost for a 1,000-mile movement. Optimizing transload facility location relative to end-use markets is therefore a high-leverage decision for every bulk commodity supply chain.
