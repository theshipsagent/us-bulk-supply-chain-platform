# Chapter 5: Lower Mississippi River Cement Corridor

**US Bulk Supply Chain Reporting Platform -- Cement & Cementitious Materials Commodity Report**

---

The Lower Mississippi River corridor -- stretching from St. Louis, Missouri to the Gulf of Mexico at Southwest Pass, Louisiana, with lateral connections through the Gulf Intracoastal Waterway (GIWW) to Houston, Texas -- functions as the primary artery for US bulk cement distribution into the interior. Approximately 5 million tons of cement per year move by barge through this corridor, originating from both import terminals (Houston, New Orleans) and domestic production plants (Holcim Ste. Genevieve, Buzzi Cape Girardeau). The corridor's infrastructure -- including the Inner Harbor Navigation Canal (IHNC) Lock in New Orleans, Harvey Lock, and Algiers Lock on the west bank -- shapes barge routing, transit times, and effective capacity. This chapter provides a detailed analysis of import terminal facilities, barge distribution networks, lock constraints, seasonal rate patterns, and the comparative economics of barge versus rail for representative inland delivery points.

---

## 5.1 Corridor Overview

### Geographic Scope

The Lower Mississippi River cement corridor encompasses:

- **Mainline Lower Mississippi** -- St. Louis, MO (River Mile 180 Upper Mississippi) through New Orleans, LA to Southwest Pass (River Mile 0), approximately 1,050 river miles
- **Houston Ship Channel** -- Galveston Bay entrance through the 52-mile ship channel to the Port of Houston turning basin
- **Gulf Intracoastal Waterway (GIWW)** -- Houston to New Orleans segment, approximately 350 miles, connecting the Houston Ship Channel to the Lower Mississippi River system
- **Atchafalaya River / Old River Control Structure** -- Alternative route from the Mississippi to Morgan City, LA and the GIWW
- **Upper reaches** -- NOLA upriver to Memphis (~700 miles), St. Louis (~1,050 miles), and Cincinnati (~1,500 miles via Ohio River)

## 5.5 Lock Infrastructure

### Critical Locks for Cement Barge Movements

Navigation locks represent the primary capacity constraints on the Lower Mississippi River corridor for cement barge movements. While the main stem of the Lower Mississippi River (below St. Louis) is lock-free, connecting waterways require lock transits.

### Inner Harbor Navigation Canal (IHNC) Lock -- New Orleans

| Specification | Detail |
|--------------|--------|
| Location | New Orleans, LA -- connecting Mississippi River to IHNC/GIWW |
| Lock Chamber | 640 ft x 75 ft |
| Year Built | 1923 |
| Condition | Aged; replacement authorized but not funded |
| Capacity | 1 barge width (75 ft); must break tows |
| Average Delay | 8--16 hours (varies seasonally) |
| Impact on Cement | Significant -- all GIWW-to-Mississippi and vice versa transits |
| Proposed Replacement | New 1,200 ft x 110 ft lock (USACE project, unfunded) |

The IHNC Lock is the single most significant bottleneck for cement barge movements connecting the Houston-GIWW corridor to the Mississippi River. Its 75-foot width requires large tows to be broken into individual barges for lockage, adding 8--16 hours of delay per transit. Cement barges moving from Houston import terminals through the GIWW to the Mississippi River (for upriver distribution to Memphis and St. Louis) must transit this lock, as must barges moving from Mississippi River terminals to GIWW destinations.

### Lock Impact Summary

| Lock | Annual Cement Tonnage Affected | Avg Delay per Transit | Annual Cost Impact (est.) |
|------|-------------------------------|----------------------|--------------------------|
| IHNC Lock | 2--3 MT | 8--16 hours | $3--6 million |
| Harvey Lock | 0.5--1 MT | 4--12 hours | $1--2 million |
| Algiers Lock | 0.5--1 MT | 4--8 hours | $0.5--1 million |
| **Total** | **3--5 MT** | -- | **$4.5--9 million** |

The lock delay costs are borne by the barge operator (towboat hourly costs during delay) and passed through to the cement shipper in the freight rate. These costs effectively add $1--3/ton to cement barge movements through the NOLA lock complex, reducing the barge cost advantage over rail for some origin-destination pairs.

## 5.7 Seasonal Rate Patterns

### Barge Rate Seasonality

Cement barge rates on the Lower Mississippi exhibit pronounced seasonal patterns driven by:

1. **Construction season demand** -- Peak cement consumption (April--October) increases barge demand.
2. **Grain harvest competition** -- Fall grain harvest (September--November) competes for barge capacity, driving rates sharply higher for all commodities including cement.
3. **Low water conditions** -- Late summer/fall low water on the Lower Mississippi can restrict draft, reducing barge capacity and increasing rates.
4. **High water/flooding** -- Spring flooding can halt navigation temporarily, creating post-flood rate spikes.

### Representative Rate Index: NOLA → Memphis (Cement, per ton)

| Month | Typical Rate ($/ton) | Index (Jan = 100) | Notes |
|-------|---------------------|-------------------|-------|
| January | $14 | 100 | Low season, ample capacity |
| February | $14 | 100 | Low season |
| March | $15 | 107 | Spring construction begins |
| April | $16 | 114 | Construction ramp-up |
| May | $17 | 121 | Peak season building |
| June | $18 | 129 | Strong demand |
| July | $18 | 129 | Peak construction |
| August | $18 | 129 | Peak construction, low water risk |
| September | $20 | 143 | Grain harvest begins, capacity tight |
| October | $22 | 157 | Peak grain + cement + low water |
| November | $20 | 143 | Grain harvest winding down |
| December | $15 | 107 | Season end, capacity loosens |

The October peak represents a confluence of three factors: residual construction demand, peak grain barge demand (corn and soybean harvest), and potential low-water draft restrictions. Cement shippers who do not have annual contract rates face significant cost exposure during this period, with spot rates potentially 50--70% above winter lows.

### GTR Rate Basis

Government Transportation Request (GTR) rates for cement barge movements are published by major operators and provide a reference point for contract negotiations. GTR rates are typically:

- **Basis:** Per ton, origin-to-destination, including barge hire, towage, and fleeting
- **Adjustments:** Fuel surcharge (indexed to diesel), lock delay surcharge (where applicable)
- **Terms:** 30-day payment, standard tariff conditions
- **Applicability:** Federal/military projects; also used as reference for commercial contracts

Cincinnati delivery demonstrates the practical limit of barge-origin import cement distribution. The Ohio River route also adds lock transits (Locks 52, 53, and others) that increase transit time and cost variability.

## 5.9 Corridor Infrastructure Outlook

### USACE Investment Priorities

The US Army Corps of Engineers (USACE) maintains the Lower Mississippi River navigation channel and associated lock infrastructure. Key investment priorities affecting cement logistics:

| Project | Status | Estimated Cost | Impact on Cement |
|---------|--------|---------------|------------------|
| IHNC Lock Replacement | Authorized, not fully funded | $3--5 billion | Eliminates major GIWW-Mississippi bottleneck |
| Lower Miss Channel Deepening (50 ft) | In progress | $2+ billion | Enables larger import vessels at NOLA |
| Harvey Lock Rehabilitation | Planning | $200--500 million | Reduces west bank GIWW delays |
| Algiers Lock Maintenance | Ongoing | $50--100 million | Maintains alternative GIWW access |
| Upper Miss Lock Rehabilitation | Multiple projects | $1+ billion | Improves upriver cement distribution |

### IHNC Lock Replacement: Transformative Potential

The proposed IHNC Lock replacement -- from the current 640 ft x 75 ft chamber to a new 1,200 ft x 110 ft chamber -- would be the single most impactful infrastructure project for cement barge logistics on the Gulf Coast:

- **Current constraint:** 75-foot width requires breaking 15-barge tows into individual barges, adding 8--16 hours per transit.
- **New lock capacity:** 110-foot width would accommodate standard 3-wide barge configurations without breaking tow, reducing transit to 1--2 hours.
- **Cement impact:** Estimated savings of $1--3/ton on all GIWW-to-Mississippi barge movements, improving import cement competitiveness for upriver distribution.
- **Timeline:** Project authorized under WRDA but faces multi-billion dollar funding gap. Construction timeline of 8--12 years once fully funded.

## 5.10 Corridor Summary

The Lower Mississippi River cement corridor is a complex, multimodal logistics system that connects Gulf Coast import terminals to interior US consumption markets. Key takeaways:

1. **Scale** -- Approximately 5 million tons per year of cement moves through this corridor by barge, representing roughly 5% of total US consumption and 25% of waterborne import volume.

2. **Import gateway** -- Houston and New Orleans function as the primary import receiving points, with Houston handling the largest volume. SESCO Cement's Houston operation serves as a case study in specialized import terminal strategy (white + gray cement, dual-origin sourcing).

3. **Lock constraints** -- The IHNC Lock in New Orleans is the single largest infrastructure bottleneck, adding cost and time to all GIWW-Mississippi transfer movements. Replacement is authorized but unfunded.

4. **Seasonal dynamics** -- Barge rates exhibit 50--70% seasonal variation, with October peaks driven by grain harvest competition and potential low-water conditions. Contract rates provide cost certainty for volume shippers.

5. **Modal competition** -- Barge and rail compete for inland distribution, with the lowest-cost mode depending on origin point and destination. Import cement (barge from NOLA) is competitive with domestic rail delivery for Memphis but loses competitiveness at St. Louis and beyond due to long transit distances and lock delays.

6. **Investment drivers** -- Federal lock infrastructure investment (IHNC replacement) and private terminal expansion (SESCO Florida, CEMEX Houston) are the two primary capital deployment themes shaping the corridor's future capacity and cost structure.
