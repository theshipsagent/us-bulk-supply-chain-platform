# Chapter 6: Competitive Landscape

## US Cement & Cementitious Materials Commodity Report

**US Bulk Supply Chain Reporting Platform**
**Report Period:** Q1 2026

---

The US cement industry is a moderately concentrated market dominated by a handful of multinational producers and supplemented by a growing import terminal network serving coastal and Gulf regions. The top five domestic producers -- LafargeHolcim, Lehigh Hanson (Heidelberg Materials), CEMEX, Buzzi Unicem/RC Lonestar, and Ash Grove (CRH) -- control approximately 60% of nameplate production capacity, while the remaining 40% is distributed among Eagle Materials, Martin Marietta, Argos USA, Titan America, CalPortland, and several smaller operators. This chapter maps the national competitive landscape, profiles major producers and import terminal operators, analyzes regional competitive dynamics in key demand corridors, and evaluates the structural forces that define market concentration, pricing power, and competitive entry barriers.

---

## 6.1 National Market Structure

### 6.1.1 Industry Overview

The US cement industry in 2024 comprised 99 active plants operating across 34 states and Puerto Rico, with a combined nameplate capacity of approximately 120 million metric tons per year. Actual production reached approximately 86 million metric tons, yielding a capacity utilization rate of roughly 72% -- below the historical average of 78-82% and well below the 90%+ levels observed during the 2005-2007 construction peak. Total domestic consumption, including imports, reached approximately 110 million metric tons valued at $17 billion.

The industry is characterized by several structural features that shape competitive dynamics:

**Capital intensity and barriers to entry.** A new greenfield cement plant requires $300-500 million in capital investment and 3-5 years for permitting and construction. Environmental permitting under Clean Air Act and state-level regulations adds additional time and cost. This capital barrier effectively limits new domestic capacity additions to brownfield expansions and kiln upgrades at existing facilities.

**Geographic fragmentation with regional concentration.** Cement is a heavy, low-value-to-weight commodity that typically moves no more than 200-300 miles by truck from the point of production. This creates regional markets with distinct competitive dynamics, pricing structures, and supply-demand balances. A producer dominant in the Midwest may have no competitive relevance in the Southeast.

**Import dependency as structural feature.** The 22% import penetration rate in 2024 reflects a long-term structural shift. No major greenfield plant has been built in the US in over a decade, and capacity additions have been limited to kiln line expansions and efficiency upgrades. The economics of domestic expansion -- high capital costs, stringent environmental permitting, and competitive import pricing -- have made import terminals a more capital-efficient path to serving marginal demand.

### 6.1.2 Producer Concentration Metrics

| Metric | Value | Implication |
|--------|-------|-------------|
| Number of active plants | 99 | Moderate fragmentation |
| Number of companies | ~30 | Consolidation through M&A |
| Top 5 producer share (capacity) | ~60% | Moderate concentration |
| Top 10 producer share (capacity) | ~82% | High concentration |
| HHI index (estimated) | 1,100-1,300 | Moderately concentrated per DOJ guidelines |

The Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI), while moderate at the national level, is significantly higher in individual regional markets where one or two producers may control 50-70% of local supply. This regional concentration is partially offset by import competition in coastal and Gulf markets, where waterborne cement provides a competitive check on domestic pricing power.

## 6.2 Major US Cement Producers

### 6.2.1 LafargeHolcim / Holcim US

**Capacity:** 35+ million metric tons per year
**Plants:** Approximately 15 integrated cement plants across the US
**Parent:** Holcim Group (Switzerland)
**Headquarters (US):** Chicago, Illinois

Holcim US is the largest cement producer in the United States by a wide margin, operating a network of plants concentrated in the Midwest, Mountain West, and mid-Atlantic regions. The company's US footprint was assembled through decades of acquisition, including the legacy Lafarge North America and Holnam operations, consolidated under the Holcim brand following the 2015 LafargeHolcim merger.

| Region | Key Plants | Estimated Capacity (MT/yr) |
|--------|-----------|---------------------------|
| Midwest | Alpena MI, Paulding OH, Joppa IL, Ste. Genevieve MO | ~12M |
| Mountain/West | Florence CO, Holnam MT, Devil's Slide UT | ~6M |
| Mid-Atlantic | Whitehall PA, Union Bridge MD | ~4M |
| South/Southeast | Birmingham AL, Holly Hill SC | ~4M |
| Pacific Northwest | Seattle WA area | ~2M |
| Other | Various grinding/blending stations | ~7M |

**Competitive position:** Holcim US benefits from unmatched geographic diversification and production scale. The company's Ste. Genevieve, Missouri plant -- one of the largest in North America at approximately 4 million MT/yr capacity -- anchors its Mississippi River distribution network, supplying downstream barge terminals throughout the Lower Mississippi corridor. The company also operates a significant network of grinding and blending facilities that extend its distribution reach beyond plant-proximate markets.

**Strategic initiatives:** Holcim has committed to net-zero carbon by 2050 and is investing in carbon capture and utilization (CCU) at several US plants, as well as expanding its alternative fuels program. The company is also actively developing its ECOPact low-carbon concrete product line, positioning itself for anticipated green procurement mandates in federal and state infrastructure spending.

**Vertical integration:** Holcim US operates one of the largest US ready-mix concrete networks, with hundreds of plants nationwide. This vertical integration creates both competitive advantage (captive demand) and competitive tension (conflict of interest with independent ready-mix customers who purchase cement from Holcim but compete with Holcim-owned ready-mix in downstream markets).

### 6.2.2 CEMEX USA

**Capacity:** Approximately 12 million metric tons per year
**Plants:** 6 integrated cement plants
**Parent:** CEMEX S.A.B. de C.V. (Mexico)
**Headquarters (US):** Houston, Texas

CEMEX is the second-largest cement producer in the US and the dominant player in the Texas and Florida markets. The company operates a vertically integrated business model encompassing cement production, ready-mix concrete, aggregates, and building materials distribution.

| Plant | Location | Estimated Capacity (MT/yr) | Market Served |
|-------|----------|---------------------------|---------------|
| Balcones | New Braunfels, TX | 3.0M | Central/South Texas |
| Odessa | Odessa, TX | 2.0M | West Texas, Permian Basin |
| Knoxville | Knoxville, TN | 1.5M | Tennessee, Southeast |
| Brooksville South | Brooksville, FL | 1.5M | Tampa Bay, Central Florida |
| Miami | Miami-Dade, FL | 1.1M | South Florida |
| Lyons | Lyons, CO | 0.9M | Colorado, Mountain West |

**Competitive position:** CEMEX's US strategy is anchored by its Texas and Florida strongholds, the two largest cement-consuming states. In Texas, CEMEX's Balcones and Odessa plants, combined with import capacity at the Port of Houston, give the company an estimated 20-25% share of the state market. In Florida, the Brooksville and Miami plants plus Mobile, Alabama import terminal provide approximately 25% of Florida production capacity.

**Import operations:** CEMEX supplements domestic production with imports primarily from its Mexican and Colombian operations. However, the March 2025 increase in tariffs on Mexican imports to 25% has materially impacted CEMEX's import economics, as its legacy supply chain relied on intra-company transfers from Mexican plants at the former 10% rate. The company has responded by redirecting some import sourcing to Turkish and other Mediterranean-origin suppliers.

**Strategic challenges:** CEMEX faces the dual challenge of rising import costs from Mexico and increasing competitive pressure from low-tariff Mediterranean importers. The company's extensive US ready-mix network, while providing captive demand, also creates the vertically integrated supplier tension that independent importers like SESCO can exploit in customer acquisition.

### 6.2.3 Buzzi Unicem / RC Lonestar

**Capacity:** Approximately 8 million metric tons per year
**Plants:** 6 integrated plants (operating as Buzzi Unicem USA and legacy RC Lonestar)
**Parent:** Buzzi S.p.A. (Italy)
**Headquarters (US):** Bethlehem, Pennsylvania

Buzzi Unicem's US operations, formerly branded as RC Lonestar prior to full integration, are concentrated in the central US and Texas markets. The company's plants are strategically positioned along major transportation corridors, with strong rail connectivity to distribution terminals throughout the Midwest and South.

| Plant | Location | Estimated Capacity (MT/yr) | Market Served |
|-------|----------|---------------------------|---------------|
| Maryneal | Maryneal, TX | 2.0M | West/Central Texas |
| Greencastle | Greencastle, IN | 1.8M | Indiana, Midwest |
| Cape Girardeau | Cape Girardeau, MO | 1.5M | Mississippi Valley |
| Stockertown | Stockertown, PA | 1.2M | Mid-Atlantic |
| Pryor | Pryor, OK | 0.9M | Oklahoma, South Central |
| Signal Mountain | Chattanooga, TN | 0.6M | Southeast |

**Competitive position:** Buzzi Unicem occupies a solid mid-tier position, competing effectively in the Midwest and South-Central regions. The Cape Girardeau plant's Mississippi River location provides barge access for distribution to downstream markets, while the Greencastle plant serves as a hub for the Indiana/Illinois market. The company's Maryneal, Texas plant competes directly with CEMEX and other Texas producers for the Permian Basin oil and gas construction market.

**Market approach:** Buzzi Unicem has pursued a more conservative growth strategy than Holcim or CEMEX, focusing on operational efficiency and margin management rather than aggressive market share expansion. The company does not operate a significant ready-mix concrete business, positioning it as a less conflicted cement supplier to independent ready-mix producers -- a competitive advantage similar to SESCO's positioning in the import segment.

### 6.2.4 Lehigh Hanson / Heidelberg Materials

**Capacity:** Approximately 10 million metric tons per year
**Plants:** 8 integrated plants
**Parent:** Heidelberg Materials AG (Germany, formerly HeidelbergCement)
**Headquarters (US):** Irving, Texas

Lehigh Hanson is the US operating subsidiary of Heidelberg Materials (rebranded from HeidelbergCement in 2023), one of the world's largest building materials companies. The company operates a significant cement production network with particular strength in the mid-Atlantic, Southeast, and emerging Texas markets.

| Plant | Location | Estimated Capacity (MT/yr) | Market Served |
|-------|----------|---------------------------|---------------|
| Mitchell | Mitchell, IN | 2.0M | Indiana, Midwest |
| Union Bridge | Union Bridge, MD | 1.8M | Mid-Atlantic |
| Leeds | Leeds, AL | 1.5M | Alabama, Southeast |
| Evansville | Evansville, IN | 1.2M | Ohio Valley, barge corridor |
| Mason City | Mason City, IA | 1.0M | Upper Midwest |
| York | York, PA | 0.8M | Mid-Atlantic |
| White Cement TX | Waco, TX | 0.14M | National (white cement only) |
| New Plant TX | Mitchell County, TX (under construction) | 2.0M+ | Texas statewide |

**Competitive position:** Lehigh Hanson is notable for two strategically significant assets. First, its Lehigh White Cement plant near Waco, Texas is the only domestic white Portland cement producer in the United States, with a capacity of approximately 140,000 MT/yr. This plant supplies the entire US white cement market from domestic production, competing with importers including SESCO, CIMSA Americas, and fragmented container importers. Second, the company's new integrated plant under construction in Mitchell County, Texas represents the first major greenfield cement plant in the US in over a decade. With a targeted capacity of 2+ million MT/yr and expected completion in the 2026-2027 timeframe, this plant will materially alter the Texas supply-demand balance and may create overcapacity concerns in the state.

**White cement significance for SESCO analysis:** Lehigh White Cement's 140,000 MT/yr capacity is insufficient to fully serve US white cement demand, which is estimated at 350,000-500,000 MT/yr nationally. The plant's Texas location imposes significant freight costs for serving Florida and Southeast customers -- trucking from Waco to Tampa adds $65-80/MT to delivered cost, compared to $8-10/MT from a Tampa Bay import terminal. This freight differential creates the structural competitive opening that SESCO and other importers exploit in the white cement segment.

**Texas new plant impact:** The Mitchell County greenfield plant is being closely watched by the industry. If it achieves its targeted 2+ million MT/yr capacity, it would add approximately 10% to Texas state capacity at a time when the state already has approximately 18 million MT of production capacity against 22 million MT of consumption. While the 4 million MT deficit still requires imports, the new plant could reduce import demand and pressure pricing in the Texas market.

### 6.2.5 Ash Grove Cement / CRH Group

**Capacity:** Approximately 8 million metric tons per year (US operations)
**Plants:** 7 integrated plants
**Parent:** CRH plc (Ireland)
**Headquarters (US):** Overland Park, Kansas

CRH acquired Ash Grove Cement in 2018, adding one of the most respected US cement brands to its global building materials portfolio. The Ash Grove operations, now integrated under CRH's Americas Materials division, operate a geographically diversified plant network spanning the Pacific Northwest, Midwest, South Central, and Southeast regions.

| Plant | Location | Estimated Capacity (MT/yr) | Market Served |
|-------|----------|---------------------------|---------------|

| Montana City | Montana City, MT | 1.0M | Montana, Northern Rockies |

**Competitive position:** Ash Grove/CRH's Florida operations are particularly relevant to the Southeast competitive landscape. The Sumterville and Branford plants together provide 2.2 million MT of Florida-based production capacity, making CRH the third-largest producer in the state behind Argos USA and CEMEX. However, both plants are located in rural North-Central Florida, at some distance from the high-demand Tampa Bay and South Florida markets, creating a freight disadvantage relative to the Argos Tampa plant and coastal import terminals.

**CRH integration:** CRH's acquisition of Ash Grove brought the US cement operations into a global building materials conglomerate with $32+ billion in annual revenue. CRH's scale provides advantages in procurement, capital investment, and sustainability initiatives, but the integration has also introduced bureaucratic complexity to what was historically a nimble, regionally focused cement company. CRH's broader US operations include Oldcastle APG (concrete products), Oldcastle BuildingEnvelope, and Oldcastle Infrastructure, creating cross-selling opportunities but also the integrated supplier tension noted for other vertically integrated producers.

### 6.2.6 Eagle Materials

**Capacity:** Approximately 6 million metric tons per year
**Plants:** 5 integrated plants
**Parent:** Eagle Materials Inc. (Dallas, TX -- publicly traded)
**Headquarters:** Dallas, Texas

Eagle Materials is a pure-play US building products company with significant cement and wallboard operations. The company's cement plants are concentrated in the central US, serving the high-growth Texas and Midwest markets.

| Plant | Location | Estimated Capacity (MT/yr) | Market Served |
|-------|----------|---------------------------|---------------|
| Buda | Buda, TX (near Austin) | 1.5M | Central Texas |
| LaSalle | LaSalle, IL | 1.5M | Chicago, Northern Illinois |
| Sugar Creek | Sugar Creek, MO | 1.2M | Kansas City, Missouri |
| Laramie | Laramie, WY | 0.6M | Wyoming, Mountain West |
| Tulsa | Tulsa, OK | 0.7M | Oklahoma, South Central |

**Competitive position:** Eagle Materials is notable for its financial discipline and focus on returns on invested capital. The company's Buda, Texas plant is well-positioned to serve the Austin and San Antonio markets, two of the fastest-growing metros in the US. The LaSalle, Illinois plant competes in the highly contested Chicago market, where Holcim and Buzzi Unicem are also significant players.

**Publicly traded advantage:** As the only major US publicly traded pure-play cement company (distinct from the diversified building materials conglomerates), Eagle Materials provides the most transparent financial disclosure in the industry. The company's quarterly earnings reports serve as a bellwether for US cement pricing and demand trends.

### 6.2.7 Martin Marietta Materials

**Capacity:** Approximately 5 million metric tons per year (combination of limited domestic production and import terminal capacity)
**Plants:** Limited domestic production; primary cement operations through import terminals
**Parent:** Martin Marietta Materials Inc. (Raleigh, NC -- publicly traded)
**Headquarters:** Raleigh, North Carolina

Martin Marietta is primarily the nation's second-largest aggregates producer but has built a significant cement import operation, particularly through its Tampa Bay terminal operations and other Gulf Coast and Atlantic terminals. The company imported 4.1 million MT through US ports during the 2023-2025 period, making it the largest cement importer in the country.

**Competitive position:** Martin Marietta's competitive strategy in cement is distinctive. Rather than investing in domestic kilns, the company leverages its extensive aggregates distribution network and ready-mix operations to bundle cement imports with aggregates deliveries to captive and independent customers. This bundling strategy creates switching costs -- customers receiving aggregates, cement, and logistics services from a single provider face significant operational disruption in moving cement purchases to an alternative supplier.

**Import strategy:** Martin Marietta sources cement primarily from Turkey and Greece, benefiting from the favorable 10% tariff rate on Mediterranean-origin cement. The company's Tampa Bay terminal is one of the highest-volume cement import facilities in the Southeast, with an estimated throughput capacity of 4.5 million MT/yr and storage capacity exceeding 100,000 MT.

## 6.3 Import Terminal Operators

Import terminals have become critical infrastructure in the US cement supply chain, serving as the physical interface between waterborne imports and inland distribution networks. The following profiles cover the major import terminal operators relevant to Gulf Coast and Southeast markets.

### 6.3.1 SESCO Cement Corporation

**Terminal:** Houston Ship Channel (primary), Port Redwing, Gibsonton, FL (under commissioning)
**Parent:** SESCO Group (Egypt)
**Import volume:** 1.99 million MT (2023-2025, Houston)
**Import rank:** #3 nationally by volume

SESCO operates the world's largest privately owned single-user cement import terminal in Houston (22 acres, 150,000 MT storage, with expansion targets for 2025). The Houston terminal serves as the hub for SESCO's Gulf Coast distribution, with approximately 70% of volume distributed by truck within a 150-mile radius and 30% moving by rail to Dallas, San Antonio, and Oklahoma markets. SESCO's sources include Royal El Minya White Cement (Egypt) and Nuh Cement gray Portland (Turkey), both at the favorable 10% tariff rate. A detailed analysis of SESCO's competitive position appears in Chapter 9 of this report.

### 6.3.2 Argos USA Import Operations

**Terminals:** Savannah, GA; Mobile, AL; Newbern, AL
**Parent:** Cementos Argos S.A. (Colombia)
**Import volume:** 1.09 million MT (2023-2025)
**Integration:** Supplements domestic production at Tampa, FL and Newberry, FL plants

Argos USA uses its import terminal network to supplement domestic production and serve markets beyond the reach of its Florida plants. The Savannah terminal supplies Georgia and the Carolinas, while the Newbern and Mobile terminals serve Alabama and Mississippi markets. Argos benefits from competitive Colombian-origin cement at 10% tariff rates, leveraging its parent company's production base.

### 6.3.3 Votorantim/VCNA Cement

**Terminals:** Multiple Florida locations
**Parent:** Votorantim Cimentos S.A. (Brazil)

VCNA (Votorantim Cement North America) operates cement grinding and distribution facilities in the US, with a particular focus on the Southeast. The company imports clinker from Brazilian parent operations and grinds finished cement at US terminals, benefiting from lower tariff rates on clinker versus finished cement in certain product categories.

### 6.3.4 CEMEX Import Terminals

**Terminals:** Houston, TX; Mobile, AL; multiple Gulf Coast locations
**Integration:** Supplements domestic production network

CEMEX operates import terminals at several Gulf Coast ports to supplement its six-plant US production network. The company's import operations historically relied heavily on intra-company transfers from Mexican production facilities, but the 2025 tariff increase to 25% on Mexican imports has forced a strategic rebalancing toward Mediterranean and other origin sources. CEMEX's Houston terminal operations compete directly with SESCO in the Texas import market.

## 6.4 Regional Competitive Dynamics

### 6.4.1 Texas: Capacity Expansion and Overcapacity Risk

Texas is the largest cement market in the US, with estimated consumption of 22 million MT/yr against in-state production capacity of approximately 18 million MT/yr. The 4 million MT annual deficit has historically been served by imports through the Port of Houston and cross-border flows from Mexico.

**Current producer landscape:**

| Producer | Texas Plants | Estimated Capacity (MT/yr) |
|----------|-------------|---------------------------|
| CEMEX | Balcones, Odessa | 5.0M |
| Holcim | Midlothian | 3.0M |
| Buzzi/RC Lonestar | Maryneal | 2.0M |
| Eagle Materials | Buda | 1.5M |
| TXI/Martin Marietta | Midlothian | 1.5M |
| Lehigh Hanson | New plant (construction) | 2.0M+ |
| Other | Various | 3.0M |

The Lehigh Hanson greenfield plant in Mitchell County, targeting 2+ million MT/yr capacity, introduces the prospect of overcapacity in Texas for the first time in decades. Combined with weakening import economics from Mexico (25% tariff) and robust domestic capacity, the Texas market may shift from structural deficit to approximate balance or even modest surplus by 2027-2028. This shift would intensify price competition and could pressure margins for both domestic producers and import terminal operators in the Houston market.

**Competitive implications for importers:** Houston-based importers including SESCO, CEMEX, and others face a potentially tightening Texas market. Import terminal operators may need to redirect volume to other Gulf Coast and Southeast markets or develop export capabilities to maintain throughput utilization. SESCO's Tampa expansion can be understood partly as a geographic diversification strategy to reduce concentration risk in the Texas market.

### 6.4.2 Florida: Import-Dependent Growth Market

Florida presents the inverse of the Texas dynamic. The state is the 4th largest producer but the 2nd largest consumer, creating a structural supply deficit of approximately 3.6 million MT/yr that must be met through imports. Florida's import dependency is further amplified by the fact that actual production (at 72% utilization) likely yields only 7.5 million MT against 14 million MT of consumption, pushing effective import reliance to approximately 46% of state consumption -- more than double the national average.

**Florida producer landscape:**

| Producer | Plants | Capacity (MT/yr) | Florida Market Share |
|----------|--------|------------------|---------------------|
| Argos USA | Tampa, Newberry | 3.5M | 34% of production capacity |
| CEMEX | Brooksville, Miami | 2.6M | 25% |
| Ash Grove/CRH | Sumterville, Branford | 2.2M | 21% |
| Titan America | Pennusco (Miami) | 2.1M | 20% |

All four Florida producers operate integrated ready-mix businesses, creating the vertically integrated supplier dynamic that advantages independent importers. Tampa Bay receives 75% of Florida's cement imports (6.8 million MT through Tampa Bay, Tampa, and Port Manatee combined), making it the import gateway for the state and a strategically critical market for any import-focused competitor.

**Competitive dynamics:** The Florida market is characterized by integrated producer dominance in local production, offset by a large import-dependent segment that supplies independent ready-mix producers and spot demand. Independent importers compete on price, service, and the absence of downstream vertical conflict. White cement represents a specialty niche with limited domestic supply (Lehigh White in Texas is the only producer), creating a differentiated market entry path for importers with Mediterranean or Egyptian sourcing.

### 6.4.3 Midwest: Barge-Rail Competition Corridor

The Midwest cement market is served by a combination of domestic plants (Holcim, Buzzi, Eagle Materials) and waterborne supply moving by barge on the Mississippi and Ohio River systems. The Lower Mississippi corridor handles approximately 5 million tons of cement per year, with barge rates of $6-12/ton for 500-1,500 mile movements offering a significant cost advantage over rail ($12-25/ton for 300-800 miles) and truck ($8-15/ton for 50-100 miles).

**Key competitive dynamics in the Midwest:**

- **Barge-accessible markets** (St. Louis, Memphis, New Orleans corridor) are highly competitive, as multiple producers can deliver via river at similar freight costs.
- **Inland markets** beyond barge reach (Indianapolis, Chicago suburbs, Minneapolis) rely on rail and truck, where proximity to production assets creates stronger local competitive moats.
- **Import competition** is limited in the Midwest interior, as the cost of barging or railing imported cement from Gulf Coast terminals adds $15-30/ton in freight, partially eroding the CIF price advantage of imports.
- **Seasonal constraints** on the Upper Mississippi (ice closure December-March) create seasonal supply dynamics that favor local producers with rail-connected terminals.

### 6.4.4 California: Constrained Supply, Premium Pricing

California is the third-largest cement consuming state with approximately 13 million MT of annual demand, served by approximately 10 million MT of in-state production from Holcim, CEMEX, CalPortland, Lehigh Hanson, and others. The state's stringent environmental regulations, including cap-and-trade carbon pricing and South Coast Air Quality Management District (SCAQMD) rules, create the highest production costs in the US and correspondingly the highest ex-plant pricing ($155-175/ton versus the national average of $140-160/ton).

Import competition in California comes primarily through the Ports of Long Beach and Los Angeles, with Asian-origin cement (Vietnam, China, Thailand) facing elevated tariffs and Section 301 vessel fees that partially offset lower FOB prices. California's premium pricing and constrained supply create opportunities for import terminal operators, though the regulatory and community opposition to new industrial facilities near the ports limits terminal development.

### 6.4.5 Northeast: High-Cost, Import-Accessible

The Northeast market (New York, New Jersey, Pennsylvania, New England) features the highest delivered cement prices in the US, driven by a combination of limited local production, high labor and transportation costs, and strong infrastructure demand. Import terminals in the Port of New York/New Jersey, Baltimore, and Boston serve approximately 15-20% of regional demand, primarily sourced from Turkey and Greece.

## 6.5 Market Concentration and Competitive Dynamics

### 6.5.1 National Concentration

The US cement industry has experienced steady consolidation over the past three decades, driven by cross-border acquisitions as European and Latin American multinationals acquired domestic producers. The major transactions include:

| Year | Transaction | Acquirer | Significance |
|------|------------|----------|--------------|
| 2005 | Holnam acquisition | Lafarge | Created largest US producer |
| 2015 | LafargeHolcim merger | Holcim | Consolidated #1 position |
| 2018 | Ash Grove acquisition | CRH | Removed major independent |
| 2019 | US Concrete acquisition | Adbri (later Eagle Materials) | Vertical integration |
| 2020-23 | Various terminal acquisitions | Multiple | Import infrastructure consolidation |

The result is a market where the top 5 producers control approximately 60% of US capacity and the top 10 control approximately 82%. However, the presence of imports -- representing 22% of consumption -- provides a competitive check on domestic pricing power that would not exist in a purely domestic market.

### 6.5.2 Competitive Forces Assessment

**Porter's Five Forces -- US Cement Industry:**

| Force | Intensity | Assessment |
|-------|-----------|------------|
| **Rivalry among existing competitors** | Moderate-High | Regional concentration, price competition in surplus markets |
| **Threat of new entrants** | Low (domestic) / Moderate (imports) | High capital barriers domestically; import terminals lower barrier |
| **Bargaining power of buyers** | Moderate | Large ready-mix chains have leverage; small buyers price-takers |
| **Bargaining power of suppliers** | Low | Limestone abundant; energy costs market-driven |
| **Threat of substitutes** | Low | No viable substitute for Portland cement in concrete |

**Key competitive dynamics shaping the market:**

1. **Integrated vs. independent tension.** The major producers (Holcim, CEMEX, Argos, Titan) all operate ready-mix concrete businesses that compete with the independent ready-mix producers who are also their cement customers. This structural conflict creates a market opening for non-integrated suppliers, whether domestic or import-based, that can credibly position themselves as partners rather than competitors to independent buyers.

2. **Tariff-driven origin economics.** The complex US tariff regime creates significant cost differentials among import origins. The 10% rate for Turkey, Greece, Egypt, and Saudi Arabia versus 25% for Canada and Mexico and 46% for Vietnam produces a tiered competitive landscape where Mediterranean and Middle Eastern sources enjoy a structural cost advantage of $12-27/MT on tariffs alone.

3. **Section 301 vessel fee uncertainty.** The Section 301 maritime fees targeting Chinese-built vessels introduce a new variable that could add $16-33/ton to import costs for affected shipments. Importers operating on non-Chinese-built tonnage gain a significant advantage, while those relying on spot-market chartering of Chinese-built bulkers face material cost increases.

4. **Regional supply-demand shifts.** The Lehigh Hanson Texas plant, the only greenfield project of scale in the US, could shift Texas from deficit to balance. Simultaneously, the Southeast and Florida markets continue to experience growing import dependency. These shifts will reallocate competitive pressure and may trigger strategic repositioning by import terminal operators.

### 6.5.3 Competitive Outlook: 2026-2030

The competitive landscape is expected to evolve along several axes through the end of the decade:

**Continued import share growth.** Absent a significant wave of domestic capacity additions -- which current economics do not support outside of Texas -- the import share of US consumption is projected to increase from 22% toward 25-28% by 2030. This trend favors import terminal operators and disfavors high-cost domestic producers.

**Consolidation in import operations.** The fragmented import terminal sector is likely to see consolidation as larger operators acquire smaller terminals to build scale and geographic coverage. Companies with both production and import capabilities (CEMEX, Argos, Titan) will continue to develop hybrid supply chains.

**Sustainability as competitive differentiator.** Federal green procurement standards under the Buy Clean California Act model and emerging EPA embodied carbon rules will increasingly favor producers and importers who can demonstrate lower carbon footprints. This could advantage newer, more efficient plants and importers sourcing from modern production facilities.

**White cement market expansion.** As sustainable architecture and energy-efficient building design gain traction, white cement demand for reflective facades and aesthetically driven construction is projected to grow at 3-5% annually, faster than gray cement demand. The limited domestic production base (Lehigh White Cement at 140,000 MT/yr) ensures continued reliance on imports for this premium segment.

## 6.6 Chapter Summary

The US cement competitive landscape is defined by moderate national concentration (top 5 at 60% share) overlaid with high regional concentration in specific markets. The major multinational producers -- Holcim, CEMEX, Lehigh Hanson/Heidelberg, CRH/Ash Grove, and Buzzi/RC Lonestar -- dominate domestic production but face growing competition from import terminal operators who can deliver waterborne cement at landed costs below domestic ex-plant pricing in coastal markets. The import segment, led by Martin Marietta, SESCO, and several producer-affiliated terminal operations, has become a structural feature of the market rather than a cyclical supplement. Regional dynamics vary sharply: Texas faces potential overcapacity from the Lehigh greenfield plant; Florida remains chronically import-dependent; the Midwest is contested between barge-rail corridors and local producers; and California commands premium pricing under environmental regulatory constraints. For importers, the combination of favorable tariff positioning (10% for Mediterranean/Middle Eastern origins), non-integrated supplier status, and specialty product capability (white cement) provides competitive entry paths even in a concentrated market.
