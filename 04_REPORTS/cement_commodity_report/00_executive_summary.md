The United States cement market stands at a critical inflection point, shaped by converging forces of constrained domestic capacity, evolving trade policy, declining supplementary cementitious material (SCM) availability, and unprecedented federal infrastructure investment. This commodity report provides a comprehensive analysis of the US cement and cementitious materials supply chain, from primary production through terminal distribution, with particular focus on import dynamics, SCM market transitions, and Mississippi River corridor logistics.

## Market Snapshot

The US cement market consumed approximately 110 million metric tons in 2024, valued at roughly $17 billion at the producer/importer level. Domestic production from 99 plants across 34 states and Puerto Rico reached approximately 86 million metric tons of Portland and blended cement, representing a capacity utilization rate of approximately 72% against a nameplate capacity of 120 million metric tons. The gap between domestic production and consumption was filled by imports totaling 19.8 million metric tons, representing a 22% import penetration rate -- a historically elevated share reflecting both constrained domestic capacity and competitive landed pricing from overseas producers.

The top four producing states -- Texas, Missouri, California, and Florida -- account for 43% of national output. Texas and Florida also rank as the two largest consuming states, driven by population growth, residential construction, and infrastructure spending. The Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA), signed in November 2021, continues to inject federal dollars into highways, bridges, and water systems, sustaining demand in the medium term even as the broader residential construction cycle has softened.

## Import Dynamics and Trade Policy

The import supply chain faces a transformative regulatory development. Section 301 maritime fees targeting Chinese-built vessels, announced in early 2025 and phased in through 2026, add an estimated $16--33 per ton to waterborne cement imports carried on Chinese-constructed bulkers. Given that Chinese-built vessels carry an estimated 40--50% of global dry bulk tonnage, the impact on cement import economics is significant and asymmetric. Importers relying on spot-market bulk carriers will bear the highest cost increases, while those with long-term charter arrangements on non-Chinese-built tonnage gain a structural advantage.

Turkey dominates US cement imports with 7.16 million metric tons (approximately 36% of import volume), followed by Canada at 4.85 million metric tons and Vietnam at 4.17 million metric tons. Egypt, Greece, and Mexico round out the top six origins. The tariff landscape is complex: Turkey and Greece face a 10% duty rate; Canada and Mexico are subject to 25% tariffs under Section 232 actions; Vietnam faces 46% combined duties under antidumping and countervailing duty orders. These rate differentials, combined with Section 301 vessel fees, are actively reshaping origin economics and trade flows.

## SCM Market Transition

The supplementary cementitious materials market, valued at approximately $28 billion globally, is undergoing a structural transformation in the United States. Fly ash -- the single most widely used SCM -- faces secular supply decline as coal-fired power plant retirements accelerate. Over 200 US coal plants have retired since 2010, removing the primary source of fresh fly ash. US fly ash consumption of 15--20 million tons per year increasingly depends on harvested (landfilled) ash, with companies such as EMT/Eco Material Technologies processing legacy coal combustion residuals. Fly ash prices have doubled from $30/ton to $60--80/ton over the past decade, fundamentally altering the economics of blended cement and concrete mix design.

Ground granulated blast furnace slag (GGBFS) supply remains constrained by the declining US integrated steelmaking base, with production concentrated at a handful of blast furnace operations. Natural pozzolans -- including volcanic deposits in Nevada and New Mexico, and calcined clays under the emerging LC3 (Limestone Calcined Clay Cement) technology -- represent a growing but still nascent alternative. The SCM transition has implications for cement demand: as SCM availability declines or costs rise, concrete producers may increase their Portland cement content, effectively tightening an already constrained cement supply chain.

## Supply Chain and Logistics

Cement logistics in the US rely on a multimodal network of truck, rail, and barge transportation. While truck dominates last-mile delivery (accounting for 98% of cement moves from terminal to end user), long-haul movements depend heavily on barge and rail. The Mississippi River system is the backbone of interior US cement distribution, with approximately 5 million tons per year moving by barge through the Lower Mississippi corridor. Import terminals at Houston, New Orleans, Tampa, and other Gulf and Atlantic ports serve as the entry points for waterborne cement, which is then distributed inland by barge or rail.

Terminal infrastructure -- including pneumatic unloading systems, bulk silo storage, and truck/rail loadout facilities -- represents critical bottleneck capacity. Import terminals typically operate at 400 tons per hour unloading rates, with silo storage costing $3--5 per ton per month. The integration of vessel-to-barge lightering, barge-to-terminal transfer, and terminal-to-truck distribution creates a complex chain with multiple cost and capacity constraints.

## SESCO Cement: A Case Study in Niche Positioning

SESCO Cement Corporation exemplifies the emerging model of specialized import operations in the US cement market. Sourcing white cement from Egyptian producers (Royal El Minya, Cleopatra) and gray cement from Turkish mills (Nuh Cimento), SESCO operates through Houston-area terminal infrastructure and is expanding into the Florida market. The US white cement market is severely supply-constrained, with only a single domestic producer (Lehigh White Cement in Texas, 140,000 MT/year capacity). White cement commands a 2--3x price premium over gray ($180--280/ton versus $79--95/ton), driven by demand from stucco/EIFS manufacturers, tile adhesive producers, and architectural precast fabricators.

SESCO's dual-origin sourcing strategy provides supply security, while its favorable tariff position (10% on both Egyptian and Turkish cement) and established import logistics create a defensible competitive position. The company's expansion into Florida targets the second-largest cement-consuming state in the US, where hurricane building codes, coastal construction requirements, and demographic growth sustain strong demand for both white and gray cement products.
