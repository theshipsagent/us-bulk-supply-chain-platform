# Chapter 7: Pricing & Cost Analysis

## US Cement & Cementitious Materials Commodity Report

**US Bulk Supply Chain Reporting Platform**
**Report Period:** Q1 2026

---

Cement pricing in the United States reflects a complex interplay of domestic production costs, freight economics, import landed costs, and regional supply-demand balances. As of early 2026, domestic ex-plant prices range from $140 to $160 per ton nationally, while import prices average $79 per ton CIF -- a divergence that underpins the structural competitiveness of waterborne imports in coastal and Gulf markets. This chapter provides a detailed analysis of cement pricing structures, regional price variations, delivered cost models for both domestic and imported cement, and the freight economics that determine competitive reach for producers, import terminal operators, and distribution networks. Particular attention is given to the cost models relevant to Gulf Coast import operations, including proforma vessel cost analysis, rail corridor economics, and the tariff regime that shapes origin-country competitiveness.

## 7.1 Domestic Cement Pricing

### 7.1.1 National Price Levels

US cement pricing is generally quoted on an ex-plant or ex-terminal basis, representing the cost of cement loaded onto the buyer's truck at the production facility or distribution terminal, excluding delivery freight. As of Q1 2026, national average ex-plant pricing falls in the $140-160 per ton range for Type I/II Portland cement, reflecting incremental price increases of 3-5% per year over the past several years.

**National Ex-Plant Price Trend:**

| Year | Avg. Ex-Plant Price ($/ton) | YoY Change | Key Driver |
|------|----------------------------|------------|------------|
| 2020 | $120-130 | +2-3% | Pandemic demand softness |
| 2021 | $125-135 | +4-5% | Demand recovery, energy costs |
| 2022 | $130-145 | +6-8% | Inflation pass-through, energy spike |
| 2023 | $135-150 | +4-5% | Continued inflation, tight supply |
| 2024 | $140-160 | +3-5% | Stabilization at elevated levels |
| 2025 (est) | $142-162 | +1-3% | Moderating inflation, competitive pressure |

**Price announcement cycle:** Major US cement producers typically announce annual price increases in December-January for implementation in the following spring (April-May). The 2025 price increase cycle saw announcements of $8-12/ton increases from major producers, though actual realized increases were closer to $4-8/ton after market negotiations and competitive adjustments. The 2026 cycle is expected to see similar announced increases of $8-10/ton with realized increases of $3-6/ton, reflecting the moderating inflationary environment.

### 7.1.2 Regional Price Variation

Cement pricing varies significantly by region, driven by local supply-demand balances, production costs, freight economics, and competitive intensity. The following table presents estimated ex-plant or ex-terminal pricing by major market region:

**Regional Ex-Plant/Ex-Terminal Pricing (Q1 2026, Type I/II Portland Cement):**

| Region | Price Range ($/ton) | Key Drivers | Competitive Intensity |
|--------|--------------------|-----------|-----------------------|
| **Southeast (FL, GA, AL, SC)** | $130-145 | Import competition, high capacity, multiple producers | High |
| **Midwest (IL, IN, OH, MI, MO)** | $135-150 | Moderate supply, barge/rail competition, seasonal demand | Moderate |
| **Texas** | $125-140 | High capacity, import competition, new plant under construction | High |
| **California** | $155-175 | Environmental costs, constrained supply, regulatory burden | Low |
| **Northeast (NY, NJ, PA, NE)** | $150-170 | Limited production, high input costs, strong demand | Low-Moderate |
| **Pacific Northwest** | $145-160 | Limited producers, moderate demand | Low |
| **Mountain West** | $140-155 | Remote production, limited competition, freight barriers | Low |
| **South Central (OK, AR, LA)** | $130-145 | Multiple producers, barge access, moderate demand | Moderate |

**Drivers of regional price differentiation:**

1. **California premium ($155-175/ton).** California's pricing reflects the highest domestic production costs, driven by cap-and-trade carbon pricing ($30+ per ton of CO2, translating to $25-30 per ton of cement in carbon cost), stringent emissions regulations under SCAQMD rules, and high labor costs. The state's environmental regulatory burden adds an estimated $15-25/ton to production costs relative to Midwest or Southern plants.

2. **Texas discount ($125-140/ton).** The Texas market has historically featured lower pricing due to competition among six domestic producers (CEMEX, Holcim, Eagle, Buzzi, Lehigh, Martin Marietta), robust import supply through Houston, and the absence of carbon pricing. The impending addition of the Lehigh Hanson greenfield plant (2+ million MT/yr) is expected to maintain competitive pricing pressure through the end of the decade.

3. **Southeast moderation ($130-145/ton).** Southeast pricing is held in check by import competition, particularly in Florida where waterborne cement from Turkey, Greece, and Egypt enters at landed costs of $70-85/ton. Domestic producers must price competitively against imports to maintain market share, effectively capping ex-plant realizations.

4. **Northeast premium ($150-170/ton).** Limited local production, high transportation costs, and strong infrastructure demand -- particularly in the New York City metro area -- support premium pricing. Import terminals in the Port of New York/New Jersey and Baltimore provide some competitive pressure but face higher port costs than Gulf Coast facilities.

**Tariff regime as competitive determinant:** The tariff landscape creates a tiered competitive structure among import origins. Turkey, Greece, Egypt, Saudi Arabia, and Colombia benefit from the 10% Most Favored Nation (MFN) rate, providing a structural cost advantage of $12-27/MT over Canadian and Mexican imports (25% rate) and Vietnamese imports (46% combined AD/CVD rate). This tariff differential is the single most important factor in the current reshaping of US cement import origin patterns.

## 7.3 Delivered Cost Structure: Freight Mode Economics

### 7.3.1 Truck Freight

Truck freight is the dominant last-mile delivery mode for cement, accounting for approximately 98% of terminal-to-end-user movements. Cement is transported in pneumatic bulk trailers with a typical payload of 25-27 metric tons.

**Truck Freight Cost Schedule (Cement, Pneumatic Bulk Trailer):**

| Distance | Drive Time | Cost Per Ton | Cost Per Load (26 MT) | Trips/Day | Notes |
|----------|-----------|-------------|----------------------|-----------|-------|
| 0-25 miles | <1 hour | $6-8 | $156-208 | 3-4 | Short haul, multiple trips |
| 25-50 miles | 1-1.5 hours | $8-12 | $208-312 | 2-3 | Standard local delivery |
| 50-100 miles | 1.5-3 hours | $12-18 | $312-468 | 1-2 | Extended local |
| 100-150 miles | 3-4 hours | $18-25 | $468-650 | 1 | Day trip with return |
| 150-200 miles | 4-5 hours | $25-35 | $650-910 | 1 | Full day, possible overnight |
| 200-300 miles | 5-7 hours | $35-50 | $910-1,300 | 1 | Overnight required |

**Truck freight cost drivers:**

1. **Driver compensation.** The ongoing driver shortage has pushed per-mile compensation up 15-20% since 2020. Pneumatic trailer operators command a premium over standard flatbed or van drivers due to specialized training requirements.

2. **Fuel costs.** Diesel at $3.50-4.00/gallon translates to approximately $0.55-0.65/mile in fuel cost for a loaded cement trailer, representing roughly 30-35% of total truck operating cost.

3. **Empty miles.** Cement trucks frequently return empty (deadhead), as bulk pneumatic trailers have limited backhaul capability. This effectively doubles the per-mile cost for cement delivery versus commodities with balanced freight flows.

4. **Loading/unloading time.** Cement loading takes 20-30 minutes at a modern terminal with automated systems; unloading at customer silos takes 30-45 minutes. This dwell time reduces daily truck productivity and increases per-load cost, particularly for short-haul deliveries where dwell time represents a larger proportion of total trip time.

### 7.3.2 Rail Freight

Rail freight serves the long-haul segment of cement distribution, typically for movements of 300-800+ miles from production facilities or import terminals to inland distribution terminals. Cement moves in covered hopper cars, typically carrying 100-110 tons per car.

| Distance | Cost Per Ton | Transit Time | Minimum Carload | Notes |
|----------|-------------|--------------|-----------------|-------|
| 200-400 miles | $12-16 | 2-4 days | 1 car / 100 tons | Short-haul rail, limited advantage vs. truck |
| 400-600 miles | $16-20 | 3-5 days | 5+ cars | Mid-range, competitive with truck |
| 600-800 miles | $20-25 | 4-7 days | 10+ cars (unit train) | Long-haul, clear rail advantage |
| 800-1,200 miles | $22-28 | 5-10 days | 25+ cars (unit train) | Major corridor movements |
| 1,200+ miles | $25-35 | 7-14 days | 50+ cars (unit train) | Cross-country, limited for cement |

Rail costs from Houston to Dallas (approximately 250 miles) average $12-16/ton, adding to the landed terminal cost of $66-79/ton for a delivered cost of $78-95/ton at Dallas-area rail terminals. This is competitive with domestic ex-plant pricing in the Dallas market ($135-145/ton delivered from local plants minus truck freight of $8-12/ton = $123-137/ton net).

### 7.3.3 Barge Freight

Barge freight offers the lowest per-ton-mile cost for cement distribution, approximately one-third the cost of rail and one-tenth the cost of truck on a per-ton-mile basis. However, barge distribution is limited to the Mississippi/Ohio River system and the Gulf Intracoastal Waterway (GIWW).

**Barge Freight Cost Structure (Cement, Covered Barge):**

| Distance | Cost Per Ton | Transit Time | Barge Load | Notes |
|----------|-------------|--------------|------------|-------|
| 200-500 miles | $6-8 | 3-5 days | 1,500 tons/barge | Short barge haul |
| 500-1,000 miles | $8-12 | 5-10 days | 1,500 tons/barge | Standard barge movement |
| 1,000-1,500 miles | $10-15 | 8-14 days | 1,500 tons/barge | Long-haul barge |
| 1,500-2,000 miles | $12-18 | 12-18 days | 1,500 tons/barge | Upper Mississippi to Gulf |

**USDA Grain Transportation Report (GTR) barge rate benchmarks:**

The USDA GTR publishes weekly barge rate indices for the Mississippi River system. While primarily focused on grain movements, barge rates for cement and other dry bulk commodities closely track the grain barge market because the covered barge fleet is shared across commodity types.

| GTR Region | Dec 2025 Rate Index (% of tariff) | Implied $/ton for Cement | Route Example |
|------------|----------------------------------|-------------------------|---------------|
| Mid-Mississippi | 350-400% | $8-10/ton | St. Louis to Memphis |
| Lower Mississippi | 375-425% | $10-12/ton | Memphis to New Orleans |
| Ohio River | 325-375% | $7-9/ton | Louisville to Cairo |
| Upper Mississippi | 375-450% | $10-14/ton | Minneapolis to St. Louis |
| GIWW | 300-350% | $8-10/ton | Houston to New Orleans |

**Barge fleet constraints:** The US covered barge fleet of 13,321 barges (2023) is at a historic high, but 22% of the fleet is 25+ years old, and new barge construction has slowed since 2015. Covered barges are essential for cement (moisture protection), and competition for covered barge capacity from grain, fertilizer, and salt movements creates seasonal rate volatility. Spring planting season (March-May) and fall harvest (September-November) are peak grain barge demand periods, driving up rates and reducing availability for cement movements.

**Florida limitation:** Florida has no navigable inland waterway connection to the Mississippi/Ohio River system. Tampa-based cement distribution relies exclusively on ocean vessel (for imports), truck (for local and regional delivery), and rail (for longer-haul movements via CSX). Barge freight economics are not directly applicable to the Florida market but are relevant for Gulf Coast and Midwest distribution from Houston and New Orleans terminals.

## 7.5 Section 301 Maritime Fee Impact on Import Costs

### 7.5.1 Fee Structure

The Section 301 maritime fees targeting Chinese-built vessels, announced in early 2025, impose per-voyage charges on vessels built in Chinese shipyards calling at US ports. For bulk carriers in the cement trade, the relevant fee structure is as follows:

| Vessel Category | Fee Range ($/voyage) | Effective Date | Phase-in |
|-----------------|---------------------|----------------|----------|
| Chinese-built dry bulk carrier | $500,000 - $1,000,000 | 2025-2026 | Phased by vessel age/class |
| Non-Chinese-built carrier | $0 | N/A | Exempt |

**Per-ton impact on cement imports:**

| Vessel Size | Cargo (MT) | Fee (Low) | Fee (High) | Per-Ton Impact (Low) | Per-Ton Impact (High) |
|-------------|-----------|-----------|-----------|---------------------|----------------------|
| Handysize 38K | 30,000 | $500,000 | $1,000,000 | $16.67 | $33.33 |
| Supramax 58K | 45,000 | $500,000 | $1,000,000 | $11.11 | $22.22 |

### 7.5.2 Market Impact Assessment

**Magnitude of impact:** At $16-33/ton for a 30,000 MT Handysize cargo, the Section 301 fee would increase landed import costs by 20-45% above current ocean freight rates of $5.50-8.00/ton. This is a potentially transformative cost increase that could fundamentally alter import economics for affected shipments.

**Asymmetric impact:** The critical variable is whether the carrying vessel was built in China. Approximately 40-50% of the global dry bulk fleet was constructed in Chinese shipyards, meaning the probability of a spot-market chartered vessel being Chinese-built is significant. However, importers with dedicated charter arrangements or COAs (Contracts of Affreightment) specifying non-Chinese tonnage can avoid the fee entirely, creating a structural advantage for sophisticated import operators.

**SESCO exposure assessment:** SESCO's vessel fleet and charter arrangements require evaluation for Chinese-built exposure. If SESCO vessels are Chinese-built, the Section 301 fee would add $16-33/ton to landed costs, materially reducing or eliminating the tariff-driven cost advantage over Canadian and Mexican imports. If SESCO can secure non-Chinese-built tonnage, it retains its full competitive advantage and benefits from the fee's impact on competitors who cannot avoid Chinese-built vessels.

### 7.7.2 Pricing Sensitivity to Key Variables

**Import landed cost sensitivity (base case: Turkish gray cement to Houston, $75/ton delivered):**

| Variable | Change | Impact on Landed Cost | New Landed Cost |
|----------|--------|----------------------|-----------------|
| FOB price | +$5/ton | +$5.50/ton (includes 10% tariff) | $80.50 |
| Ocean freight | +$2/ton | +$2.00/ton | $77.00 |
| Tariff rate (10% to 25%) | +15 ppts | +$7.50/ton | $82.50 |
| Section 301 fee (Chinese-built) | +$500K/voyage | +$16.67/ton | $91.67 |
| FX rate (USD weakens 10%) | -10% USD | +$5-7/ton | $80-82 |
| Truck freight (50 mi) | +$3/ton | +$3.00/ton | $78.00 |

**Key risks to import economics:**

1. **Tariff escalation.** If Turkey or Egypt tariff rates are increased from 10% to 25% (as occurred with Canada and Mexico), the landed cost impact is $7-8/ton, reducing but not eliminating the competitive advantage over domestic ex-plant pricing.

2. **Section 301 vessel fees.** If applied to SESCO or competitor vessels, the $16-33/ton impact would materially alter competitive positioning. Non-Chinese-built tonnage becomes a strategic imperative.

3. **Freight rate volatility.** While forward rates indicate softening, geopolitical disruption (Red Sea rerouting, Suez Canal closure) could spike freight rates by $5-15/ton, as occurred during the 2021-2022 period.

4. **Domestic price correction.** If the Lehigh Hanson Texas plant and other capacity additions trigger domestic price compression, the import cost advantage narrows, potentially making marginal import terminal operations uneconomic.

## 7.8 Chapter Summary

US cement pricing exhibits substantial regional variation, from $125-140/ton in competitive Texas markets to $155-175/ton in regulatory-constrained California. The $140-160/ton national average for domestic ex-plant pricing compares to import landed costs of $65-90/ton delivered within 50 miles of major Gulf Coast terminals -- a $50-95/ton gap that sustains the 22% import penetration rate. The tariff regime is the dominant competitive variable in import economics, creating a $12-27/ton advantage for 10%-rate origins (Turkey, Egypt, Greece) over 25-46%-rate origins (Canada, Mexico, Vietnam). Delivered cost modeling confirms that imports cannot displace local production on a cost basis but are competitive with other importers and advantaged in the import-dependent segment of coastal markets. White cement pricing at $200-280/ton import versus $280-350/ton domestic production provides a margin-rich specialty segment for differentiated importers. The Section 301 vessel fee introduces significant uncertainty, with potential per-ton impacts of $16-33 on Chinese-built vessels that could reshape import economics for affected operators.
