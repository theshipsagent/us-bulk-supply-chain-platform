# Chapter 8: Demand Drivers & Outlook

## US Cement & Cementitious Materials Commodity Report

**US Bulk Supply Chain Reporting Platform**
**Report Period:** Q1 2026

---

US cement demand is driven by a convergence of legislative, demographic, and macroeconomic forces that point toward sustained growth of 2-3% annually through 2030, despite cyclical headwinds from elevated interest rates and residential construction softness. The Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA) alone is injecting $1.2 trillion in federal spending into highways, bridges, water systems, and broadband, with cement-intensive project types reaching peak spending velocity in the 2025-2028 period. Concurrently, the CHIPS and Science Act and the Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) are catalyzing semiconductor fabrication facility and clean energy construction that represents new demand categories for cement. Regional demand hotspots -- led by Texas, Florida, and the broader Southeast -- are amplified by population migration patterns, manufacturing reshoring, and energy infrastructure investment. This chapter analyzes the leading indicators, federal policy drivers, regional demand dynamics, and risk factors that shape the cement consumption outlook through the end of the decade.

## 8.1 National Demand Overview

### 8.1.1 US Cement Consumption Trends

US cement consumption in 2024 reached approximately 110 million metric tons, valued at roughly $17 billion. While this represented a 6% decline from 2023 levels, reflecting the lagged impact of interest rate increases on residential construction and general construction softness, the medium-term outlook remains positive due to the unprecedented volume of federal infrastructure spending now entering the execution phase.

**US Cement Consumption Trend (2018-2026):**

| Year | Consumption (million MT) | YoY Change | Key Drivers |
|------|------------------------|------------|-------------|
| 2018 | 99 | +2.5% | Strong residential, infrastructure |
| 2019 | 101 | +2.0% | Continued growth, commercial expansion |
| 2020 | 95 | -5.9% | COVID-19 pandemic shutdown |
| 2021 | 103 | +8.4% | Rapid recovery, residential boom |
| 2022 | 117 | +13.6% | Peak residential, infrastructure spending begins |
| 2023 | 117 | +0.0% | Rate impacts begin, flat activity |
| 2024 | 110 | -6.0% | Residential pullback, commercial softness |
| 2025 (est) | 112-115 | +2-4% | Infrastructure ramp-up, residential stabilization |
| 2026 (proj) | 115-120 | +3-4% | IIJA peak spending, manufacturing construction |

**Portland Cement Association (PCA) baseline forecast:** The PCA projects US cement consumption growth of 2-3% annually through 2030, driven by federal infrastructure spending, manufacturing facility construction, and gradual residential recovery as interest rates moderate. Cumulative consumption over the 2025-2030 period is projected at 690-720 million MT, compared to 642 million MT over the 2019-2024 period -- representing a 7-12% increase in the six-year consumption total.

### 8.1.2 Consumption by End-Use Sector

Cement consumption is distributed across construction sectors with varying growth trajectories and cement intensity characteristics:

**US Cement Consumption by End-Use Sector (2024 Estimated):**

| Sector | Share of Consumption | Volume (million MT) | Trend | Key Drivers |
|--------|---------------------|--------------------|----|-------------|
| **Ready-mix concrete** | 72-75% | 79-83 | Stable | Broad construction demand |
| **Concrete products** | 12-14% | 13-15 | Growing | Infrastructure, precast growth |
| **Contractors (direct)** | 6-8% | 7-9 | Growing | Highway, bridge, IIJA projects |
| **Oil well cement** | 3-4% | 3-4 | Cyclical | Permian Basin drilling activity |
| **Masonry/other** | 4-6% | 4-7 | Stable | Residential, Florida/Southeast |

**Sector-specific outlook:**

**Ready-mix concrete (72-75% share):** The dominant end-use sector encompasses residential, commercial, industrial, and infrastructure construction. Ready-mix demand is correlated with total construction spending, which the US Census Bureau and Dodge Construction Network project at $1.8-2.0 trillion annually through 2028. Ready-mix demand is expected to grow at 2-3% annually, tracking overall construction spending.

**Concrete products (12-14% share):** Precast/prestressed concrete, concrete masonry units (CMUs), pipe, and pavers form a growing segment driven by infrastructure spending (precast bridge beams, highway barriers) and commercial construction (precast parking structures, architectural panels). This segment is projected to grow at 3-5% annually, outpacing overall cement demand growth.

**Infrastructure/contractors (6-8% share):** Direct cement purchases by large infrastructure contractors for highway paving, bridge construction, and heavy civil projects are the fastest-growing end-use segment, driven directly by IIJA spending. Growth of 5-8% annually is projected as federal project awards reach peak execution in 2026-2028.

## 8.2 Federal Policy Demand Drivers

### 8.2.1 Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA)

The Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act, signed November 15, 2021, authorized $1.2 trillion in total infrastructure spending, including $550 billion in new spending above baseline levels. The Act represents the largest federal infrastructure investment in a generation and is the single most significant demand driver for US cement through 2030.

**IIJA Cement-Relevant Spending Categories:**

| Category | Authorized Spending | Peak Execution Period | Cement Intensity | Estimated Annual Cement Demand Impact |
|----------|-------------------|---------------------|-----------------|--------------------------------------|
| **Highways & bridges** | $350 billion | 2025-2028 | Very High | 8-12 million MT/yr incremental |
| **Transit & rail** | $89 billion | 2025-2029 | High | 2-3 million MT/yr |
| **Water infrastructure** | $55 billion | 2025-2028 | Moderate-High | 1-2 million MT/yr |
| **Broadband** | $65 billion | 2025-2027 | Low | <0.5 million MT/yr |
| **Airports** | $25 billion | 2025-2028 | Moderate | 0.5-1 million MT/yr |
| **Ports & waterways** | $17 billion | 2025-2029 | Moderate | 0.3-0.5 million MT/yr |
| **Power grid** | $73 billion | 2025-2030 | Low-Moderate | 0.5-1 million MT/yr |

**Total estimated annual cement demand from IIJA: 13-20 million MT/yr at peak (2026-2028)**

**IIJA spending velocity:** Federal infrastructure dollars flow through a multi-year cycle of authorization, appropriation, obligation, and expenditure. As of Q1 2026, approximately $450 billion of the $1.2 trillion total has been obligated, with actual construction spending -- the stage that generates cement demand -- concentrated in the 2025-2028 window. The Federal Highway Administration (FHWA) has obligated $150+ billion for highway and bridge projects, with state departments of transportation now awarding construction contracts at record rates.

**Highway and bridge spending impact:** The highways and bridges category is the most cement-intensive IIJA spending stream. Concrete pavement on highways consumes approximately 300-500 tons of cement per lane-mile, while bridge construction requires 200-800 tons per bridge depending on span and design. The FHWA's bridge replacement program alone has identified 45,000+ structurally deficient bridges requiring repair or replacement, representing decades of sustained cement demand.

The CHIPS and Science Act, signed August 9, 2022, authorizes $280 billion in spending, including $52.7 billion in direct subsidies for semiconductor manufacturing and research. The Act has catalyzed a wave of semiconductor fabrication facility (fab) construction that represents a new and significant cement demand category.

**Cement intensity of semiconductor fabs:** A modern semiconductor fabrication facility requires massive concrete foundations and structural elements to support vibration-isolated cleanroom environments, heavy equipment loads, and extensive utility infrastructure. Estimated cement consumption per major fab ranges from 50,000 to 150,000 MT, with construction timelines of 2-4 years per facility. The concentration of fab construction in Arizona, Texas, Ohio, and New York creates localized demand surges that may strain regional cement supply.

The Inflation Reduction Act, signed August 16, 2022, authorized $369 billion in clean energy and climate spending over ten years. The Act's provisions for clean energy manufacturing, renewable power generation, and electric vehicle infrastructure create cement demand through facility construction.

Importers should maintain Houston terminal operations to serve the baseline import need while monitoring the new plant's ramp-up and market impact.

## 8.7 Risk Factors

### 8.7.1 Interest Rate Sensitivity

Cement demand is correlated with construction spending, which is in turn sensitive to interest rates through the mechanisms of mortgage rates (residential construction), commercial borrowing costs (commercial/industrial construction), and municipal bond rates (public infrastructure). The Federal Reserve's monetary policy trajectory is the single largest risk factor for near-term cement demand.

**Interest rate impact model:**

| Rate Scenario | Impact on Housing Starts | Impact on Cement Demand | Probability (2026) |
|---------------|------------------------|------------------------|-------------------|
| Fed cuts 100+ bps | +10-15% housing starts | +3-5% total cement demand | 30% |
| Fed cuts 50 bps | +5-8% housing starts | +1-3% total cement demand | 40% |
| Fed holds | Flat housing starts | Flat to +1% cement demand | 20% |
| Fed raises | -5-10% housing starts | -1-3% total cement demand | 10% |

### 8.7.2 Labor Shortages

The construction industry faces a chronic labor shortage estimated at 500,000-650,000 unfilled positions nationally. Labor constraints limit the pace of construction execution regardless of funding availability, potentially delaying IIJA project timelines and dampening the translation of authorized spending into actual cement demand. Skilled trades -- including concrete workers, heavy equipment operators, and specialized labor for infrastructure projects -- are particularly scarce.

**Impact on cement demand:** Labor shortages may extend construction timelines by 15-25%, effectively spreading cement demand over a longer period rather than reducing it. The total volume of cement consumed for a given project remains the same, but the annual demand rate may be lower than projected if projects take longer to complete.

### 8.7.3 Aggregate Supply Constraints

Concrete production requires approximately 6-7 tons of aggregate (sand, gravel, crushed stone) for every ton of cement. In several high-demand regions -- including South Florida, the San Francisco Bay Area, and parts of the Northeast -- aggregate supply is constrained by permitting difficulties, community opposition to quarry operations, and depletion of local deposits. These aggregate constraints can limit concrete production and, by extension, cement consumption even when cement is available.

### 8.7.4 SCM Market Transition

The declining availability and rising cost of supplementary cementitious materials (fly ash, slag) could paradoxically increase cement demand. As concrete producers face difficulty sourcing affordable fly ash and GGBFS to replace a portion of the cement in concrete mixes, they may increase Portland cement content to maintain concrete performance. A 5% reduction in average SCM replacement ratio across the US concrete industry would increase cement demand by approximately 5-6 million MT/yr -- a significant swing factor.

### 8.7.5 Policy and Regulatory Risk

**IIJA funding risk:** While the IIJA has been signed into law and significant funds have been obligated, future congressional action could rescind unspent appropriations or redirect funds to other priorities. A change in administration or fiscal austerity measures could slow the pace of federal infrastructure spending.

**Trade policy risk:** Tariff changes -- whether escalation on currently low-tariff origins or reduction on currently high-tariff origins -- could reshape import economics and alter the competitive balance between domestic and imported cement. The Section 301 vessel fee introduces additional policy uncertainty for the import segment.

**Environmental regulation:** Tightening EPA regulations on cement kiln emissions, potential federal carbon pricing, and state-level Buy Clean requirements could increase domestic production costs and either accelerate import growth or constrain domestic output.

## 8.8 Chapter Summary

US cement demand is supported by a historically strong pipeline of federal infrastructure spending (IIJA, CHIPS, IRA), manufacturing reshoring, and sustained population growth in key consuming states. The PCA and platform-level forecasts project 2-3% annual consumption growth through 2030, with imports growing faster at 4-6% annually as domestic capacity additions lag demand growth. Regional hotspots -- Texas (22M MT/yr, #1), Florida (14M MT/yr, #2), and the Southeast manufacturing corridor -- represent the highest-opportunity markets for both domestic producers and import terminal operators. Risk factors include interest rate sensitivity, labor shortages, and policy uncertainty, but the base-case outlook remains constructive for the cement supply chain. The import share of US consumption is projected to increase from 22% to 25-26% by the end of the decade, creating a favorable structural environment for import-focused operators with competitive sourcing and efficient terminal operations.
