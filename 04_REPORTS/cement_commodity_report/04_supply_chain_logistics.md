# Chapter 4: Supply Chain & Logistics

**US Bulk Supply Chain Reporting Platform -- Cement & Cementitious Materials Commodity Report**

---

The US cement supply chain operates as a multimodal network in which virtually all last-mile delivery occurs by truck, while long-haul movement depends on barge and rail to bridge the geographic gap between production/import points and consumption markets. Approximately 98% of cement reaches the end user by pneumatic tanker truck, but the economics of inland distribution are dominated by barge (Mississippi River system, Intracoastal Waterway, Houston Ship Channel) and rail (100-ton covered hopper cars in unit train and manifest service). Import logistics add a further layer of complexity: oceangoing vessels discharge at coastal terminals via pneumatic or gravity systems at 400+ tons per hour, with onward distribution by barge, rail, or direct truck loadout. Terminal operations -- encompassing silo storage ($3--5/ton/month), truck loading (up to 150 trucks per day at peak), and inventory management -- represent the critical bottleneck infrastructure linking production to consumption. This chapter analyzes each transportation mode, terminal operations, and the cost structure of cement logistics.

---

## 4.1 Transportation Mode Overview

### Modal Split

| Mode | Share of Ton-Miles | Typical Distance | Capacity per Unit | Cost per Ton-Mile | Primary Use |
|------|-------------------|-----------------|-------------------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Truck** | ~45% of ton-miles | 0--150 miles | 25--27 tons/load | $0.15--0.25 | Last-mile delivery |
| **Rail** | ~30% of ton-miles | 200--1,500 miles | 100 tons/car | $0.03--0.06 | Cross-country, plant-to-terminal |
| **Barge** | ~20% of ton-miles | 100--1,500 miles | 1,500 tons/barge | $0.01--0.03 | River system, coastal |
| **Vessel** | ~5% of ton-miles | International | 25,000--60,000 tons | $0.005--0.015 | Import delivery |

Note: While truck accounts for approximately 98% of cement delivery transactions (by count), the ton-mile distribution reflects the much longer distances covered by rail and barge. A single 15-barge tow moving 22,500 tons of cement 1,000 miles on the Mississippi River generates more ton-miles than thousands of 25-ton truck deliveries within a 50-mile radius.

## 4.2 Truck Transportation

### Equipment

Cement is transported by truck in **pneumatic tanker trailers** (also called "bulk cement trailers" or "pressure differential trailers"). Key specifications:

| Specification | Value |
|--------------|-------|
| Trailer Type | Pneumatic tanker (pressure differential) |
| Payload Capacity | 25--27 tons (50,000--54,000 lbs) |
| Compartments | Typically 2--3 (front, center, rear) |
| Unloading Method | Compressed air (15--20 psi) |
| Unloading Time | 30--45 minutes |
| Typical Service Radius | 50--150 miles from terminal/plant |
| Driver Certification | CDL Class A, tanker endorsement |

### Truck Rate Structure

Cement truck rates are typically structured as:

1. **Per-ton delivered** -- Most common for ready-mix plant deliveries. Rates include loading, hauling, and unloading. Typical range: $8--15/ton for 0--50 miles, $15--30/ton for 50--100 miles, $30--60/ton for 100--200 miles.

2. **Hourly** -- Used for short-haul, high-frequency deliveries (e.g., plant-to-ready-mix within 30 miles). Typical range: $85--120/hour.

3. **Contract (annual)** -- Negotiated rates for guaranteed volumes. Typically 10--20% below spot rates.

### Truck Logistics Challenges

- **Driver shortage** -- The pneumatic tanker segment faces acute driver shortages due to the specialized skill required (pressure differential unloading, bulk handling safety).
- **Hours of service** -- DOT hours-of-service regulations limit daily driving to 11 hours, constraining effective service radius.
- **Seasonal peaks** -- Summer construction season creates severe truck capacity shortages in major markets (Texas, Florida, California), with spot rates spiking 30--50% above contracted levels.
- **Loading queue times** -- At busy terminals, trucks may wait 1--3 hours for loading during peak periods, reducing effective daily trips.

### Unit Train Operations

For major plant-to-terminal movements, cement moves in **unit trains** -- dedicated trains of 80--110 cars moving from a single origin to a single destination without intermediate switching:

| Unit Train Metric | Value |
|------------------|-------|
| Typical Train Size | 100 cars |
| Total Payload | 10,000 tons |
| Typical Route | Plant → destination terminal |
| Transit Time (1,000 miles) | 4--7 days |
| Rate (per ton, 1,000 miles) | $20--35 |
| Annual Volume Commitment | 100,000--500,000 tons |
| Origin Infrastructure | Loop track, 110-car capacity |
| Destination Infrastructure | Loop track, rapid unloading facility |

### Manifest (Carload) Service

Smaller volumes move in **manifest service** -- individual cars or small blocks of cars attached to scheduled freight trains:

| Manifest Metric | Value |

| Typical Shipment | 1--25 cars |
| Transit Time (1,000 miles) | 7--14 days |
| Rate Premium vs Unit Train | 30--60% higher per ton |
| Service Reliability | Lower (subject to switching delays) |

### Barge Rate Structure

Barge rates for cement are typically quoted as:

- **Per ton, origin-destination** -- Most common. Includes towage, fleeting, and barge hire.
- **Government Transportation Request (GTR) basis** -- Some military/government cement moves use standard GTR rate schedules.
- **Spot vs. contract** -- Spot rates fluctuate seasonally (+30--50% in peak summer/fall); annual contracts provide rate stability at a modest premium to annual average spot.

### Barge vs. Rail Cost Comparison: Memphis Delivery

A practical cost comparison for cement delivery to Memphis, Tennessee -- a major inland distribution point -- illustrates the modal economics:

| Cost Element | Barge (ex-NOLA import) | Rail (ex-Missouri plant) | Truck (ex-nearest plant) |
|-------------|----------------------|-------------------------|------------------------|
| Origin | NOLA import terminal | Ste. Genevieve MO | Nearest regional plant |
| Distance | ~700 river miles | ~350 rail miles | ~150 road miles |
| Transit Time | 5--8 days | 3--5 days | 1 day |
| Freight Rate | $14--18/ton | $12--18/ton | $35--50/ton |
| Terminal Handling (origin) | $3--5/ton | Incl. in rate | N/A |
| Terminal Handling (dest.) | $4--6/ton | $3--5/ton | N/A |
| Storage (30-day avg) | $3--5/ton | $3--5/ton | N/A |
| **Total Delivered** | **$24--34/ton** | **$18--28/ton** | **$35--50/ton** |

Key observations from the Memphis analysis:
- **Rail has a cost advantage** for the Missouri plant-to-Memphis movement due to the shorter distance and lower terminal handling.
- **Barge is competitive** for NOLA-origin import cement, particularly when vessel-to-barge transfer avoids a second terminal handling step.
- **Truck is prohibitively expensive** for distances beyond 100 miles, confirming the 50--150 mile effective truck radius.
- **The choice between barge and rail depends on the origin** -- domestic plant cement favors rail; import cement favors barge through the river system.

## 4.5 Vessel Operations (Import Chain)

### Vessel Types for Cement Imports

| Vessel Type | DWT Range | Cement Capacity | Typical Routes | Draft |
|------------|-----------|-----------------|---------------|-------|
| Handysize | 25,000--40,000 | 20,000--35,000 tons | Med→Gulf, Asia→Pacific | 9--11 m |
| Supramax | 40,000--60,000 | 35,000--55,000 tons | Turkey→Houston, Greece→Tampa | 11--13 m |
| Panamax | 60,000--80,000 | 50,000--70,000 tons | Limited (draft constraints) | 13--15 m |

Most US cement imports arrive on Handysize and Supramax bulkers, reflecting the draft limitations at many US cement terminals and the cargo lot sizes that match market demand cycles. A typical Supramax cargo of 40,000--50,000 tons represents approximately 2--3 weeks of consumption for a mid-size terminal operation.

### Vessel Unloading

Cement is discharged from bulk carriers using one of two primary methods:

| Method | Rate (TPH) | Equipment | Cost ($/ton) | Notes |
|--------|-----------|-----------|--------------|-------|
| **Pneumatic (ship-mounted)** | 300--500 | Self-unloading system | $3--5 | Preferred for dedicated cement vessels |
| **Crane + clamshell + hopper** | 200--400 | Shore crane or ship gear | $4--7 | Standard for spot/tramp vessels |
| **Combined (pneumatic + crane)** | 400--600 | Both systems | $4--6 | Maximizes discharge rate |

### Import Terminal Flow

The standard import cement flow from vessel to end user involves multiple handling steps:

```
VESSEL (25,000-50,000 tons)
    │
    ├─── Direct discharge to TERMINAL SILO (pneumatic, 400 TPH)
    │         │
    │         ├─── TRUCK loadout (25-27 tons, 150 trucks/day peak)
    │         │         └─── READY-MIX PLANT / END USER
    │         │
    │         ├─── RAIL loadout (100-ton hopper car)
    │         │         └─── INLAND TERMINAL → TRUCK → END USER
    │         │
    │         └─── BARGE loadout (1,500 tons/barge)
    │                   └─── RIVER TERMINAL → TRUCK → END USER
    │
    └─── LIGHTERING to BARGE (vessel-to-barge transfer at anchorage)
              │
              └─── RIVER TERMINAL → TRUCK → END USER
```

### Lightering Operations

When vessels cannot reach terminal berths due to draft restrictions or when cargo is destined for inland river terminals, **lightering** (vessel-to-barge transfer) occurs at anchorage:

| Lightering Metric | Value |
|------------------|-------|
| Typical Location | Ship anchorage (e.g., Galveston Outer Bar, Southwest Pass) |
| Transfer Rate | 150--300 TPH |
| Equipment | Floating crane or vessel gear + barge alongside |
| Cost | $5--8/ton (including barge positioning) |
| Weather Sensitivity | High (sea state, wind constraints) |
| Use Cases | Oversize vessel for terminal draft; inland river destination; split cargo |

### Terminal Capacity Constraints

Terminal capacity is the binding constraint in many US cement markets, particularly coastal import-dependent regions:

- **Silo capacity** -- Limited silo volume constrains inventory, requiring more frequent vessel calls and reducing buffer against supply disruptions.
- **Truck loading throughput** -- The number of truck loading positions and loading rate (15--20 minutes per truck) determines the maximum daily truck count. A terminal with 4 loading positions can serve approximately 120--150 trucks per day.
- **Vessel berth access** -- Many terminals have single-berth access, requiring scheduled vessel arrivals and creating congestion during peak import seasons.
- **Draft limitations** -- Shallow-draft terminals (less than 35 feet) restrict vessel size to Handysize, limiting cargo lot efficiency.
- **Environmental permits** -- Dust emissions, stormwater management, and noise restrictions limit operating hours and expansion potential at many urban terminals.

## 4.8 Logistics Outlook

### Capacity Constraints and Investment Needs

The US cement logistics system faces several capacity constraints that will require investment over the 2025--2030 period:

| Constraint | Impact | Investment Required | Timeline |
|-----------|--------|--------------------|----|
| Terminal silo capacity | Limits import throughput in coastal markets | $50--100M industry-wide | 2025--2028 |
| Truck driver availability | Restricts last-mile delivery capacity | Industry recruitment/retention programs | Ongoing |
| Barge fleet age | Average barge age ~20 years; retirement > newbuilds | $500M+ for fleet replacement | 2025--2035 |
| Lock infrastructure (USACE) | Delays on aging lock systems reduce barge throughput | Federal investment (IIJA-funded) | 2025--2040 |
| Rail car availability | Periodic shortages of covered hopper cars | Car leasing/purchase by producers | As needed |

### Technology Trends

- **GPS/IoT tracking** -- Real-time silo level monitoring and truck tracking improving inventory management and reducing stockouts.
- **Automated truck loading** -- Emerging systems for unattended truck loading at terminals, extending operating hours and reducing labor costs.
- **Barge tracking** -- AIS-based barge tracking integrated with terminal management systems for improved arrival planning.
- **Pneumatic system upgrades** -- Higher-efficiency blowers and improved pipeline design increasing unloading rates at terminals.
