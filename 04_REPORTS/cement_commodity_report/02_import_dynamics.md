# Chapter 2: Import Dynamics

**US Bulk Supply Chain Reporting Platform -- Cement & Cementitious Materials Commodity Report**

---

US cement imports totaled 19.8 million metric tons in 2024, valued at $2.43 billion, representing approximately 22% of domestic consumption. Turkey dominates the import market at 7.16 million metric tons (~36% share), followed by Canada (4.85 MT), Vietnam (4.17 MT), Greece (1.82 MT), and Mexico (1.32 MT). The trade landscape is stratified by tariff regimes -- Turkey and Greece at 10%, Canada and Mexico at 25% under Section 232, Vietnam at 46% under combined ADD/CVD orders -- creating divergent landed cost economics by origin. The transformative development reshaping this market is the Section 301 maritime fee framework targeting Chinese-built vessels, which carry an estimated 40--50% of waterborne cement shipments. These fees, adding $16--33 per ton to affected cargoes, are fundamentally altering vessel chartering decisions, trade route economics, and the competitive positioning of import origins relative to domestic production.

---

## 2.1 Import Volume and Share

### Historical Context

Cement imports have historically fluctuated between 10% and 25% of US consumption, driven by the domestic supply-demand balance and the relative cost competitiveness of imported product. The post-2010 period saw a structural increase in import dependence as several domestic plants were permanently closed during the Great Recession and not replaced with new capacity.

|------|--------------------|--------------------|-----------------|-------------------------|
| 2014 | 99.4 | 10.5 | 10.6% | $92 |
| 2016 | 97.5 | 11.0 | 11.3% | $88 |
| 2018 | 102.0 | 13.0 | 12.7% | $85 |
| 2020 | 101.0 | 14.5 | 14.4% | $82 |
| 2022 | 113.0 | 18.0 | 15.9% | $86 |
| 2023 | 117.0 | 20.5 | 17.5% | $84 |
| 2024 | 110.0 | 19.8 | 18.0% | $79 |

Note: Import share calculated as imports divided by total shipments (domestic + imports). USGS reports total shipments of approximately 110 MT for 2024, with imports constituting ~22% of that figure when measured against apparent consumption (domestic shipments + imports - exports).

### Product Classification

Cement imports enter under HTS Chapter 2523, with the following subheadings:

| HTS Code | Description | MFN Duty Rate | 2024 Volume |
|----------|-------------|---------------|-------------|
| 2523.10 | Cement clinker | Free | ~3.0 MT |
| 2523.21 | White Portland cement | Free | ~0.5 MT |
| 2523.29 | Other Portland cement (gray) | Free | ~14.5 MT |
| 2523.30 | Aluminous cement | Free | Minimal |
| 2523.90 | Other hydraulic cements | Free | ~1.8 MT |

The base MFN duty rate for cement is 0% (duty-free), which means that all tariff costs on cement imports arise from trade remedy orders (ADD/CVD), Section 232 actions, or other special tariff programs -- not from standard customs duties.

### Turkey: Dominant Import Origin

Turkey has consolidated its position as the #1 cement exporter to the United States, supplying 7.16 million metric tons in 2024 -- more than one-third of all imports. Key factors:

- **Massive overcapacity** -- Turkey has approximately 100 million metric tons of cement production capacity against domestic consumption of ~65 million metric tons, leaving 30--35 million metric tons available for export.
- **Favorable geography** -- Turkish ports on the Aegean and Mediterranean offer 18--25 day transit times to US Gulf and East Coast ports, competitive with most alternatives.
- **10% tariff rate** -- Among the lowest applicable tariff rates for major exporting countries.
- **Established logistics** -- Turkish producers have developed dedicated export infrastructure, including specialized cement loading terminals at Iskenderun, Mersin, and Canakkale.

Key Turkish exporters to the US include Cimsa (Sabanci Group), Limak Cimento, Oyak Cimento, Nuh Cimento, and Cementir (Italcementi). SESCO Cement sources gray cement from Nuh Cimento for the Houston market.

### Egypt: Emerging High-Growth Origin

Egypt's cement exports to the US grew 71% year-over-year in 2024, driven by:

- **Severe domestic overcapacity** -- Egypt has ~90 million metric tons of capacity against domestic demand of ~50 million metric tons, following a massive military-backed capacity buildout.
- **Competitive FOB pricing** -- Egyptian producers offer among the lowest FOB prices globally ($60--80/ton for gray, $85--100/ton for white).
- **White cement specialization** -- Egypt is one of the world's largest white cement producers, with Royal El Minya Cement, Sinai White Cement, and Cleopatra Cement operating dedicated white cement kiln lines.
- **10% tariff rate** -- Same favorable rate as Turkey and Greece.
- **Suez Canal access** -- Egyptian ports offer efficient routing through Suez to the Atlantic and Gulf of Mexico.

SESCO Cement is a key driver of US-Egypt cement trade, sourcing white cement from Royal El Minya and Cleopatra Cement for distribution through Houston and (planned) Florida terminals.

### Historical Trade Remedy Cases

The US cement industry has a long history of antidumping and countervailing duty cases:

- **Mexico (1990--2006)** -- Antidumping order imposed 1990, duties ranging 30--60%. CEMEX was the primary respondent. The order was revoked in the mid-2000s as part of a settlement agreement, with CEMEX committing to price undertakings.
- **Japan (historical)** -- ADD order on cement from Japan, now revoked.
- **Vietnam (ongoing)** -- Combined ADD/CVD duties of approximately 46% imposed on Vietnamese cement. Vietnamese producers including Ha Tien Cement and Vicem remain subject to these orders, which significantly limit import competitiveness.
- **China (ongoing)** -- Multiple trade remedy orders on Chinese cement effectively block commercial imports from China.

### Section 232 Steel/Aluminum Extension to Cement Inputs

While cement itself is not directly subject to Section 232 tariffs, the Section 232 actions on steel and aluminum have an indirect impact on cement import infrastructure costs (terminal equipment, silo fabrication, pneumatic systems). The 25% tariff on Canadian and Mexican cement reflects broader North American trade tensions rather than cement-specific trade remedy investigations.

## 2.5 Section 301 Maritime Fees: Game-Changing Impact

### Background

The Section 301 maritime fee framework, developed by the Office of the US Trade Representative (USTR) and implemented through MARAD/CBP, targets Chinese-built and Chinese-operated vessels calling at US ports. The fees, announced in April 2025 with phased implementation beginning October 2025, represent the most significant disruption to US waterborne bulk import economics since the original Section 232 tariffs.

### Fee Structure for Bulk Carriers

| Category | Vessel Type | Fee Basis | Estimated Impact on Cement ($/ton) |
|----------|-------------|-----------|-----------------------------------|
| Chinese-built, Chinese-operated | Bulker | Per net tonnage per US port call | $25--33/ton |
| Chinese-built, non-Chinese-operated | Bulker | Per net tonnage per US port call | $16--25/ton |
| Non-Chinese-built, Chinese-operated | Bulker | Reduced rate | $8--15/ton |
| Non-Chinese-built, non-Chinese-operated | Bulker | Exempt | $0/ton |

### Impact on Cement Imports

The Section 301 vessel fees have disproportionate impact on the cement import trade for several structural reasons:

1. **High Chinese-built vessel share** -- Approximately 40--50% of global dry bulk vessel capacity was built in Chinese shipyards. For Handysize and Supramax bulkers commonly used in cement trades (25,000--60,000 DWT), the Chinese-built share may be even higher.

2. **Low-value-to-weight ratio** -- Cement is among the lowest-value bulk commodities by weight ($79/ton average import price). A $16--33/ton fee represents a 20--42% surcharge on the CIF price, compared to much smaller percentage impacts on higher-value cargoes.

3. **Single-cargo discharge** -- Cement bulkers typically discharge their full cargo at one or two US ports, concentrating the per-call fee impact on the single commodity.

4. **Spot market vulnerability** -- Importers chartering vessels on the spot market cannot guarantee non-Chinese-built tonnage availability, particularly during peak shipping seasons.

### Strategic Implications

The Section 301 fees create several strategic dynamics in the cement import market:

- **Vessel premium for non-Chinese tonnage** -- Charterers are paying $2,000--5,000/day premiums for confirmed non-Chinese-built Handysize/Supramax bulkers, compressing the economic advantage of Chinese-built vessel discounts that previously existed.
- **Long-term charter incentives** -- Importers who secure time charters on non-Chinese-built vessels gain a structural cost advantage over spot-market operators.
- **Origin shift potential** -- Canada (overland) and Mexico (short-sea with potential non-Chinese tonnage) may gain relative competitiveness versus trans-oceanic origins that depend on Chinese-built tonnage.
- **Terminal operator advantage** -- Operators with dedicated non-Chinese-built vessel contracts (or vessel ownership) can offer lower landed costs to customers, creating competitive differentiation.
- **SESCO positioning** -- SESCO Cement's established relationships with Turkish and Egyptian producers, combined with Houston terminal infrastructure, position the company to navigate the Section 301 environment by securing non-Chinese-built vessel tonnage for its dedicated trade lanes.

5. **Section 301 vessel fees can eliminate the import cost advantage** entirely for cargoes on Chinese-built vessels, particularly from Vietnam.

## 2.8 Import Market Outlook

### Near-Term (2025--2026)

- **Section 301 implementation** -- Phased fee implementation through 2026 will progressively shift vessel chartering toward non-Chinese-built tonnage, increasing freight rates industry-wide but differentially impacting importers based on their fleet access.
- **Origin diversification continues** -- Expect continued growth from Egypt, Thailand, Spain, and Saudi Arabia as importers diversify away from Vietnam (high tariff) and seek multiple sourcing options.
- **Turkey maintains dominance** -- Turkey's combination of massive overcapacity, competitive pricing, favorable tariff position, and established logistics ensures continued dominance of US imports.
- **Terminal investment** -- New terminal capacity under development in Florida, Texas, and the Southeast to accommodate growing import volumes and provide Section 301-compliant unloading infrastructure.

### Medium-Term (2027--2030)

- **Vietnamese imports decline** -- The combination of 46% ADD/CVD duties and potential Section 301 vessel fees makes Vietnamese cement increasingly uncompetitive. Volume likely to shift to Turkey, Egypt, and emerging origins.
- **Canadian imports constrained** -- 25% Section 232 tariff limits Canadian imports to cross-border markets unless trade policy changes.
- **New trade routes** -- Indian Ocean origins (India, Bangladesh) and West African origins (Nigeria) may emerge as US importers seek new supply sources, though these remain speculative.
- **Integration of import and domestic supply chains** -- Major producers increasingly blend domestic production with imported clinker/cement at coastal terminals, blurring the line between "domestic" and "imported" product.
