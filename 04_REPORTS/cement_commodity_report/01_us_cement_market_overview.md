# Chapter 1: US Cement Market Overview

**US Bulk Supply Chain Reporting Platform -- Cement & Cementitious Materials Commodity Report**

---

The United States cement industry comprises 99 active production plants across 34 states and Puerto Rico, generating approximately 86 million metric tons of Portland and blended cement annually against a nameplate capacity of 120 million metric tons. The market is dominated by a half-dozen multinational producers operating regionally concentrated kiln systems, with Texas, Missouri, California, and Florida accounting for 43% of national output. Consumption of approximately 110 million metric tons per year, valued at $17 billion, is sustained by residential construction, commercial development, and federal infrastructure investment under the IIJA. Import penetration stands at roughly 22%, filling the persistent gap between domestic capacity and demand. Ex-plant prices in the $140--160/ton range reflect tightening supply conditions, while capacity utilization of approximately 72% masks significant regional variation between surplus and deficit markets.

---

## 1.1 Industry Structure

### Production Base

The US cement industry operates through a network of 99 integrated and grinding plants distributed across 34 states and Puerto Rico. According to EPA Facility Registry Service (FRS) data, 437 facilities carry NAICS code 327310 (Cement Manufacturing -- Portland, Hydraulic), a figure that includes grinding-only operations, blending facilities, and some legacy registrations alongside active clinker-producing kilns. The core production base of ~99 plants represents the active integrated facilities (those with operational kilns producing clinker) plus standalone grinding plants that purchase clinker for finish grinding.

Cement manufacturing is among the most capital-intensive industries in the United States. A modern dry-process kiln line with a capacity of 1--2 million tons per year requires $300--500 million in capital investment and 3--5 years for permitting and construction. This capital intensity creates high barriers to entry and explains the oligopolistic market structure, where a handful of multinational companies control the vast majority of domestic capacity.

### Capacity and Utilization

| Metric | 2024 Value |
|--------|-----------|
| Total Nameplate Capacity | ~120 MT/year |
| Portland/Blended Cement Production | 84 MT |
| Masonry Cement Production | 2.2 MT |
| Total Cement Production | ~86 MT |
| Capacity Utilization | ~72% |
| Year-over-Year Production Change | Down 4% |

The 72% capacity utilization figure reported by USGS for 2024 is below the 85--92% range that characterized the 2017--2022 expansion cycle. This decline reflects both a softening in residential construction starts and planned kiln maintenance outages at several major facilities. However, headline utilization figures can be misleading: some plants operate at or near full capacity while others sit idle or run at reduced rates. In high-demand markets such as Texas and Florida, effective capacity utilization exceeds 90%, while plants in lower-demand regions may operate below 60%.

Several plants have been idled or permanently closed in the past decade, removing capacity from the system. Conversely, a small number of expansion projects and kiln upgrades have added incremental capacity. The net effect has been a slow tightening of the domestic supply-demand balance, with imports filling the structural deficit in coastal and high-growth markets.

### Industry Consolidation Trends

The US cement industry has undergone significant consolidation over the past two decades. Key transactions include:

- **CRH plc acquisition of Ash Grove Cement** (2018) -- Gave CRH a major US cement platform in the central and western states.
- **Summit Materials acquisition of Argos USA** (2024) -- Created a larger Southeast-focused cement producer with operations in Alabama, Florida, and South Carolina.
- **Martin Marietta acquisition of Texas Industries** (2014) -- Added cement manufacturing to Martin Marietta's aggregates-focused portfolio, concentrated in the high-growth Texas market.
- **Holcim capacity expansions** -- Holcim's Ste. Genevieve, Missouri plant (commissioned 2009) remains the largest single kiln line in the US at approximately 4.5 million tons per year capacity.

The consolidation trend reflects the capital intensity of the industry and the advantages of scale in procurement, logistics, and market access. Independent producers have largely been absorbed into multinational platforms, with Eagle Materials and CalPortland among the few remaining mid-size operators.

### Regional Supply-Demand Balance

The US cement market exhibits significant regional variation in the supply-demand balance:

- **Surplus regions** -- Missouri, Kansas, Oklahoma, and Indiana produce more cement than their local markets consume, exporting to neighboring states via rail and barge.
- **Deficit regions** -- Florida, the Gulf Coast, the Eastern Seaboard, and parts of the West Coast consume substantially more than local production, relying on imports and interregional rail/barge shipments.
- **Balanced regions** -- Texas and California have large production bases but also very high consumption, operating near balance with some import supplementation.

This geographic mismatch drives the long-haul logistics described in Chapter 04 (Supply Chain & Logistics) and the import dynamics covered in Chapter 02 (Import Dynamics).

## 1.4 Consumption Patterns

### Overall Consumption

US cement consumption reached approximately 110 million metric tons in 2024, representing a 6% decline from the 2023 level. This decline reflects primarily a softening in residential construction starts due to elevated mortgage rates, partially offset by continued strength in infrastructure spending.

### End-Use Sectors

Cement consumption in the US flows through several distinct channels:

| Customer Segment | Share of Consumption | Estimated Volume (MT) | Facilities (EPA FRS) |
|-----------------|---------------------|----------------------|---------------------|
| Ready-Mix Concrete | 70--75% | 77--83 MT | 11,977 plants |
| Concrete Product Manufacturers | 12% | ~13 MT | 2,919 plants |
| Contractors (direct purchase) | 8--10% | 9--11 MT | N/A |
| Other (oil well cement, soil stabilization) | 5--10% | 6--11 MT | N/A |

The ready-mix concrete industry is the overwhelmingly dominant consumer of bulk cement, operating nearly 12,000 batch plants nationwide. These facilities have a maximum service radius of approximately 60--90 minutes (the working time of fresh concrete), creating a highly distributed network of demand points. Concrete product manufacturers -- including precast (1,321 plants), concrete block/brick (1,139 plants), and concrete pipe (459 plants) -- represent the second tier of cement demand, producing durable goods that can be shipped longer distances.

## 1.5 Pricing Trends

### Ex-Plant Pricing

Cement pricing in the US is regionally determined and not publicly traded on commodity exchanges. Producers publish annual price increase letters (typically effective January 1 and sometimes mid-year), which serve as the baseline for negotiated contract prices.

| Price Metric | 2023 | 2024 | 2025 (Est.) | Trend |
|-------------|------|------|-------------|-------|
| Average Ex-Plant (bulk, FOB) | $135--150/ton | $140--160/ton | $150--170/ton | Rising |
| Delivered (50-mile radius) | $160--185/ton | $165--195/ton | $175--205/ton | Rising |
| Masonry Cement (bulk) | $170--200/ton | $180--210/ton | $190--220/ton | Rising |
| White Cement (bulk) | $180--280/ton | $180--280/ton | $190--290/ton | Stable to rising |
| Average Import Price (CIF) | $84/ton | $79/ton | TBD | Declining |

Key pricing observations:

1. **Domestic prices rising, import prices declining** -- This spread creates the economic incentive for increased imports, particularly in coastal markets with terminal access.
2. **Regional premium markets** -- Florida, the Northeast, and Southern California command the highest delivered prices due to supply constraints and strong demand.
3. **White cement premium** -- At 2--3x the price of gray cement, white cement represents a high-margin niche. With only one domestic producer (Lehigh White, Waco TX, 140,000 MT/year), the market is structurally import-dependent.
4. **Masonry cement premium** -- Florida's dominant masonry cement consumption reflects building code requirements and commands a consistent premium over Portland cement.

### Price Drivers

Several factors support continued upward pressure on domestic cement prices:

- **Energy costs** -- Cement production is energy-intensive, consuming 4--6 million BTU per ton of clinker. Natural gas prices, coal/petcoke costs, and electricity rates directly affect production costs.
- **Environmental compliance** -- EPA emission standards (NESHAP, NSPS) require ongoing capital investment in pollution control equipment, adding $2--5/ton to production costs.
- **Labor costs** -- Skilled kiln operators and maintenance technicians are in short supply, driving wage inflation in the industry.
- **Limestone/raw material costs** -- While most plants operate captive quarries, depletion of permitted reserves and increasing permitting difficulty add to long-term cost pressures.
- **CO2 regulation risk** -- Cement manufacturing accounts for approximately 1.5% of US CO2 emissions. Future carbon pricing or regulation represents a material cost risk to the industry.

## 1.6 Import Penetration

Imports filled approximately 22% of US cement consumption in 2024, totaling 19.8 million metric tons valued at $2.43 billion. This import share is historically elevated, reflecting:

1. **Domestic capacity constraints** -- Closed/idle plants and limited greenfield development restrict domestic supply response.
2. **Competitive landed pricing** -- At an average CIF price of $79/ton, imports undercut domestic ex-plant prices by a significant margin before adding terminal and distribution costs.
3. **Coastal market access** -- Import terminals in Houston, Tampa, New Orleans, Norfolk, and Long Beach provide direct access to high-consumption coastal markets.
4. **Terminal infrastructure buildout** -- Investment in dedicated import terminals by companies such as CEMEX, Holcim, and independent operators like SESCO Cement has expanded the import logistics chain.

The import dynamics, including origin economics, tariff structure, and the impact of Section 301 maritime fees, are analyzed in detail in Chapter 02 (Import Dynamics).

## 1.8 Market Outlook

### Near-Term (2025--2026)

- **Infrastructure spending peaks** -- IIJA-funded projects reach maximum execution phase, supporting cement demand at 108--112 MT.
- **Residential construction recovery** -- Mortgage rate stabilization expected to gradually improve housing starts in late 2025 through 2026.
- **Import dynamics shift** -- Section 301 vessel fees reshape the economics of waterborne cement imports, potentially reducing import volumes by 5--10% or redirecting flows to non-Chinese-built vessel capacity.
- **Price increases continue** -- Producers expected to implement $8--12/ton increases in 2025 and 2026.

### Medium-Term (2027--2030)

- **Capacity additions** -- Limited new kiln capacity under construction or permitted; supply constraints persist.
- **SCM market tightening** -- Declining fly ash supply (see Chapter 03) may increase Portland cement intensity in concrete mixes, adding 3--5 MT to effective cement demand.
- **Decarbonization investment** -- Major producers investing in carbon capture (CCS), alternative fuels, and clinker factor reduction. These investments will add to production costs but may unlock regulatory advantages.
- **Consolidation continues** -- Additional M&A expected as European/Latin American majors seek to optimize US portfolios.
