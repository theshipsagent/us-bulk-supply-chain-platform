# Chapter 3: Supplementary Cementitious Materials (SCM)

**US Bulk Supply Chain Reporting Platform -- Cement & Cementitious Materials Commodity Report**

---

The US supplementary cementitious materials market is undergoing a structural transformation driven by the accelerating retirement of coal-fired power plants, the declining domestic integrated steelmaking base, and the emerging adoption of natural pozzolans and calcined clay technologies. Fly ash -- historically the dominant SCM at 15--20 million tons per year of US consumption -- faces a secular supply decline as over 200 coal plants have closed since 2010, pushing prices from $30/ton to $60--80/ton and forcing concrete producers to reformulate mix designs. Slag cement (GGBFS) supply is similarly constrained by blast furnace closures, while natural pozzolans and LC3 (Limestone Calcined Clay Cement) technology represent nascent but growing alternatives. The global SCM market, valued at approximately $28 billion, is transitioning from a waste-stream-dependent supply model to one requiring intentional production and procurement. This chapter analyzes each major SCM category, its supply chain, standards framework, and implications for the Portland cement market.

---

## 3.1 SCM Market Overview

### Definition and Function

Supplementary cementitious materials are mineral admixtures added to concrete or blended with Portland cement to improve performance characteristics including:

- **Strength development** -- Long-term compressive strength gains through pozzolanic reaction
- **Durability** -- Reduced permeability, improved sulfate resistance, mitigation of alkali-silica reaction (ASR)
- **Workability** -- Improved rheology and finishability
- **Heat reduction** -- Lower heat of hydration for mass concrete applications
- **Sustainability** -- Reduced CO2 footprint by displacing Portland cement clinker

## 3.2 Fly Ash

### Supply Dynamics

Fly ash is the fine particulate residue captured from the flue gas of coal-fired power plants by electrostatic precipitators or baghouse filters. It is classified under ASTM C618 as:

- **Class F** -- Low-calcium fly ash from bituminous/anthracite coal combustion (SiO2 + Al2O3 + Fe2O3 >= 70%). Dominant in eastern US.
- **Class C** -- High-calcium fly ash from sub-bituminous/lignite coal combustion (SiO2 + Al2O3 + Fe2O3 >= 50%). Dominant in western US and Texas.

### Coal Plant Retirements and Fresh Fly Ash Decline

The single most consequential trend in the US SCM market is the accelerating retirement of coal-fired power plants:

| Period | Coal Plants Retired | Capacity Retired (GW) | Impact on Fresh Fly Ash |
|--------|--------------------|-----------------------|------------------------|
| 2010--2015 | ~100 | ~40 | Initial supply tightening |
| 2015--2020 | ~70 | ~35 | Significant regional shortages |
| 2020--2025 | ~50 | ~30 | Widespread supply constraints |
| 2026--2030 (projected) | ~40 | ~25 | Critical supply deficit |
| **Cumulative since 2010** | **~260** | **~130** | **60%+ reduction in fresh ash** |

In 2010, the US had approximately 580 coal-fired power plants generating ~45% of national electricity and producing approximately 70 million tons of coal combustion residuals (CCR) annually, of which roughly 50 million tons was fly ash. By 2025, coal's share of electricity generation has fallen below 15%, and the number of active coal plants has dropped below 200. Fresh fly ash production has declined to approximately 20--25 million tons per year, with the quality-graded portion suitable for concrete applications representing only 12--15 million tons.

### Harvested (Landfilled) Fly Ash

The decline of fresh fly ash has catalyzed a growing industry focused on recovering, processing, and marketing fly ash from legacy coal combustion residual (CCR) impoundments and landfills:

| Company | Operations | Estimated Capacity | Business Model |
|---------|-----------|-------------------|---------------|
| **Eco Material Technologies (EMT)** | 30+ sites nationwide | 8--12 MT/year | Largest US ash harvester; processes landfilled ash to ASTM C618 spec |
| **Boral Resources** (now Seven Hills) | SE US, Midwest | 3--5 MT/year | Marketing arm for utility ash; transitioning to harvested |
| **Salt River Materials Group** | Southeast | 2--3 MT/year | Regional marketer and processor |
| **Headwaters (now Boral/EMT)** | Historical | Consolidated | Acquired by Boral, then parts to EMT |
| **Various utility-owned operations** | Site-specific | Variable | Direct marketing from active plants |

### Fly Ash Pricing

| Period | Price Range ($/ton, FOB source) | Drivers |
|--------|-------------------------------|---------|
| 2010--2014 | $25--35 | Abundant supply, disposal cost offset |
| 2015--2018 | $35--50 | Plant retirements begin, supply tightens |
| 2019--2022 | $45--65 | Accelerating retirements, COVID disruption |
| 2023--2025 | $60--80 | Structural deficit, harvested ash premium |
| 2026 (projected) | $70--90 | Continued tightening, transportation costs |

The doubling of fly ash prices over the past decade has fundamentally altered the economics of SCM usage in concrete. Where fly ash was once essentially a waste product available at or below zero cost (utilities paying for disposal), it is now a value-added commodity commanding premium pricing. This price increase has narrowed the economic advantage of fly ash replacement over Portland cement, with implications for cement demand discussed in Section 3.6 below.

### Quality and Specification Challenges

Harvested fly ash presents distinct quality challenges compared to fresh ash:

- **Carbon content variability** -- Landfilled ash may have higher loss-on-ignition (LOI) values, requiring beneficiation (carbon burnout or separation) to meet ASTM C618 limits.
- **Moisture content** -- Pond ash requires drying before use, adding processing cost.
- **Contamination** -- Legacy impoundments may contain co-disposed bottom ash, scrubber sludge, or other materials requiring separation.
- **Consistency** -- Each landfill layer may reflect different coal sources, combustion conditions, and co-disposed materials, making quality consistency challenging.
- **Regulatory compliance** -- EPA's 2015 CCR Rule (40 CFR 257) governs closure and remediation of CCR impoundments, which affects the timeline and feasibility of ash harvesting operations.

## 3.3 Slag Cement (GGBFS)

### Production and Supply

Ground granulated blast furnace slag (GGBFS) is produced by quenching molten slag from iron blast furnaces in water, then grinding the resulting granulated material to cement fineness. GGBFS is classified under ASTM C989 in three grades based on reactivity:

- **Grade 80** -- Low activity
- **Grade 100** -- Moderate activity (most common)
- **Grade 120** -- High activity (premium product)

### US Blast Furnace Operations

The US GGBFS supply chain is constrained by the declining number of active blast furnace operations:

| Facility | Operator | Location | Status | Slag Granulation |
|----------|----------|----------|--------|-----------------|
| Gary Works | US Steel | Gary, IN | Active (reduced) | Yes - granulation plant |
| Burns Harbor | ArcelorMittal (Cleveland-Cliffs) | Burns Harbor, IN | Active | Yes - granulation plant |
| Indiana Harbor | ArcelorMittal (Cleveland-Cliffs) | East Chicago, IN | Active (reduced) | Yes - limited |
| Granite City | US Steel | Granite City, IL | Intermittent | Limited |
| Mon Valley Works | US Steel | Braddock, PA | Active (reduced) | Historical |
| Nucor (DRI/EAF) | Nucor | Various | N/A | EAF slag =/= GGBFS |

Note: Electric arc furnace (EAF) steelmaking, which dominates US steel production under Nucor, Steel Dynamics, and others, produces a different type of slag that is not suitable for GGBFS production. Only integrated blast furnace operations produce the granulated slag used in GGBFS. The ongoing shift from integrated to EAF steelmaking therefore represents a structural decline in GGBFS supply potential.

GGBFS pricing has traditionally been set at or slightly below the price of Portland cement in each regional market, providing a modest economic incentive for replacement in concrete mixes while delivering performance benefits. As domestic slag supply has tightened, prices have firmed and the discount to cement has narrowed.

## 3.4 Natural Pozzolans

### Emerging Market

Natural pozzolans represent the fastest-growing segment of the US SCM market, driven by the need to replace declining fly ash supply with sustainable alternatives. Natural pozzolans are classified under ASTM C618 as Class N materials.

### Calcined Clay and LC3 Technology

The most significant emerging SCM technology is Limestone Calcined Clay Cement (LC3), developed by EPFL (Switzerland) and now being commercialized globally:

**LC3 Concept:**
- Blend of 50% Portland clinker + 30% calcined clay (kaolinite-rich) + 15% limestone + 5% gypsum
- Reduces clinker factor from ~95% (OPC) to ~50%
- CO2 reduction of 30--40% compared to ordinary Portland cement
- Uses widely available clay deposits (kaolinite) rather than scarce fly ash or slag

**US LC3 Development:**

| Developer | Location | Status | Notes |
|-----------|----------|--------|-------|
| **Holcim US** | Multiple plants | Pilot/planning | Active testing at several kiln lines |
| **CEMEX** | Various | Research phase | Global LC3 program |
| **Heidelberg Materials** | Various | Research phase | European experience transferring to US |
| **Georgia Kaolin producers** | Central Georgia | Raw material supply | BASF, Imerys, Thiele Kaolin |
| **Texas clay deposits** | Central Texas | Exploration | Multiple potential sources |

## 3.5 Silica Fume

## 3.6 Impact of SCM Decline on Portland Cement Demand

The declining availability and rising cost of SCMs has direct implications for the Portland cement market analyzed in Chapter 01:

### Cement Intensity Effect

As fly ash and slag become less available or more expensive, concrete producers respond by:

1. **Increasing Portland cement content** -- Replacing 25--35% fly ash with Portland cement in concrete mixes increases cement demand by the same proportion.
2. **Reducing total cementitious content** -- Optimizing mix designs to achieve required strength with less total cementitious material (offsetting some of the SCM replacement).
3. **Shifting to limestone-interground cements** -- Using Portland-limestone cement (PLC, Type IL) to achieve a 10--15% clinker factor reduction without requiring external SCM procurement.
4. **Adopting performance specifications** -- Moving from prescriptive specifications (e.g., "20% fly ash replacement") to performance-based specifications that allow flexibility in mix design.

### Quantified Impact on Cement Demand

| Scenario | Fly Ash Reduction | Additional Cement Demand | Impact on 110 MT Market |
|----------|-------------------|--------------------------|----|
| **Moderate (current trend)** | 5 MT decline by 2030 | +3--4 MT cement | +2.7--3.6% |
| **Accelerated (aggressive coal retirement)** | 10 MT decline by 2030 | +6--8 MT cement | +5.5--7.3% |
| **Severe (near-total coal phase-out)** | 15 MT decline by 2035 | +9--12 MT cement | +8.2--10.9% |

Under the moderate scenario, declining fly ash supply alone could add 3--4 million tons of annual cement demand by 2030, equivalent to the output of 2--3 major kiln lines. In an industry already operating near capacity, this incremental demand would further tighten the supply-demand balance and support continued price increases (see Chapter 01, Section 1.5).

## 3.8 Regulatory Framework

### EPA Coal Combustion Residuals (CCR) Rule

The EPA's 2015 CCR Rule (40 CFR Part 257) governs the disposal and management of coal combustion residuals, including fly ash. Key provisions affecting SCM supply:

- **Closure requirements** -- Unlined CCR impoundments must close by specified deadlines, potentially making ash unavailable for harvesting unless beneficial use is established.
- **Beneficial use provisions** -- The CCR Rule explicitly encourages beneficial use of fly ash in concrete, which supports harvesting operations.
- **Groundwater monitoring** -- Sites with groundwater contamination may face accelerated closure or additional remediation requirements, affecting harvesting timelines.

### ASTM Standards Framework

| Standard | Title | Application |
|----------|-------|-------------|
| ASTM C618 | Coal Fly Ash and Raw or Calcined Natural Pozzolan for Use in Concrete | Fly ash (Class F/C), natural pozzolans (Class N) |
| ASTM C989 | Slag Cement for Use in Concrete and Mortars | GGBFS grading (80, 100, 120) |
| ASTM C1240 | Silica Fume Used in Cementitious Mixtures | Silica fume quality |
| ASTM C1697 | Blended Supplementary Cementitious Materials | Blended SCM products |
| ASTM C595 | Blended Hydraulic Cements | Portland-pozzolan, Portland-slag cements |
| ASTM C1157 | Hydraulic Cement (Performance Specification) | Performance-based cement specification |

### State DOT Specifications

State Departments of Transportation represent the largest single category of cement/concrete specification authority in the US. Many state DOTs maintain approved materials lists (AMLs) that specify allowable SCMs for highway concrete. The transition from prescriptive to performance-based specifications is proceeding unevenly across states, with implications for SCM market access:

- **Leading states** -- Texas, Florida, Georgia, and California have adopted or are adopting performance-based specifications that allow greater flexibility in SCM type and dosage.
- **Lagging states** -- Some Midwest and Northeast states maintain prescriptive specifications requiring specific fly ash types and percentages, which constrains the use of alternative SCMs.

### Key Uncertainties

1. **Coal plant retirement pace** -- Regulatory, political, and economic factors could accelerate or delay remaining coal plant closures, directly impacting fresh fly ash supply.
2. **LC3 commercialization timeline** -- The speed at which LC3 technology moves from pilot to commercial scale will determine whether calcined clay can offset fly ash and slag declines.
3. **Imported slag availability** -- Japanese and Korean blast furnace operations face their own decarbonization pressures, potentially limiting future granulated slag exports.
4. **Carbon pricing** -- Implementation of a carbon price or border adjustment mechanism would alter the relative economics of all SCMs versus Portland cement.
5. **Natural pozzolan permitting** -- Mining permits for volcanic deposits in Nevada, New Mexico, and Oregon face environmental review timelines that may constrain supply growth.
