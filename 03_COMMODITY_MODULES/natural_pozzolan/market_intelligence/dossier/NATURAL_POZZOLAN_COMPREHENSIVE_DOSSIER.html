Executive Briefing — Strategic Implications for SESCO Cement

Natural pozzolans represent the growth sector of the SCM market and a strategic diversification opportunity as both fly ash and GGBFS face structural supply declines. This briefing highlights the five most critical takeaways:

  • Natural pozzolans are the only growing domestic SCM. US shipments reached 2.5 Mt (2024) with capacity projected to reach 3.5–4.0 Mt by 2028. Unlike fly ash (declining with coal) and GGBFS (declining with blast furnaces), natural pozzolans are independent mineral resources with large US reserves and no industrial transition risk.
  • LC3 is the long-term cement game-changer. Limestone Calcined Clay Cement can cut CO2 by 40% using widely available low-grade clays. The DOE has invested $1.6B in cement decarbonization, with 3 of 6 funded projects targeting LC3. SESCO opportunity: Understanding LC3 technology positions SESCO ahead of the curve as US adoption scales from pilot to commercial (2026–2030).
  • Price parity with fly ash has arrived. Natural pozzolans at ~$115/Mt delivered are now competitive with fly ash at $118–123/Mt. As fly ash prices continue rising (supply declining), natural pozzolans become the clear cost-effective alternative. SESCO opportunity: Blending imported cement with domestic natural pozzolans creates a differentiated, cost-competitive, low-carbon product.
  • Major cement companies are moving. CRH/Ash Grove acquired Geofortis pozzolan operations (Sep 2024). Eco Material Technologies has >4 Mt/yr SCM capacity across 9 plants. Boral, Hess Pumice, and CR Minerals are expanding. First-mover advantage in pozzolan sourcing is narrowing.
  • Import potential from volcanic regions. Greece, Italy, Turkey, and Central America have abundant volcanic pozzolan deposits. Turkey is already a SESCO cement supply partner. SESCO opportunity: Co-sourcing natural pozzolans with cement from Turkey or Mediterranean origins leverages existing trade lanes and vessel logistics.

8. LC3 — LIMESTONE CALCINED CLAY CEMENT

8.1 Technology Overview

LC3-50 Cement Composition vs Ordinary Portland Cement
LC3-50 replaces 45% of clinker with calcined clay (30%) + limestone (15%), achieving up to 40% CO2 reduction while maintaining performance. Uses low-grade clays (40% kaolinite sufficient).

8.4 US LC3 Challenges

  • Insufficient calcination infrastructure (few plants have clay calciners)
  • ASTM standards allow calcined clay to 40% and limestone to 15% — but not as combined LC3 formulation
  • Industry pushing for max 70% for any combination of limestone, pozzolan, and slag
  • Some state DOTs extending acceptance testing from 28 to 42 or 56 days
  • Historical construction practices favor conventional OPC
  • Limited domestic production and suppliers at commercial scale
LC3 OUTLOOK: LC3 is the most scalable long-term alternative to declining fly ash and GGBFS. Raw materials (low-grade clay, limestone) are globally abundant and cheaply available. The $1.6B DOE investment in 6 cement decarbonization projects (3 focused on LC3) signals strong federal commitment. But commercial-scale US deployment remains 3–5 years away. Natural volcanic pozzolans are the near-term bridge SCM while LC3 infrastructure is built.

14.1 Natural Pozzolans: Advantages vs Limitations

Advantages

  • Independent mineral resource — not tied to coal or steel
  • Consistent quality (geological deposit vs variable industrial byproduct)
  • Large identified US reserves (>25 Mt pumice; 39 Mt Kirkland; massive NV deposits)
  • Simple processing: mine, dry, grind, classify
  • Now cost-competitive with fly ash (~$115 vs $118)
  • ASTM C618 Class N compliant
  • Superior long-term durability (up to 5x OPC lifespan)
  • 95–97% lower embodied CO2 than OPC

Limitations

  • Geographic concentration in Western US; high distribution costs to East
  • Slower early strength development (7-day) vs OPC
  • Higher water demand for some types (diatomaceous earth)
  • Quality varies by deposit; not all volcanic materials qualify
  • Lighter bulk density = more storage volume needed
  • Calcined clay/metakaolin requires energy for kiln processing
  • Market awareness still growing; some specifiers unfamiliar
  • State DOT acceptance still expanding (not yet universal)
NET ASSESSMENT: Natural pozzolans are positioned for strong growth as the only expanding-supply SCM category in the US. The convergence of fly ash price increases ($118/Mt), GGBFS supply constraints, new mine openings (Kirkland 39 Mt, Hazen, Nevada deposits), major company investment (CRH, Eco Material), federal LC3 funding ($1.6B DOE), and carbon regulation (Buy Clean, LEED v5) creates a multi-year growth runway. NPA projects capacity growing from 2.5 Mt (2024) to 3.5–4.0 Mt by 2028 — and LC3 commercial deployment starting 2028–2030 could multiply calcined clay demand by an order of magnitude. The primary constraints are Western geographic concentration and the 3–5 year lead time for LC3 infrastructure. Natural volcanic pozzolans serve as the near-term bridge SCM while LC3 scales.
