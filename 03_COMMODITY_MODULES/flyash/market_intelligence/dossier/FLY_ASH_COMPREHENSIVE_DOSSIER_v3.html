EXECUTIVE SUMMARY: Fly ash is the most widely used supplementary cementitious material (SCM) in US concrete, but faces a structural supply crisis as coal-fired power generation declines. US coal plant capacity has fallen 48% from the 2011 peak (318 GW) to ~165 GW (end 2025), with an additional 68.8 GW scheduled to retire by 2030. Total CCP production fell from 111 Mt (2017) to 67 Mt (2023). Beneficial use reached a record 72% (2024) but total available fly ash for concrete is shrinking. US prices have risen to $118–123/Mt while Asian FOB prices remain $16–27/Mt, creating a $91–107/Mt import arbitrage. South Korea (49%), Turkey (26%), and China (23%) are the primary import sources. The fly ash supply gap is projected to widen to 12–18 Mt by 2028–2030, driving increased reliance on imports, reclaimed ash from landfills, and alternative SCMs.

Executive Briefing — Strategic Implications for SESCO Cement

The fly ash market presents both challenges and opportunities for SESCO’s cement import and distribution operations. This briefing highlights the five most critical takeaways:

  • Domestic fly ash supply is in structural decline. US coal capacity has fallen 48% since 2011 and will lose another 68.8 GW by 2030. Total CCP production dropped from 111 Mt (2017) to 67 Mt (2023). This is permanent — coal plant closures are irreversible. The SCM most widely used in US concrete is disappearing.
  • Import arbitrage is compelling. US fly ash trades at $118–123/Mt while Asian FOB prices are $16–27/Mt. Even with ocean freight ($30–50/Mt), landed cost of $46–77/Mt represents 37–61% savings vs domestic. SESCO opportunity: SESCO’s Houston terminal infrastructure and vessel agency expertise position the company for fly ash import/distribution.
  • Tariff barriers are selective. Chinese fly ash faces a 145% tariff, effectively blocking that origin. South Korean (49% of imports) and Turkish (26%) fly ash face minimal barriers. SESCO opportunity: Turkey is already a SESCO cement supply partner (Nuh Cement); fly ash co-sourcing from Turkey is a natural extension.
  • Reclaimed ash is a growing domestic source. Previously disposed ash reclamation has doubled from 2.5 Mt (2021) to 5.0+ Mt (2024 est). Companies like Eco Material Technologies (EMT) and Charah Solutions are commercializing landfill ash processing. Quality variability and ASTM compliance remain challenges.
  • The combined SCM deficit is accelerating. Fly ash supply is declining (coal closures) while GGBFS supply is declining (EAF transition). US cement demand continues rising (IIJA $1.2T through 2028). This dual SCM crisis creates sustained pricing pressure and import demand. SESCO opportunity: Building a diversified SCM import portfolio (fly ash + GGBFS + natural pozzolans) alongside cement creates a differentiated competitive position.

CRITICAL DISTINCTION: Only Class F and Class C fly ash have significant SCM value for concrete. Class F (from bituminous coal, dominant in eastern US) is the premium product for durability applications. Class C (from sub-bituminous coal, dominant in western US) has self-cementing properties but higher CaO limits its use in some applications. The ~14.5 GW retiring in 2025 is primarily MISO and PJM capacity (Midwest/Mid-Atlantic) — the heartland of Class F production.

Supply Threats

  • Coal retirements: 68.8 GW scheduled to close 2025–2030
  • Production decline: 111 Mt CCP (2017) → 67 Mt (2023) → ~45 Mt by 2030
  • Capacity factor: Dropped from 67% (2010) to 42% (2023)
  • Regional loss: MISO + PJM losing 66% of 2025 retirements (Class F heartland)
  • EPA regulations: Mercury/Air Toxics (MATS), CCR Rule driving closures

Supply Stabilizers

  • Beneficial use rate: 72% (2024 record) — extracting more from less
  • Reclaimed ash: 4.5 Mt (2023), doubling annually
  • Imports growing: Korea, Turkey filling domestic gap
  • CCS at coal plants: Could extend some plant lifespans
  • Western coal: PRB plants retiring more slowly (Class C)

DUAL SCM SUPPLY CRISIS: The US faces a unique crisis — both primary SCMs are in structural decline simultaneously from unrelated industrial transitions. Fly ash is declining because coal plants are closing. GGBFS is declining because blast furnaces are being replaced by EAFs. 7.2 Fly Ash Performance in Concrete

Advantages

  • 15–35% cement replacement (typical); higher in mass concrete
  • Improved workability and pumpability
  • Reduced water demand (ball-bearing effect of spherical particles)
  • Reduced heat of hydration (mass concrete, hot weather)
  • Enhanced sulfate resistance (Class F)
  • Reduced ASR (alkali-silica reaction) expansion
  • Lower permeability at later ages
  • 86–95% lower embodied CO2 than OPC
  • LEED v5 / Buy Clean compliance
  • Cost savings vs Portland cement ($40–60/Mt less)

Limitations

  • Slower early strength gain (3 and 7-day)
  • Quality variability by coal source and plant
  • LOI (loss on ignition) can vary — high LOI reduces air entrainment
  • Supply increasingly constrained and unreliable
  • Class C high-CaO limits sulfate resistance
  • Cold weather extends set times further
  • Color variability (aesthetic applications)
  • Fineness varies between sources

CARBON VALUE PROPOSITION: At just 20–50 kg CO2e/tonne, fly ash has the lowest embodied carbon of any major SCM. With Buy Clean mandates and LEED v5 now requiring embodied carbon quantification, fly ash is the most impactful ingredient for decarbonizing concrete — but the irony is clear: the cleanest SCM is disappearing because its source (coal combustion) is too dirty.

Terminal infrastructure mirrors cement terminals — silos, pneumatic transfer, covered loading — and can share existing cement import infrastructure. SESCO’s Houston terminal already has the physical infrastructure for fly ash handling.
