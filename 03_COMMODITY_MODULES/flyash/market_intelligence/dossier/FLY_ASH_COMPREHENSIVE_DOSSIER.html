FLY ASH COMPREHENSIVE DOSSIER

Prepared: December 10, 2025 | Purpose: Meeting Research Notes | Classification: Internal Use

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY: Fly ash market presents strategic opportunity due to declining U.S. domestic supply (530 plants in 2009 → 263 plants in 2024), sustained IIJA infrastructure demand through 2028, and favorable import economics (Asian FOB $16-27/MT vs. U.S. $118-123/MT). Reclaimed ash from sites like Duke Beckjord represents emerging domestic supply but faces quality, regulatory, and technical challenges. Beneficial use rate reached 72% in 2024 - highest ever recorded.

2.2 U.S. Domestic Supply Crisis

CRITICAL: U.S. coal plants declined from 530 (2009) to 263 (2024), with 150-160 projected to close by 2030. Domestic fly ash production has declined 50%+ since 2008.

2023 ACAA Survey Data:

  • Total CCP Produced: 66.7 million tons
  • Beneficial Use Rate: 69% (46.3 million tons) - up from 62% in 2022
  • 2024 Beneficial Use: 72% (10th consecutive year >50%)
  • Fly Ash in Concrete: 11.9 million tons (up from 10.9 million in 2022)

2.3 Import Trade Dynamics

  • Import Growth: Slag + ash imports grew from $41 million (2016) to $71 million (2018), continuing to increase
  • Key Import States: Florida, Washington, Louisiana, Nevada, Ohio, Texas
  • Supply Chain Issues (Nov 2024): ILA strikes, South China Sea typhoons, port congestion, strained shipping capacity
  • Asia-Pacific Dominance: 44-57% global market share; competitive pricing due to abundant supply

MOISTURE SENSITIVITY: Even 1-2% moisture creates significant handling problems through capillary forces binding particles. Class C (high-calcium) is particularly problematic as moisture triggers cite cementitious reactions that can solidify material within silos.

4. TERMINAL INFRASTRUCTURE REQUIREMENTS

4.1 Storage Systems

  • Silo Design: Steep cone angles ≥65-70 degrees for mass flow; prevent ratholing
  • Typical Capacity: 10,000-100,000 metric tons across multiple silos
  • Aeration Systems: Fluidizing plates (sintered metal, ceramic, or multi-layer fabric)
  • Discharge Aids: Vibrators, pneumatically operated gates, air slides
  • Requirements: Explosion-proof systems, absolute moisture-proofing, filtered venting

4.2 Pneumatic Conveying

  • Dense Phase (Preferred): Max 2,500 ft/min air velocity; 20-150 lbs/lb conveying gas ratio
  • Dilute Phase: 3,600-4,800 ft/min - causes more wear
  • Pipeline: Ceramic-lined components, hard chrome plating, AR235 abrasion-resistant steel
  • Long Distance: >1,000m requires 16-18" pipe diameters; 175 m³/min compressor

5.3 European Smart Logistics Model (EP Power Minerals)

Germany → UK Supply Chain:
  1. Inland silo vessels load at Ruhr area power plants
  2. Ship-to-ship transfer at ARA ports (Amsterdam, Rotterdam, Antwerp)
  3. Seagoing coasters deliver to UK receiving stations
  4. Coasters use "cement holes" for dust-free pneumatic injection
  5. Silo push barges (2,300 MT capacity) serve as floating storage to eliminate demurrage

7. RARE EARTH ELEMENTS (REE) POTENTIAL

7.1 Strategic Value

USGS Research Finding: Coal fly ash is a potential resource for valuable rare earth elements (REEs), which are retained and concentrated upon combustion. REEs are critical for defense, renewable energy, magnets, batteries, and catalysts.

7.2 Key Findings (USGS 2024)

  • Concentration: REEs enriched in fly ash vs. source coal
  • Best Sources: U.S. Appalachian Basin coals; volcanic ash-influenced coals especially enriched
  • Extraction Challenge: Eastern U.S. ash = less extractable than Western U.S. ash (despite higher REE content)
  • Supply Context: U.S. produces only 9% of global REE supply (one California mine); China dominates at 70%
  • Development Needed: Technology for REE concentration and purification to saleable purity

7.3 Resource Scale

  • Annual fly ash production + stored ash in landfills/impoundments = massive potential REE reserve
  • ~50% of U.S. fly ash currently goes to storage (landfills, impoundments)
  • Represents future recovery opportunity as extraction technology matures

8.2 Regulatory Issues

EPA ENFORCEMENT (January 2022): EPA demanding better cleanup - unlined pits must comply with federal CCR rules. EPA position: minimum federal standards apply because cleanup companies are moving coal ash from one pit to another.

Legal Action: 100+ Clermont County residents suing CLP; NOPE (Neighbors Opposed to Pit Expansion) filed federal lawsuit December 2021.

8.3 Reclaimed Ash: Pros & Cons

PROS

  • Domestic supply source as coal plants close
  • Reduces landfill/pond environmental liability
  • Lower transport costs if near market
  • Strong market demand for quality ash
  • Concrete industry willing to sign long-term contracts
  • Supports circular economy/sustainability goals
  • Potential REE recovery in future

CONS

  • Variable quality - contamination from storage
  • High moisture content requires drying
  • Possible agglomeration/compaction
  • May not meet ASTM C618 specifications
  • Regulatory uncertainty (EPA CCR Rule)
  • Testing/beneficiation costs
  • Community opposition/litigation risk
  • Heavy metals leaching concerns

8.4 Technical Challenges for Reclaimed Ash

  1. Moisture: Ponded ash requires drying; dusty nature makes drying challenging
  2. Quality Testing: Microscopy + colorimetry to map composition; delineate CCR content
  3. Beneficiation Needs:
    • Carbon burnout (heat to 1,300-1,800°F) reduces LOI to ~2%
    • Triboelectrostatic separation (STET) reduces LOI from 9.5% to <3%
    • Additional processing for contamination/agglomeration
  4. pH/Chemistry: High pH (8-12) may cause Fe, Mn, Cu, Zn, P deficiencies; toxic B levels possible
  5. Pozzolanic Hardening: Cemented/compacted layers from long-term storage

8.5 Successful Reclamation Examples

  • Eco Material Technologies (2024): Developed technology to reclaim fly ash from landfills for sustainable concrete
  • DOE Project FE0032038: 2 million tons harvested CCR from inactive pond + old FGD landfill for mine reclamation
  • Dominion Energy Sites: Technologies commercially available producing high-quality ash from landfilled/ponded sources

11. DEMAND DRIVERS & OUTLOOK

11.1 Infrastructure Investment (IIJA)

$1.2 TRILLION IIJA: Ensures demand through at least 2028. As of December 2024, agencies had obligated $275.1 billion (47%) of available funds. Each ton of fly ash replacing cement avoids ~1 ton of CO₂ emissions - aligns with Buy Clean mandates.

11.2 Sustainability Drivers

  • CO₂ Reduction: 1 ton fly ash = ~1 ton CO₂ avoided vs. Portland cement
  • Geopolymer Cement: 60-80% lower CO₂ than Portland; Class F preferred
  • Green Building: Government-backed initiatives driving demand
  • Net-Zero Goals: Companies developing reclamation technologies (Eco Material Technologies)

11.3 Seasonal Demand Pattern

  • Peak: Spring through Fall (construction season)
  • Temperature Limit: Fly ash concrete requires ≥50°F (10°C) day temperatures
  • Terminal Strategy: Build pre-season inventory in Q1/early Q2
