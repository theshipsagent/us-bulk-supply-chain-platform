SLAG COMPREHENSIVE DOSSIER

Prepared: February 18, 2026 | Version: 1.0 | Focus: GGBFS & Iron/Steel Slag — US Supply/Demand, SCM Markets & Trade Flow Analysis

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY: Ground granulated blast furnace slag (GGBFS) is a supply-constrained, high-value supplementary cementitious material (SCM) facing a structural crisis. All available US GGBFS (~3–5 Mt/year) is consumed — the market is defined by supply limitations, not demand constraints. GGBFS represents less than 3% of total US slag tonnage but approximately 80% of its $600M value. The accelerating shift from blast furnaces to electric arc furnaces (EAFs now ~70% of US steelmaking) is progressively eliminating GGBFS feedstock, since EAF slag cannot substitute as an SCM. US import dependence is growing — Japan (52%), China (23%), and Brazil (11%) dominate. Meanwhile, carbon regulations (Buy Clean, LEED v5) are driving accelerating demand: GGBFS has 93–96% lower embodied CO2 than Portland cement (~35–67 vs. 844–967 kg CO2e/tonne). Supply is shrinking while demand is surging — this is the central tension in the slag market.

Executive Briefing — Strategic Implications for SESCO Cement

The GGBFS market presents a structural supply crisis that directly impacts SESCO’s cementitious product portfolio and competitive positioning. This briefing highlights the five most critical takeaways:

  • Supply is shrinking permanently. The US steel industry’s shift from blast furnaces to electric arc furnaces (now ~70% of production) is eliminating GGBFS feedstock. EAF slag cannot substitute as an SCM. Only 4 integrated BF-BOF mills remain as primary domestic GGBFS sources, and further closures are planned.
  • Import dependence is rising. US GGBFS imports reached ~2 Mt in 2024, with Japan (52%), China (23%), and Brazil (11%) as primary origins. Import prices are rising (Japan GGBFS at $120–160/Mt CIF), and global competition for limited BFS tonnage is intensifying.
  • Pricing power is extraordinary. GGBFS prices have risen from $50–80/Mt (2018) to $100–140+/Mt (2024). As supply tightens, pricing is expected to continue rising. SESCO opportunity: GGBFS import and distribution represent a high-margin supply chain position.
  • Carbon regulations are accelerating demand. GGBFS has 93–96% lower embodied CO2 than Portland cement. Buy Clean mandates, LEED v5 carbon limits, and state-level procurement policies are creating guaranteed demand growth even as supply contracts.
  • The SCM gap is widening. Combined with fly ash decline (-64% since 2007 peak) and constrained GGBFS, the US faces a growing SCM deficit. Natural pozzolans and LC3 are emerging alternatives but cannot fully replace GGBFS performance in high-performance concrete. SESCO opportunity: Diversified SCM sourcing (slag + pozzolans) creates competitive advantage over single-source competitors.

CRITICAL DISTINCTION: Only GGBFS has significant cementitious (SCM) value. Steel slag from EAFs — which accounts for ~70% of US steelmaking — CANNOT substitute for GGBFS. Only ~1% of steel slag is used as a cement admixture vs. ~94% of GGBFS. This makes the blast-furnace-to-EAF transition the existential supply risk for the GGBFS market.

3. THE EAF TRANSITION — EXISTENTIAL SUPPLY RISK

US Steelmaking: Blast Furnace (BF-BOF) vs Electric Arc Furnace (EAF) Share
Sources: CATF, Argus Media, Fastmarkets. EAF share has grown from ~50% (2005) to ~70% (2025). EAF steelmaking produces NO granulated blast furnace slag suitable for use as an SCM. Only ~1% of steel/EAF slag is used as a cement admixture.
STRUCTURAL SUPPLY CRISIS: The US is adding 16.1 Mt/yr of new EAF steelmaking capacity (Q4 2023 through 2025) while blast furnaces are being idled and converted to DRI. Every BF closure permanently eliminates GGBFS feedstock. Cleveland-Cliffs' Middletown Works BF is planned for hydrogen-DRI conversion ($500M DOE funding). Dearborn Works BF was idled March 2025. The only bright spot: Nippon Steel committed $3.1B to reline 4 blast furnaces at Gary Works through 2030 — but this preserves existing capacity, it does not grow it.

DUAL SCM SUPPLY CRISIS: Both of the US's primary SCMs — fly ash AND GGBFS — face structural supply declines driven by unrelated but concurrent industrial transitions. Fly ash supply is declining because coal-fired power plants are closing (-48% coal capacity since 2011). GGBFS supply is declining because blast furnaces are being replaced by EAFs (~70% of US steelmaking). There is no near-term large-scale domestic alternative. LC3 (limestone calcined clay cement) is positioned as the long-term replacement but US adoption is still minimal.

7.2 GGBFS Performance in Concrete

Advantages

  • 25–50% cement replacement (up to 70% for durability, 95% in special applications)
  • Superior long-term strength development
  • Reduced permeability and porosity
  • Greater resistance to chlorides, sulfates, and ASR
  • Lower heat of hydration (ideal for mass concrete)
  • Lighter color (aesthetic advantage)
  • 93–96% lower embodied CO2 than OPC
  • LEED v5 and Buy Clean compliance

Limitations

  • Slower early strength gain (7-day)
  • Requires activator (Portland cement) for hydration
  • Supply-constrained — not always available
  • Sensitive to temperature (cold weather curing challenges)
  • Quality varies by source & granulation conditions
  • Higher replacement rates require careful mix design
  • Limited domestic supply (~3–5 Mt vs ~35 Mt fly ash)

CARBON VALUE PROPOSITION: Every ton of GGBS used instead of Portland cement saves approximately 650 kg CO2 in embodied emissions.
