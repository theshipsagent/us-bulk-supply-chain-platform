## Modal Cost Economics

### Cost Crossover Distances (approximate, from USDA sources)

| Threshold | Distance | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Truck → Rail crossover | ~250 miles | Rail becomes cheaper beyond this |
| Rail → Barge crossover | ~700 miles | Barge cheaper on long-distance where available |

**Source:** USDA, Agricultural Transportation Challenges for the 21st Century; CRS RL32720 Figure 1

**Important caveats:**
- Crossovers vary significantly by commodity, origin, season, and market conditions
- Multimodal routes include transshipment costs (~$0.05/bushel per transfer point)
- An initial truck leg of 25-50 miles can shift the break-even point substantially
- Barge only competitive where waterway is accessible (limits geographic applicability)

### Rate Benchmarks (historical reference values)

**Rail rates (STB Waybill, 2022 approximate):**
- Short haul (20-500 miles): $0.20-$0.50/bushel
- Medium haul (501-1000 miles): $0.40-$0.90/bushel
- Long haul (1001-1500 miles): $0.70-$1.20/bushel
- Very long haul (1500+ miles): $0.90-$1.50/bushel

**Barge rates (AMS GTR, Illinois River benchmark):**
- Expressed as % of tariff (e.g., 95% of tariff = ~95% of published river rate)
- Illinois River to Gulf: approximately $0.25-$0.60/bushel depending on market conditions
- Columbia River to PNW port: approximately $0.20-$0.40/bushel

**Truck rates:**
- Short haul (25-75 miles, farm to country elevator): ~$0.05-$0.15/bushel
- Beyond 100 miles truck becomes expensive vs. rail or barge

**Transshipment/handling cost:**
- Country elevator: ~$0.05-$0.10/bushel
- Sub-terminal elevator: ~$0.05/bushel
- River port (truck to barge transfer): ~$0.05/bushel
- Export elevator (terminal charge): ~$0.10-$0.20/bushel
- Sources: Jaller (2022) assumes $0.05/bushel at each transfer point

## Rail Shuttle Economics

### Shipment Size Thresholds (USDA AMS, Prater & Sparger 2013)

| Category | Cars | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Carload | 1-5 | Individual cars, highest cost/ton |
| Small carload | 6-49 | Traditional grain rail service |
| Unit train | 50-74 | Slightly discounted rates |
| **Shuttle train** | **75+** | Significantly discounted rates; dominant for corn/soy |

### Why Shuttle Trains Lowered Costs
1. Single origin, single destination → no switching costs
2. High-speed load-out at sub-terminal elevators (fill train in 15 hours)
3. Faster turnaround → more trips per car per year
4. Longer, heavier railcars (286,000-315,000 lbs vs. 263,000 lbs traditional)
5. Railroads can commit to reliable schedules → farmers and grain companies plan accordingly

### Grain's Rail Revenue Importance (2009)
- Grain = 7.9% of all rail tons
- Grain = 8.4% of total rail revenue
- Railroads haul 33% of all U.S. grain transported
- Grain = 94% of all farm commodities by rail tonnage

## Waterway Infrastructure — Lock Capacity Reference

### UMR-IWW Lock System
- Standard chamber (built 1930-1950): **600 feet**
- Modern tow length: **1,100 feet** (~4 football fields)
- 600-ft lock passage time: **~90 minutes** (must break tow in 2 passes)
- 1,200-ft lock passage time: **~45 minutes** (tow passes intact)
- Standard tow: 15 barges × 52,500 bu/barge = 787,500 bu per tow
- Grain = ~50% of total UMR-IWW freight tonnage

### Columbia-Snake River System
- 8 locks (4 SNR + 4 Columbia): standard dimensions accommodate current tows
- Snake River transport: **2-2.6 MMT/year** wheat to PNW ports (13-16% of PNW exports)
- At-risk under dam removal scenario (Jaller 2022)
