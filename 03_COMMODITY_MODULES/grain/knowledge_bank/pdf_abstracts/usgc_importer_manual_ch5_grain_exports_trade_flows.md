## Overview

Chapter 5 of the U.S. Grains Council Importer Manual provides a historical and structural overview of the global grain trade, with emphasis on the rise of the United States as the dominant grain exporting nation, the forces that created and disrupted that dominance, and the competitive landscape as of the early 2020s.

The USGC produces this manual to educate foreign grain importers on the U.S. grain system. It provides the best single-source narrative covering the political economy of grain trade from the 19th century through China's emergence as the dominant importer.

## Global Grain Trade — Historical Arc

### Scale of International Trade

| Period | International trade as share of consumption |
|---|---|
| 19th Century | <0.03% of world consumption |
| 2021 | >20% of world consumption |
| 2021 absolute volume | ~576 million metric tonnes (USDA estimate) |

### U.S. Position (End of 20th Century)

- U.S. exported **1/3 of all internationally traded wheat**
- U.S. exported **70% of all internationally traded corn**
- U.S. was the world's **No. 1 grain exporter** across most major commodities

## Major Historical Events Shaping U.S. Grain Trade

### The Great Russian Grain Robbery (1971-73)

- Soviet Union began systematic annual grain imports from 1971 forward
- In 1972, secretly bought 10 million tons of U.S. wheat (~25% of annual U.S. production)
- U.S. government was subsidizing Soviet purchases unknowingly (Brookings estimate: $300M wasted)
- Global food prices increased up to 30% in 1973 — called the "Great Grain Robbery"
- Net effect: Created a permanent new U.S.-Soviet grain trade relationship; Soviet imports grew to 27 MMT (1975) → 47 MMT (record 1985 high)

### U.S.-Russia Grain Embargo (1979-80)

- President Carter imposed embargo on Soviet Union following Afghanistan invasion (January 1980)
- Embargo lifted by Reagan in 1981
- **Impact on U.S.:** Grain prices fell sharply; farmers bore the brunt
- **Impact on Soviet Union:** Minimal — they shifted purchases to Argentina, Brazil, Venezuela
- **Long-term lesson:** Unilateral grain embargoes damage the exporter's reputation as a reliable supplier more than they damage the target country
- Led to establishment of robust USDA crop monitoring/forecasting programs (NASS, FAS, NOAA) to prevent future supply intelligence failures

### Floating Exchange Rates (1971-73)

- Bretton Woods fixed exchange rate system collapsed 1968-1973
- Dollar devaluation made U.S. grain **relatively cheaper** for importing countries
- Contributed to agricultural export boom of the 1970s
- Ongoing: Dollar strength/weakness remains a key driver of U.S. grain export competitiveness

### Rise of Globalization (Post-WWII)

- GATT (1947) → WTO (1995) removed trade barriers
- NAFTA (1994) created integrated North American grain market
- Green Revolution (1970s) + GMO adoption (late 1990s) provided productivity to supply growing demand
- Global grain exports rose from 8.5% to 16.2% of world production, 1970-2001

## Competitive Landscape Shifts (Current)

### Brazil

- Emerged as major soybean exporter in the 1970s
- **Displaced the U.S. as #1 soybean exporter** — Brazil's #1 position now firmly established
- Continues expanding corn exports
- Lower cost of land and labor; logistical disadvantages (port congestion, infrastructure) partially offset

### Russia

| Period | Net trade position |
|---|---|
| 1996-2000 | Net importer, avg 3 MMT/yr |
| 2000 | Began consistent exportable surpluses |
| 2020/21 | Net exporter of 49 MMT (wheat + coarse grains) |
| 2020/21 wheat alone | Exported 39.1 MMT of 85.3 MMT harvest |

- **Russia is now the world's largest wheat exporter**
- Production increased 66% in last 30 years; yields up 76%
- U.S. has lost wheat export market share to Russia primarily in MENA markets

### Ukraine

- Major wheat, corn, sunflower oil exporter
- Black Sea corridor competes with U.S. Gulf in European, African, and Middle Eastern markets

## China — The Dominant Import Story

### China's Rise as Grain Importer

| Year | Development |
|---|---|
| 1990s | Grain imports first surge following economic reforms |
| 2001 | WTO accession; soybean imports accelerate |
| 2021 | Became **largest corn importer** (29 MMT) and **largest soybean importer** (100 MMT) |
| 2021 | Also importing 10+ MMT each of grain sorghum and wheat |

**China's constraint:** 7% of world's arable land for 20%+ of world's population

**Sorghum specifics:** China became primary destination for U.S. grain sorghum — driven by domestic demand for Baijiu production (35-60% alcohol colorless liquor) and feed grains

### China as a Structural Driver

- China's continued economic development and protein consumption growth makes it a long-term dominant importer
- One-fifth of world's population; protein-rich diet adoption continues
- Imports of corn and feed grains expected to remain elevated for the foreseeable future

## Supply Chain Advances Enabling Trade Growth

### Transportation Improvements

- **Unit train (100-car):** Single origin → single destination; dramatically lowered rail grain transport costs
- **Larger ocean vessels:** More tonnage per vessel call; reduced per-unit shipping costs
- **Container grain:** Small but growing niche for IP (Identity Preserved) and Organic grains; also used for processed grain products (meal, starch, flour)

### Information Technology

- Instantaneous global access to pricing, production estimates, market data
- Satellite crop monitoring (replacing field-report extrapolation era)
- Reduced information asymmetry = more liquid global grain markets

## "Food vs. Fuel" Debate

Recurring tension: Should farmland/crops go to biofuels or food?
- Biofuel mandates (RFS, REDD+) divert corn, soybeans from food chain
- Climate policy creates pressure for electric vehicles → reduces biofuel demand long-term
- No consensus resolution; subject to policy volatility
