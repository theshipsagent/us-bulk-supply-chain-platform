**Source:** Congressional Research Service (CRS), Library of Congress
**Author:** John F. Frittelli, Specialist in Transportation — Resources, Science, and Industry Division
**Date:** January 5, 2005
**Pages:** 20 | **File:** `RL32720.pdf`
**Relevance:** HIGH — foundational modal economics reference; infrastructure policy context

## Summary

Comprehensive analysis of U.S. grain supply chain modal dynamics, infrastructure investment priorities,
and competitive balances among truck, rail, and barge. Covers the 109th Congress infrastructure
reauthorization debates (WRDA, TEA-21, rail trust fund proposals).

---

## Key Data Points

### Tonnage Growth
- Grain transported increased **69%** from 1978 to 2000 (242M → 404M short tons)
- All modes grew, but at different rates:
  - Truck: +170% (1978-2000)
  - Barge: +43%
  - Rail: +13%

### Modal Shares (2000, by last-leg method, USDA)
| Mode | Domestic Market | Export Market |
|---|---|---|
| Truck | ~67% | ~7% |
| Rail | ~33% | ~38% |
| Barge | <2% | ~55% |

### Overall Modal Share (all grain, 2000)
- Truck: ~49%
- Rail: ~31%
- Barge: ~18%

### Commodity-Specific Patterns
- **Corn/Soybeans (export):** ~90% by barge to Gulf (UMR-IWW corridor)
- **Wheat (export):** Primarily rail (northern/central plains, far from waterways)
- **Domestic grain:** Trucks dominate (<250 mile hauls to processors)

### Industry Structure
- 4 of top 10 barge operators owned by grain companies: ADM, ConAgra, Cargill, Bunge
- These 4 firms control ~40% of the barge fleet
- 10,000+ grain elevators in U.S. network (as of 2005); 1 in 4 predicted to close within a decade

## Supply Chain Structure

**Export Grain Flow:**
```
Producer → On-farm Storage → Country Elevator → Sub-Terminal Elevator → Export Elevator → Destination
```
**Transport legs:**
- Farm → Country Elevator: Truck (typcally <50 miles)
- Country Elevator → Sub-Terminal: Truck or short-line rail
- Sub-Terminal → Export Elevator: Class I rail OR barge
- Export Elevator → Vessel: Conveyor/ship loading

**Modal Cost Crossover (Figure 1):**
- Truck cheapest: short distances
- Rail cheaper: beyond ~250 miles
- Barge cheapest: longest distances (where waterway available)
- Trucks pay variable infrastructure costs (fuel taxes); rail pays fixed (own track maintenance);
  barge pays variable (fuel tax, but subsidized lock/dam construction)

## Infrastructure Issues Identified

### 1. Mississippi/Illinois River Locks (UMR-IWW)
- Locks built 1930-1950; standard chamber = 600 ft
- Modern tows: 1,100 ft (4 football fields); must break up for 600-ft chambers
- 600-ft lock passage: ~90 min; 1,200-ft lock: ~45 min
- Congress evaluating extension from 600→1,200 ft
- Grain = ~50% of total UMR-IWW freight tonnage

### 2. Short-Line Railroad Track
- 170,000 total U.S. rail track miles; ~50,000 owned by ~550 short lines
- Short lines need to upgrade track for 286,000-lb railcars (replacing 263,000-lb cars)
- Industry estimated cost: ~$7 billion to upgrade short line systems

### 3. Rural Roads and Bridges
- Heavy grain trucks increasing wear on rural county roads
- Roads built before trucking boom; insufficient tax base in rural counties for maintenance

### 4. Main Line Railroad Capacity
- Class I railroads single-tracked on many corridors
- Peak capacity stress during fall harvest season (rail car shortages common)
- Grain farmers complain of railcar shortages and train delays

### 5. Intermodal Connectors
- Access roads to marine/rail terminals inadequate
- At-grade crossings impede truck flow to port terminals

## Policy Issues and Competitive Dynamics

### Volume-chain vs. Value-chain Trade-off
Three distinct grain supply chains identified:
1. **Bulk export** — river navigation + Class I rail trunk lines to seaports
2. **Domestic bulk** — rural interstates + short-line rail to processors/feed lots
3. **Containerized export** — interstate highways + intermodal rail ramps to seaports (specialty/identity-preserved grain)

- 2002: 15% of ag product exports by weight were containerized; 52% by **value**
- Containerized grain ~1% of production (2005); projected to reach 3% within 5 years
- PNW ports leading gateway for containerized grain (Asia demand for specialty varieties)

### Differential Railroad Pricing (Key Concept)
- Railroads charge higher rates where shipper has no viable alternatives (truck/barge not feasible)
- "Inverse rate" example: BNSF charged less to ship from eastern North Dakota than western ND
  to PNW ports — counterintuitive because eastern ND is farther
  - Reason: Eastern ND competed with Canadian Pacific (Vancouver route)
  - BNSF needed to capture all inland revenue on the longer western haul
- Infrastructure subsidies (waterway lock maintenance) affect competitive balance between modes

## Farm/Rail Consolidation Drivers

### Farm Consolidation (1980-1998)
- Number of farms decreased 15%; average farm size increased 11%
- Larger farms find it economical to own trucks; flexibility to time deliveries for best price

### Rail Consolidation
- Class I railroads concentrated operations around sub-terminal elevators with rapid load-out
- Unit trains: 50-54 cars; Shuttle trains: 100-110 cars
- New jumbo covered hoppers: 286,000-315,000 lbs loaded (vs. 263,000 lbs traditional)
- By 1999: ~50% of U.S. rail corn shipments and ~10% of wheat moved by unit trains
- 1970-1999: ton-miles on Class I increased 87% while track miles decreased 43%

## Analytical Framework

**Systems-level perspective needed:** Grain supply chain is deeply intermodal — policy
decisions affecting one mode affect all others. GAO (1981) recommended integrated analysis
before the problem was as acute as it later became.

**Competitive balance principle:** Any major investment in grain transport infrastructure
will shift competitive dynamics between modes, not just improve the targeted mode.
