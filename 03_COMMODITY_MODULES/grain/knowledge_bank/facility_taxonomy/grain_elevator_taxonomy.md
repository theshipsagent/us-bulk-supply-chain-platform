# Grain Elevator Taxonomy and Facility Classification

**Last Updated:** 2026-03-02
**Source:** EPA AP-42 §9.9.1 (2003); CRS RL32720 (2005); USDA FGIS; industry standards

---

## The Grain Elevator Network

U.S. grain moves through a layered elevator network from farm to ocean vessel.
Each tier adds storage capacity, transportation connectivity, and market aggregation.

---

## Tier 1: Country Elevators (First Point of Sale)

**Function:**
- Receive grain by truck directly from farms during and after harvest
- First commercial point in the supply chain
- Weigh, grade (moisture, test weight, dockage), and price grain
- May clean, dry, and blend grain
- Provide short-term storage (typically seasonal)

**Characteristics:**
- Scale: 100,000 to 2,000,000 bushels storage capacity
- Location: Rural, positioned to serve a 20-50 mile farm truck radius
- Equipment: Truck dumps, leg buckets, bins, grain dryers, scales
- Transportation access: Truck (all); short-line rail (many); barge (limited, river access)

**Modern vs. Traditional Design:**
- Traditional: Enclosed headhouse/gallery structure; movable tripper belt; concrete silos
- Modern: Open structural design; enclosed conveyors/distributors; automated; steel bins

**NAICS:** 115114 (Postharvest Crop Activities) | 424510 (Grain Merchant Wholesalers)

**EPA Classification:** Country Elevator (AP-42 §9.9.1)

**Industry estimate:** ~10,000+ country elevators in U.S. (as of 2005);
declining as consolidation accelerates (est. 1 in 4 to close within decade per USDA)

---

## Tier 2: Sub-Terminal Elevators (Aggregation Points)

**Function:**
- Receive grain from multiple country elevators by truck or short-line rail
- Accumulate sufficient volume for Class I rail unit/shuttle trains or large barge loads
- Provide intermediate storage and merchandising
- "Rapid load-out" capability — fill 100+ car shuttle train in <15 hours

**Characteristics:**
- Scale: 2,000,000 to 20,000,000 bushels storage capacity
- Location: Class I railroad junction OR river port
- Equipment: High-speed loading spouts, 100-car track capacity, barge loading where applicable
- Transportation access: Class I rail (required); barge (many)

**Key features:**
- 75+ car track capacity to qualify for shuttle train discounts
- High-speed load-out (500-1,000 bu/min into railcars)
- Often directly owned or contracted by major grain trading companies

**NAICS:** 493130 (Farm Product Warehousing and Storage) | 424510

**Rail service:** Unit trains (50-54 cars) or shuttle trains (100-110 cars) from this tier

---

## Tier 3: Terminal Elevators (Inland, Major Markets)

**Function:**
- Large-scale accumulation and redistribution hubs
- Receive by rail, barge, or truck
- Blend to specifications, provide extended storage
- Redistribute to export elevators OR domestic processors

**Characteristics:**
- Scale: 5,000,000 to 50,000,000 bushels storage capacity
- Location: Major inland river ports (Memphis, Cairo, St. Louis, Chicago)
- Transportation: Rail + barge (both directions)

**NAICS:** 493130 | 424510

---

## Tier 4: Export Elevators (Port Terminals)

**Function:**
- Final grain accumulation point before ocean export
- Load bulk cargo vessels (Panamax, Supramax, Handymax, Handysize)
- Provide official USDA FGIS inspection and weighing
- High-throughput operations (100,000-200,000 MT per vessel)

**Characteristics:**
- Scale: 10,000,000-100,000,000 bushels annual throughput
- Location: U.S. seaports — Gulf of Mexico, Pacific Northwest, Great Lakes, Atlantic
- Equipment: Ship loaders (marine legs and conveyors); draft gauging; sampling systems
- Receiving: Barge unloaders (continuous marine legs) + rail receiving tracks

**NAICS:** 488320 (Marine Cargo Handling) | 493130

**FGIS Requirement:** USDA FGIS official weighing and inspection required for most bulk grain exports

---

## Export Elevator Barge Unloading

**Bucket elevator method:** Continuous barge unloader or marine leg extended into barge hold
**Ship loading:** Typically by in-house leg or self-unloading system designed into vessel
(most grain bulk carriers are NOT self-unloading — facility provides the loading mechanism)

## Geographic Distribution of Key Elevator Types

### Gulf Export Elevators (Primary Corn/Soy Export Complex)
**Location:** River Mile 0-230, Lower Mississippi River (Baton Rouge to Gulf)
**Key operators:** ADM, Bunge, Cargill, Louis Dreyfus, CHS, Zen-Noh, Gavilon
**Capacity:** World's largest grain export complex; handles ~60% of U.S. corn/soy exports

### PNW Export Elevators (Wheat/Corn/Soy PNW)
**Ports:** Portland OR, Vancouver WA, Kalama WA, Longview WA
**Key operators:** CHS, TEMCO (Cargill/CHS JV), Columbia Grain, Louis Dreyfus, United Harvest
**System:** Columbia River provides barge access 320 miles inland; Snake River adds 140+ miles

### Great Lakes Export Elevators
**Ports:** Duluth-Superior MN/WI, Toledo OH, Burns Harbor IN
**Seaway:** St. Lawrence Seaway provides access to Atlantic (seasonal, closed Dec-March)
