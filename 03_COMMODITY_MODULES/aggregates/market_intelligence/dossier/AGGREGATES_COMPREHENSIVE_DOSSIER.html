US CONSTRUCTION AGGREGATES COMPREHENSIVE DOSSIER

Prepared: February 18, 2026 | Version: 1.0 | Focus: US Supply/Demand, Market Structure & Competitive Landscape

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY: The US construction aggregates industry is a ~$39 billion/year sector producing approximately 2.38 billion metric tons annually (2024) across crushed stone and sand & gravel. Despite a 6% production dip in 2024 driven by severe weather and elevated interest rates, structural fundamentals remain strong: IIJA infrastructure spending ($550B new through FY2026), persistent pricing power (5.5% CAGR over the past decade), and irreplaceable urban quarry reserves create durable competitive moats. Limestone/dolomite dominates at 70% of crushed stone output (~1.05 Bt). Transportation costs represent ~50% of delivered price, making quarry proximity to demand centers the single most valuable strategic asset. Industry consolidation is accelerating — the $11.5B Quikrete-Summit deal (Nov 2024) signals continued rollup among top producers.

3.2 IIJA / Bipartisan Infrastructure Law

INFRASTRUCTURE TAILWIND: The IIJA authorized $1.2 trillion total ($550 billion in new spending) through FY2026 for highways, bridges, water/wastewater, transit, and ports. IIJA Section 11526 specifically established a working group to ensure sustainable access to quality aggregate resources. The industry anticipates needing "billions of tons of construction materials" to fulfill funded projects.

3.3 Demand Headwinds (2024-2025)

2024 DEMAND SOFTENING: Severe weather events (hurricanes, flooding) disrupted production. Interest rates at 23-year highs suppressed residential housing starts. Portland & blended cement consumption fell 6% in 2024. Federal funding deployment has been slower than expected, with some IIJA awards facing delays and freezes.

TRADE CONTEXT: The US is essentially self-sufficient in construction aggregates. Total imports of ~14 Mt represent less than 0.6% of domestic production (~2,380 Mt). Unlike fly ash (where import arbitrage is $91-107/Mt), aggregates are too low-value relative to weight to justify long-distance ocean shipping. Cross-border trade with Canada and marine-delivered stone from the Caribbean/Central America serve niche coastal markets.

7.3 Lime Industrial Uses

  • Steelmaking: Largest single end use — flux for blast furnaces and BOF
  • Chemical Manufacturing: Fertilizer, glass, paper/pulp, precipitated calcium carbonate
  • Flue Gas Treatment: SO2 scrubbing at power plants (declining with coal closures)
  • Water Treatment: Municipal and industrial water purification
  • Sugar Refining: Carbonatation process in beet sugar production
  • Construction: Soil stabilization, asphalt anti-strip, mortar/plaster
LIMESTONE MARKET OUTLOOK: North America limestone market valued at $28.94 billion (2024), projected to reach $45.29 billion by 2033 at 5.1% CAGR. Portland-limestone cement (PLC) adoption is the fastest-growing trend — now approved in all 50 states, with blended cements reaching 58% of total cement shipments. This trend increases limestone demand per ton of cement produced.

The 50-Mile Rule: Transportation Cost vs. Material Value
Conceptual illustration. Beyond ~50 miles by truck, transportation cost exceeds the value of the aggregate itself. This makes quarry proximity to demand centers the single most important competitive advantage in the industry.
LOGISTICS = THE MOAT: Transportation costs represent approximately 50% of delivered aggregate price. This is why: (1) quarry reserves near urban centers are irreplaceable strategic assets; (2) rail- and barge-served quarries can serve markets 100-500+ miles away that truck-only operators cannot; (3) aggregates imports are negligible (<1% of consumption) because ocean freight makes distant sourcing uneconomical for a $16/ton commodity.

11.2 Permitting Reality

7-10 YEAR PERMITTING TIMELINE: Opening a new aggregate quarry typically requires 7-10 years and $1+ million in permitting costs across federal (MSHA, EPA, USACE), state (environmental quality, surface mine, water discharge), and local (zoning, conditional-use) agencies. This creates a structural barrier to entry and supports long-term pricing power for existing quarry operators. Once a quarry permit is secured, it is an irreplaceable asset.

11.3 Urban Supply Squeeze

  • NIMBYism: Community opposition blocks new quarries and expansions near population centers
  • Zoning: Quarry-zoned land is increasingly re-zoned for residential/commercial development
  • Irreversible loss: Once reserves are paved over, they are permanently unavailable
  • Price impact: Urban supply constraints drive longer haul distances and higher delivered prices

NET ASSESSMENT: US construction aggregates are positioned for sustained volume recovery (+3-5% annually) and continued pricing power (+5-7% annually) through 2030. Supply constraints (urban permitting, reserve depletion) are structural and irreversible. IIJA infrastructure spending, interest rate normalization, and emerging demand from data center construction provide multi-year demand tailwinds. The industry's low-value/high-weight characteristics, local market dominance, and permitting barriers create one of the most durable competitive moats in industrial materials. Major producers (Vulcan, Martin Marietta, CRH) are positioned to compound earnings through volume growth, pricing, and continued acquisitions.
